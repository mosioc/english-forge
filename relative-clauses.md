## 18. Relative Clauses (Adjective Clauses)

Relative clauses give extra information about a noun. They begin with a **relative pronoun** (*who, whom, whose, which, that*) or a **relative adverb** (*where, when, why*) and can be **defining** or **non‑defining**. They also have reduced forms.

Think of relative clauses as "add-on" pieces of information. They are dependent clauses that modify or describe a noun (or pronoun) in the main part of the sentence. They answer the questions: *"Which one?"* or *"What kind?"*

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 16. Pronouns and Determiners](./pronouns-determiners.md)* for relative pronouns and *[Section 17. Clause Concepts](./clause-concepts.md)* for relative adverbs.

---

### A. Defining vs. Non‑Defining Relative Clauses

#### 1. Defining (Restrictive) Relative Clauses

These are **essential**. If you remove them, the meaning of the sentence changes drastically, or it becomes unclear which noun you're referring to. They define or restrict the noun.

> **Punctuation:** No commas
> **Relative pronouns/adverbs:** *who, whom, which, that, whose, where, when, why* (often uses *that*)

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| *The man **who lives next door** is a doctor.* | Which man? The one who lives next door. |
| *The book **that you recommended** was excellent.* | Which book? The one you recommended. |
| *Students **who study regularly** get better results.* | Which students? Those who study regularly. |
| *The place **where I was born** is very quiet.* | Which place? The one where I was born. |
| *The year **when we graduated** was unforgettable.* | Which year? The one when we graduated. |
| *The reason **why she left** is unknown.* | Which reason? The one why she left. |

#### 2. Non‑Defining (Non‑Restrictive) Relative Clauses

These add **extra, interesting, but non‑essential** information. The sentence would still make sense and clearly identify the noun even if you removed the clause.

> **Punctuation:** Commas (or dashes/parentheses)
> **Relative pronouns:** Typically *who* (people) or *which* (things); **never *that*** (except in informal speech)

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| *My brother, **who lives in Canada**, is visiting next month.* | I have only one brother; the extra info is non‑essential. |
| *The car, **which I bought last year**, is already having problems.* | The car is already identified; the purchase date is extra. |
| *Sara, **whose mother is a teacher**, speaks five languages.* | Sara is already identified; her mother's job is extra. |
| *Paris, **which is the capital of France**, attracts millions of tourists.* | Paris is already known; the capital fact is extra. |
| *The Eiffel Tower, **which was built in 1889**, is a famous landmark.* | The Eiffel Tower is already identified; the date is extra. |

> **Important:** *That* is generally **not** used in non‑defining clauses. Use *who* for people and *which* for things.

### B. Relative Pronouns and Relative Adverbs

#### 1. Relative Pronouns (for People and Things)

| Pronoun | Use | Example |
|---------|-----|---------|
| ***who*** | People (subject) | *The singer **who** won the award is famous.* |
| ***whom*** | People (object - formal) | *The artist **whom** I admire is coming.* (I admire **him**) |
| ***whose*** | Possession (people, animals, things) | *The student **whose** project was chosen won a prize.* / *The company **whose** stock rose made a profit.* |
| ***which*** | Things or animals | *The cafe **which** I love has closed.* / *The dog **which** barked was noisy.* |
| ***that*** | People or things (defining clauses only) | *The car **that** hit the fence was red.* / *The person **that** helped me was kind.* |

> **Tip for *whom*:** Replace with *him* or *her*. If it fits, *whom* is correct.
> - *The artist **whom** I admire* -> *I admire **him*** ✓

#### 2. Relative Adverbs (for Place, Time, and Reason)

| Adverb | Use | Replaces | Example |
|--------|-----|----------|---------|
| ***where*** | Place | *in/at/on which* | *The house **where** I grew up is beautiful.* (= in which) |
| ***when*** | Time | *in/on/at which* | *I remember the day **when** we met.* (= on which) |
| ***why*** | Reason | *for which* | *That's the reason **why** I left.* (= for which) |

> **Note:** Relative adverbs are **never pronouns**. They are always adverbs introducing a full clause (subject + verb).

**Distinction Between Relative Pronouns and Relative Adverbs:**

| | Relative Pronoun | Relative Adverb |
|--|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Words** | *who, whom, whose, which, that* | *where, when, why* |
| **Function in clause** | Acts as subject or object | Acts as adverb (modifies the verb) |
| **Example** | *the house **which** I bought* (I bought **it**) | *the house **where** I live* (I live **there**) |
| **Can be omitted?** | Yes (when object) | No (but can be replaced by *that* informally) |

### C. Reduced Relative Clauses

Reduced relative clauses are **shortened forms** using *-ing*, *-ed*, or infinitive phrases. They are used when the relative pronoun is the **subject** of the clause.

#### 1. Active Meaning -> *-ing* (Present Participle)

When the noun is **doing** the action described in the relative clause.

| Full Form | Reduced Form |
|-----------|--------------|
| *The man **who is sitting** over there is my uncle.* | *The man **sitting** over there is my uncle.* |
| *Students **who work hard** succeed.* | *Students **working hard** succeed.* |
| *The woman **who is talking** to John is my boss.* | *The woman **talking** to John is my boss.* |

#### 2. Passive Meaning -> *-ed* (Past Participle)

When the noun is **receiving** the action described in the relative clause.

| Full Form | Reduced Form |
|-----------|--------------|
| *The books **that were written** by her are popular.* | *The books **written** by her are popular.* |
| *The house **which was damaged** by the storm needs repair.* | *The house **damaged** by the storm needs repair.* |
| *The proposal **that was rejected** by the committee was unfair.* | *The proposal **rejected** by the committee was unfair.* |

#### 3. With Infinitive (*to + V1*)

Often appears with **superlatives** (*first, last, best, only*) or **ordinal numbers** (*second, third*). It implies a future action or a specific instance.

| Full Form | Reduced Form |
|-----------|--------------|
| *The first person **who arrives** will win a prize.* | *The first person **to arrive** will win a prize.* |
| *She was the only one **who understood** the problem.* | *She was the only one **to understand** the problem.* |
| *This is the best movie **that you can watch** this weekend.* | *This is the best movie **to watch** this weekend.* |
| *He is the next person **who will speak**.* | *He is the next person **to speak**.* |

#### 4. Negative Reduced Clauses

To make a reduced relative clause negative, use *not* before the participle or infinitive.

| Full Form | Reduced Form |
|-----------|--------------|
| *Anyone **who does not have** a ticket cannot enter.* | *Anyone **not having** a ticket cannot enter.* |
| *The only student **who did not pass** the exam was John.* | *The only student **not to pass** the exam was John.* |

#### 5. Reduced Relative Clauses with Prepositions

When a relative clause contains a preposition, the reduced form can keep the preposition.

| Full Form | Reduced Form |
|-----------|--------------|
| *The people **who I work with** are friendly.* | *The people **working with me** are friendly.* |
| *The bed **that I slept in** was uncomfortable.* | *The bed **slept in** was uncomfortable.* (awkward - better to rephrase) |

### D. Special Cases and Notes

#### 1. Prepositions in Relative Clauses

| Register | Position of Preposition | Example |
|----------|------------------------|---------|
| **Formal** | Before *whom* or *which* | *The person **to whom** I spoke was helpful.* / *The house **in which** I live is old.* |
| **Informal** | At the end of the clause | *The person **who** I spoke **to** was helpful.* / *The house **which** I live **in** is old.* |
| **Informal (no pronoun)** | At the end of the clause | *The person I spoke **to** was helpful.* / *The house I live **in** is old.* |

#### 2. Omitting the Relative Pronoun (Defining Clauses Only)

In defining relative clauses, the relative pronoun can be omitted when it is the **object** of the clause (not the subject).

| Type | Example | Can Omit? |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Subject (cannot omit)** | *The man **who called** you is here.* | ✗ (Cannot omit *who*) |
| **Object (can omit)** | *The book **(that/which)** you gave me is great.* | ✓ (Can omit) |
| **With preposition** | *The person **(who/whom/that)** I spoke **to** is nice.* | ✓ (Can omit) |
| **Possessive (cannot omit)** | *The student **whose** phone rang was embarrassed.* | ✗ (Cannot omit *whose*) |
| **Relative adverb (cannot omit)** | *The house **where** I live is old.* | ✗ (Cannot omit *where* in formal English) |

> **Note:** Relative adverbs (*where, when, why*) cannot be omitted in formal English. In informal English, they can be replaced by *that* or omitted entirely with different word order.
> - Formal: *The day **when** we met was rainy.*
> - Informal: *The day **that** we met was rainy.* / *The day we met was rainy.*

#### 3. *Whose* for Things

Although *whose* is traditionally for people, it is also commonly used for things to avoid awkward phrasing like *of which*.

| Awkward | Better |
|---------|--------|
| *The house **the roof of which** was damaged...* | *The house **whose roof** was damaged...* |
| *A book **the plot of which** is confusing...* | *A book **whose plot** is confusing...* |
| *The company **the stock of which** rose...* | *The company **whose stock** rose...* |

#### 4. *Which* Referring to a Whole Clause

In non-defining relative clauses, *which* can refer to the **entire previous clause**, not just a single noun.

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| *He was late, **which** was unusual for him.* | *Which* refers to the fact that he was late |
| *She passed the exam, **which** made her parents happy.* | *Which* refers to the fact that she passed |
| *They cancelled the meeting, **which** was a mistake.* | *Which* refers to the cancellation |

> **Note:** *That* cannot be used this way. Only *which* can refer to a whole clause.

### E. Quick Reference Summary Table

| Feature | Defining (Restrictive) | Non‑Defining (Non‑Restrictive) |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Essential?** | Yes - defines the noun | No - adds extra information |
| **Commas?** | No commas | Commas around the clause |
| **Can use *that*?** | Yes | No (use *who/which*) |
| **Can use *which* for a whole clause?** | No | Yes |
| **Can omit pronoun?** | Yes (when object) | No |
| **Example** | *The book **that** you lent me...* | *This book, **which** you lent me...* |

| Reduced Form | Meaning | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| *-ing* (present participle) | Active | *The man **sitting** there is my uncle.* |
| *-ed* (past participle) | Passive | *The book **written** by her is famous.* |
| *to + V1* (infinitive) | Future / superlative / ordinal | *The first **to arrive** gets a prize.* |

### F. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| *The book which I read it was great.* | *The book which I read was great.* | No repeated pronoun in relative clause |
| *This is the man which helped me.* | *This is the man **who** helped me.* | Use *who* for people, *which* for things |
| *She is the teacher that I told you about her.* | *She is the teacher (that) I told you about.* | No extra pronoun |
| *My car, that I bought last year, is blue.* | *My car, **which** I bought last year, is blue.* | *That* cannot be used in non-defining clauses |
| *The reason why he left was because he was tired.* | *The reason why he left was **that** he was tired.* | Use *that*, not *because*, after *the reason* |
| *The house where I live in is old.* | *The house **where** I live is old.* or *The house I live **in** is old.* | Don't use both *where* and a preposition |
| *I don't like people who talk loudly, and they annoy me.* | *I don't like people who talk loudly **because** they annoy me.* | Incorrect relative clause structure |

### G. Complete Comparison: Relative Pronouns vs. Relative Adverbs

| Word | Type | Refers to | Function | Example |
|------|------|-----------|----------|---------|
| *who* | Relative Pronoun | People | Subject | *The man **who** called...* |
| *whom* | Relative Pronoun | People | Object | *The man **whom** I saw...* |
| *whose* | Relative Pronoun | People/things | Possessive | *The man **whose** car...* |
| *which* | Relative Pronoun | Things/animals | Subject/Object | *The car **which** I bought...* |
| *that* | Relative Pronoun | People/things | Subject/Object | *The car **that** I bought...* |
| *where* | Relative Adverb | Place | Adverb | *The house **where** I live...* |
| *when* | Relative Adverb | Time | Adverb | *The day **when** we met...* |
| *why* | Relative Adverb | Reason | Adverb | *The reason **why** I left...* |

**Memory Trick:**

| If the relative word... | Then it is a... |
|-------------------------|-----------------|
| Can be replaced by *he/she/it/they* | **Relative Pronoun** |
| Can be replaced by *there/then/because* | **Relative Adverb** |
| Can be omitted | **Relative Pronoun (when object)** |
| Cannot be omitted | **Relative Pronoun (when subject)** or **Relative Adverb** |
