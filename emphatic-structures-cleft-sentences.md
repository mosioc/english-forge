## 12.1 Emphatic Structures (Cleft Sentences)

Cleft sentences are used to **emphasize** a particular part of a sentence. The word "cleft" means "split" or "divided" - a cleft sentence splits a standard sentence into two parts to highlight a specific element (subject, object, adverb, etc.).

> **Related Sections:** See *Section 11. Inversion* for other emphasis structures and *Section 17. Clause Concepts* for clause types.

---

### A. What Is a Cleft Sentence?

A **simple sentence** can be rewritten as a **cleft sentence** to give emphasis to one element.

| Simple Sentence | Cleft Sentence (Emphasis on "John") |
|-----------------|-------------------------------------|
| *John broke the window yesterday.* | *It was **John** who broke the window yesterday.* |

The cleft sentence splits the information into two clauses:
- **Clause 1 (the emphasis):** *It was John*
- **Clause 2 (the rest):** *who broke the window yesterday*

---

### B. Types of Cleft Sentences

There are two main types of cleft sentences: **It-clefts** and **Wh-clefts** (also called pseudo-clefts).

---

#### 1. It-Cleft Sentences

> **Structure:** *It + be + emphasized element + that / who / which / when / where + rest of the sentence*

The emphasized element comes immediately after *It + be*. The second clause is introduced by a relative pronoun (*that, who, which, when, where*).

##### Basic Sentence for Reference

| Element | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Complete sentence | *John broke the window yesterday.* |

##### Emphasizing Different Elements

| Emphasized Element | Cleft Sentence |
|--------------------|----------------|
| **Subject** (John) | *It was **John** who broke the window yesterday.* |
| **Object** (the window) | *It was **the window** that John broke yesterday.* |
| **Adverb of time** (yesterday) | *It was **yesterday** that John broke the window.* |
| **Adverb of place** (if added) | *It was **in the garden** that John broke the window.* |

##### More Examples

| Emphasis | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Person** (subject) | *It was **Sarah** who called you last night.* |
| **Person** (object) | *It was **John** whom I saw at the party.* (or *that* I saw) |
| **Thing** | *It is **patience** that we need most right now.* |
| **Time** | *It was **in 2015** that they first met.* |
| **Place** | *It was **in Paris** where they fell in love.* |
| **Reason** | *It was **because of the rain** that we cancelled the picnic.* |

##### Using *Not Until* in It-Clefts

To emphasize a time expression with *not until*, use the following structure:

> **Structure:** *It was not until + time expression + that + clause*

| Simple Sentence | Cleft Sentence |
|-----------------|----------------|
| *I didn't finish the report until midnight.* | *It was **not until midnight** that I finished the report.* |
| *She didn't leave the hospital until the doctor arrived.* | *It was **not until the doctor arrived** that she left the hospital.* |

---

#### 2. Wh-Cleft Sentences (Pseudo-Clefts)

> **Structure:** *What / Where / When / Why / How + clause + be + emphasized element*

Wh-clefts begin with a *wh-* word clause (usually *what*), followed by *be*, followed by the emphasized element.

##### Basic Sentence for Reference

| Element | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Complete sentence | *I need a good night's sleep.* |

##### Emphasizing Different Elements

| Emphasis | Cleft Sentence |
|----------|----------------|
| **What I need** | *What I need is **a good night's sleep**.* |
| **What I need to do** | *What I need to do is **get a good night's sleep**.* |

##### More Examples with *What*

| Simple Sentence | Wh-Cleft Sentence |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| *I want a new car.* | *What I want is **a new car**.* |
| *He gave me a book.* | *What he gave me was **a book**.* |
| *She loves chocolate the most.* | *What she loves most is **chocolate**.* |
| *You should take a break.* | *What you should do is **take a break**.* |
| *They need more time.* | *What they need is **more time**.* |

##### Examples with Other Wh-Words

| Wh-Word | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Where** | *Where we met was **in college**.* |
| **When** | *When I need you most is **when I'm alone**.* |
| **Why** | *Why he left is **a mystery**.* |
| **How** | *How she succeeded was **through hard work**.* |

---

### C. Reversed Wh-Cleft Sentences

In reversed wh-clefts, the emphasized element comes first, followed by the *wh-* clause.

> **Structure:** *Emphasized element + be + wh- clause*

| Standard Wh-Cleft | Reversed Wh-Cleft |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| *What I need is **a holiday**.* | ***A holiday** is what I need.* |
| *What she gave me was **a watch**.* | ***A watch** was what she gave me.* |
| *What he did was **apologize**.* | ***Apologize** was what he did.* |

---

### D. All-Cleft Sentences

Another emphatic structure uses *all* instead of *what* to mean "the only thing."

> **Structure:** *All + clause + be + emphasized element*

| Simple Sentence | All-Cleft Sentence |
|-----------------|--------------------|
| *I need a little rest.* | *All I need is **a little rest**.* |
| *She wants you to call her.* | *All she wants is **for you to call her**.* |
| *They did was complain.* | *All they did was **complain**.* |

---

### E. Emphatic *Do* (Alternative Emphasis Structure)

Although not a cleft sentence, the emphatic *do* is another common way to add emphasis, especially in spoken English.

> **Structure:** *Subject + do/does/did + base verb*

| Simple Statement | Emphatic Statement |
|------------------|--------------------|
| *I like your dress.* | *I **do like** your dress!* |
| *She cares about you.* | *She **does care** about you!* |
| *He called me yesterday.* | *He **did call** me yesterday!* |

| Context | Emphatic Do Example |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Contradicting** | *"You don't understand." - "I **do** understand!"* |
| **Expressing strong feeling** | *I **do** apologize for the delay.* |
| **Adding conviction** | *We **will** succeed!* (using *will* for emphasis) |

> **Note:** The emphatic *do* is used only in **affirmative** statements. It does not change the tense - *do/does* for present, *did* for past.

---

### F. Emphatic *It* Structures (Without Cleft)

Sometimes *it* is used as a dummy subject to emphasize an infinitive or *that*-clause.

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *It + be + adjective + infinitive* | *It is **difficult to understand** this theory.* |
| *It + be + adjective + that-clause* | *It is **surprising that** he didn't come.* |
| *It + be + noun + infinitive* | *It is **a pleasure to meet** you.* |
| *It + be + noun + that-clause* | *It is **a shame that** you missed the concert.* |

---

### G. Comparison Table: Emphasis Structures

| Structure | Example | Emphasis On |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **It-cleft** | *It was **John** who broke the window.* | Subject (John) |
| **It-cleft** | *It was **the window** that John broke.* | Object (the window) |
| **It-cleft (time)** | *It was **yesterday** that John broke the window.* | Time (yesterday) |
| **Wh-cleft** | *What John did was **break the window**.* | Action (break the window) |
| **Wh-cleft** | *What John broke was **the window**.* | Object (the window) |
| **All-cleft** | *All John did was **break the window**.* | The only action |
| **Reversed wh-cleft** | ***Breaking the window** was what John did.* | Action (breaking) |
| **Emphatic do** | *John **did break** the window.* | Verb (did break) |
| **Inversion** (see Section 10) | *Never **had John** broken a window before.* | Negative time phrase |

---

### H. Common Uses of Cleft Sentences

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Correcting misinformation** | *"Sarah broke the vase." - "No, it was **John** who broke the vase."* |
| **Adding dramatic effect** | *It was **then** that I realized the truth.* |
| **Making writing more dynamic** | *What the research shows is **a clear link between diet and health**.* |
| **Introducing a new topic** | *What I'd like to talk about today is **the future of renewable energy**.* |
| **Expressing contrast** | *It wasn't **that he was lazy**; it was **that he was afraid**.* |

---

### I. Quick Reference Summary

| Cleft Type | Structure | Example |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| **It-cleft (subject)** | *It + be + subject + who/that + rest* | *It was **Anna** who called.* |
| **It-cleft (object)** | *It + be + object + that + subject + verb* | *It was **a book** that he gave me.* |
| **It-cleft (time)** | *It + be + time expression + that + clause* | *It was **last week** that I saw her.* |
| **It-cleft (place)** | *It + be + place expression + where + clause* | *It was **in London** where they met.* |
| **Wh-cleft** | *What/Where/When/Why/How + clause + be + emphasis* | *What I need is **rest**.* |
| **Reversed wh-cleft** | *Emphasis + be + wh-clause* | ***Rest** is what I need.* |
| **All-cleft** | *All + clause + be + emphasis* | *All I need is **rest**.* |
| **Emphatic do** | *Subject + do/does/did + base verb* | *I **do need** rest.* |

---

### J. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| *It was John **which** broke the window.* (person with *which*) | *It was John **who** broke the window.* ✓ |
| *What I need **are** rest.* (subject-verb agreement) | *What I need **is** rest.* ✓ |
| *It was **in yesterday** that I saw her.* (redundant *in*) | *It was **yesterday** that I saw her.* ✓ |
| *What did he was leave.* (incorrect formation) | *What he did was **leave**.* ✓ |
| *I **do to like** it.* (incorrect verb form) | *I **do like** it.* ✓ |
