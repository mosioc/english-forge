## 5.2 Advanced Tenses: Present, Past, Future, and Perfect Forms

English uses a variety of tense forms to express time relationships, from habitual actions to completed events and future possibilities. This section covers present, past, present perfect, and future structures, including their contrasts and common usage patterns.

---

### A. Present Tenses

#### 1. Present Simple (I do)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | Base verb / Base verb + -s/-es (does) |
| **Use** | Habits, routines, facts, and general truths |

**Examples:**
- *I work in an office.*
- *The sun rises in the east.*
- *Anna often plays tennis.*
- *I work in a bank, but I don't enjoy it much.*
- *Do you like parties?*
- *It doesn't rain so much in summer.*

---

#### 2. Present Continuous (I am doing)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | am/is/are + verb-ing |
| **Use** | Actions happening now, temporary situations, and future arrangements |

**Examples:**
- *I am eating right now.*
- *She is staying with her friend this week.*
- *"Where's Anna?" "She's playing tennis."*
- *Please don't disturb me now. I'm working.*
- *Hello! Are you enjoying the party?*
- *It isn't raining at the moment.*

---

#### 3. Present Simple vs. Present Continuous

| Contrast | Present Simple | Present Continuous |
|----------|----------------|--------------------|
| **Repeated vs. now** | *I read every day.* (habit) | *I am reading a great book these days.* (temporary) |
| **State vs. change** | *I think this is a good idea.* (state) | *He is getting better.* (changing situation) |

---

#### 4. State Verbs (Stative Verbs)

State verbs describe a **state or condition** rather than an action. They are **not normally used in continuous tenses** (even for present time).

> **Rule:** Use present simple, not present continuous, with state verbs.

| Verb Type | Examples | Incorrect (Continuous) | Correct (Simple) |
|-----------|----------|------------------------|------------------|
| **Thoughts and opinions** | *believe, think, agree, understand, know, remember, forget, mean* | *I am thinking you are right.* | *I **think** you are right.* |
| **Emotions and feelings** | *love, hate, like, dislike, prefer, want, wish, need* | *I am loving this movie.* | *I **love** this movie.* |
| **Senses** (perception) | *see, hear, smell, taste, feel* | *I am seeing a bird.* | *I **see** a bird.* |
| **Possession** | *have (own), belong, own, possess* | *I am having a car.* | *I **have** a car.* |
| **Measurements** | *weigh, measure, cost, contain, fit* | *The bag is weighing 5 kg.* | *The bag **weighs** 5 kg.* |
| **Relationships** | *be, seem, appear, look (seem), resemble* | *She is seeming tired.* | *She **seems** tired.* |
| **Existence** | *exist, live (permanently), consist of, include* | *Water is consisting of H₂O.* | *Water **consists** of H₂O.* |

##### Common Mistakes with State Verbs

| ❌ Incorrect | ✓ Correct | Why |
|-------------|-----------|-----|
| *I am knowing the answer.* | *I **know** the answer.* | *Know* is a state verb |
| *She is wanting to leave.* | *She **wants** to leave.* | *Want* is a state verb |
| *This is belonging to me.* | *This **belongs** to me.* | *Belong* is a state verb |
| *I am understanding you.* | *I **understand** you.* | *Understand* is a state verb |
| *We are having a car.* | *We **have** a car.* | *Have* for possession is state |
| *The soup is tasting good.* | *The soup **tastes** good.* | *Taste* (sense) is state |

##### State Verbs That Can Be Action Verbs (Meaning Change)

Some verbs can be **both** state and action, with different meanings.

| Verb | State (Simple) - No Continuous | Action (Continuous) - Possible |
|------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| *think* | *I **think** he is right.* (opinion) | *I **am thinking** about the problem.* (mental process) |
| *have* | *I **have** a car.* (possession) | *I **am having** dinner.* / *We **are having** a party.* |
| *see* | *I **see** the bird.* (perception) | *I **am seeing** my doctor tomorrow.* (meeting) |
| *hear* | *I **hear** the music.* (perception) | *I **am hearing** good things about you.* (informal) |
| *taste* | *The soup **tastes** salty.* (quality) | *The chef **is tasting** the soup.* (action) |
| *smell* | *The flower **smells** sweet.* (quality) | *She **is smelling** the flower.* (action) |
| *feel* | *I **feel** tired.* (state) | *I **am feeling** the fabric.* (action) |
| *look* | *She **looks** beautiful.* (appearance) | *He **is looking** at the painting.* (action) |
| *weigh* | *The bag **weighs** 10 kg.* (measurement) | *The clerk **is weighing** the fruit.* (action) |

##### Why It Matters

Using a state verb in continuous form is one of the most common errors for English learners.

| ❌ Incorrect | ✓ Correct |
|-------------|-----------|
| *I am needing your help.* | *I **need** your help.* |
| *She is understanding the lesson.* | *She **understands** the lesson.* |
| *They are preferring coffee.* | *They **prefer** coffee.* |
| *He is seeming nice.* | *He **seems** nice.* |

---

#### 5. Performatives

Performative verbs are used when **saying something *is* doing something**. The utterance itself performs the action.

> **Key idea:** When you say *I promise*, you are *making* a promise. The words *are* the action.

##### Common Performative Verbs

| Verb | Example | Action Performed |
|------|---------|------------------|
| *promise* | *I **promise** to be on time.* | Making a promise |
| *apologize* | *I **apologize** for my mistake.* | Making an apology |
| *warn* | *I **warn** you: it's dangerous.* | Giving a warning |
| *suggest* | *I **suggest** we leave early.* | Making a suggestion |
| *recommend* | *I **recommend** the fish.* | Giving a recommendation |
| *insist* | *I **insist** that you stay.* | Insisting |
| *request* | *I **request** your presence.* | Making a request |
| *demand* | *I **demand** an answer.* | Making a demand |
| *congratulate* | *I **congratulate** you on your success.* | Offering congratulations |
| *thank* | *I **thank** you for your help.* | Giving thanks |
| *advise* | *I **advise** you to wait.* | Giving advice |
| *propose* | *I **propose** a toast.* | Making a proposal |

##### Grammar of Performatives

Performatives are typically used in **present simple** and often include the word *hereby* (formal).

| Example | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| *I hereby **declare** the meeting open.* | Formal, official |
| *I **name** this ship the Titanic.* | Ceremonial |
| *I **pronounce** you husband and wife.* | Ceremonial |
| *I **bet** you $10 he won't come.* | Informal |
| *I **challenge** you to a duel.* | Formal/playful |

##### Performatives vs. Descriptions

| Performative (Action performed by speaking) | Description (Reporting an action) |
|---------------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| *I **apologize** for being late.* (apologizing now) | *I **am apologizing** because I have to.* (describing) |
| *I **promise** to help you.* (making a promise now) | *She **promised** to help me yesterday.* (reporting) |
| *I **suggest** we go home.* (making a suggestion now) | *He **suggested** we go home.* (reporting) |

##### Performatives with Continuous Forms

Performatives can also appear in continuous form, but the meaning changes slightly.

| Simple (Performative) | Continuous (Descriptive) |
|----------------------|--------------------------|
| *I **promise** to help.* (Making a promise now) | *I **am promising** to help, but I'm not sure.* (Describing the act) |

### B. Past Tenses

#### 6. Past Simple (I did)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | Past tense form of verb (V2) |
| **Use** | Completed actions in the past |

**Examples:**
- *I visited London last year.*
- *Anna played tennis yesterday afternoon.*
- *I lost my key a few days ago.*
- *There was a film on TV last night, but we didn't watch it.*
- *What did you do when you finished work yesterday?*

---

#### 7. Past Continuous (I was doing)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | was/were + verb-ing |
| **Use** | Actions in progress at a specific past time |

**Examples:**
- *I was cooking at 7 p.m.*
- *I saw Anna at the sports centre yesterday. She was playing tennis.*
- *I dropped my key when I was trying to open the door.*
- *The TV was on, but we weren't watching it.*
- *What were you doing at this time yesterday?*

---

#### 8. Past Simple vs. Past Continuous

| Contrast | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Interrupted action** | *I was watching TV **when** he called.* |
| **Simultaneous actions** | *He called **while** I was watching TV.* |

---

#### 9. Used to (do)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Past habits or states that no longer continue |

**Examples:**
- *I **used to** smoke, but I quit.*
- *She **used to** live in Paris.*

> **Note:** *Used to* has no present form. For present habits, use present simple.

---

#### 10. Past Perfect (I had done)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | had + past participle (V3) |
| **Use** | Action completed before another past action |

**Examples:**
- *When I arrived, they **had already left**.*
- *It wasn't her first game of tennis. She **had played** many times before.*
- *They couldn't get into the house because they **had lost** the key.*
- *The house was dirty because I **hadn't cleaned** it for weeks.*

---

#### 11. Past Perfect Continuous (I had been doing)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | had + been + verb-ing |
| **Use** | Emphasizes duration before a past action |

**Examples:**
- *She was tired because she **had been working** all day.*
- *Anna was tired yesterday evening because she **had been playing** tennis in the afternoon.*
- *James decided to go to the doctor because he **hadn't been feeling** well.*

---

### C. Present Perfect Tenses

#### 12. Present Perfect Simple (I have done)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | have/has + past participle (V3) |
| **Use** | Life experiences at an unspecified time; present results from past actions |

**Examples:**
- *I **have visited** Japan.*
- *Anna **has played** tennis many times.*
- *I've lost my key. **Have you seen** it anywhere?* (I don't have it now)
- *How long **have you and Sam known** each other?*
- *A: Is it still raining? B: No, it **has stopped**.*
- *The house is dirty. I **haven't cleaned** it for weeks.*

---

#### 13. Present Perfect Continuous (I have been doing)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | have/has + been + verb-ing |
| **Use** | Action started in the past and continues to now; emphasis on duration |

**Examples:**
- *I **have been studying** for two hours.*
- *Anna is tired. She **has been playing** tennis.*
- *You're out of breath. **Have you been running**?*
- *How long **have you been learning** English?*
- *It's still raining. It **has been raining** all day.*
- *I **haven't been feeling** well recently.*

---

#### 14. Present Perfect Continuous vs. Simple

| Contrast | Present Perfect Simple | Present Perfect Continuous |
|----------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **Result vs. duration** | *I have cleaned the house.* (result - the house is clean) | *I have been cleaning the house.* (activity - may not be finished) |

---

#### 15. How Long Have You (Been)...? / For and Since / When vs. How Long

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **How long + present perfect** | *How long have you lived here?* |
| **How long + present perfect continuous** | *How long have you been waiting?* |
| **For + period of time** | *I have lived here **for 10 years**.* |
| **Since + point in time** | *I have lived here **since 2014**.* |
| **When + past simple** (asking about a specific past time) | *When did you move here?* |

---

#### 16. Present Perfect vs. Past Simple

| Contrast | Present Perfect | Past Simple |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Unspecified vs. specific time** | *I have seen that movie.* | *I saw that movie **yesterday**.* |
| **Recent events vs. finished time** | *I have just eaten.* | *I ate at 6 p.m.* |

---

#### 17. Have and Have Got

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Possession and relationships (often interchangeable) |

**Examples:**
- *I **have** a car.* / *I've **got** a car.*
- *She **has** two brothers.* / *She's **got** two brothers.*

> **Note:** *Have got* is more common in British English and informal speech. For negatives and questions:
> - *I don't have a car.* / *I haven't got a car.*
> - *Do you have a car?* / *Have you got a car?*

---

### D. Future Forms

English uses several different structures to talk about the future, depending on whether the action is a plan, a schedule, a decision made at the moment, or a prediction.

---

#### 18. Present Continuous for the Future (I am doing)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Arrangements already made (time, place, or means confirmed) |

**Examples:**
- *I'm meeting Sarah tomorrow.*
- *I'm leaving tomorrow. I've got my plane ticket.*

---

#### 19. Present Simple for the Future (I do)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Timetables, schedules, and fixed programs |

**Examples:**
- *The train leaves at 9.*
- *What time does the film begin?*

---

#### 20. (be) going to (I am going to do)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Pre-existing intention or decision; evidence-based predictions |

**Examples:**
- *I've decided not to stay here any longer. I'm going to leave tomorrow.*
- *Look at those black clouds! It's going to rain.*

---

#### 21. Will and Shall

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Spontaneous decisions, promises, offers, predictions, future facts, polite suggestions |

**Examples:**
- *That bag looks heavy. I'll help you with it.* (decision now)
- *I won't tell anyone. I promise.*
- *You'll love this book.* (prediction)
- *Shall we go?* (polite suggestion)

> **Note:** *Shall* is less common in modern English, especially in American English. It is mostly used with *I* and *we* for suggestions or formal statements.

---

#### 22. Will vs. (be) going to

| Contrast | Will | (be) going to |
|----------|------|---------------|
| **Decision timing** | *I will call you later.* (decision at the moment of speaking) | *I'm going to call her.* (already planned) |
| **Prediction basis** | *I think it will rain.* (general prediction) | *Look at those clouds! It's going to rain.* (evidence-based) |

---

#### 23. Future Continuous (will be doing)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Action in progress at a specific future time; polite inquiries about future plans |

**Examples:**
- *This time tomorrow, I will be flying to Paris.*
- *This time next week I'll be lying on a beach.*
- *What time will you be leaving tomorrow?* (polite)

---

#### 24. Future Perfect (will have done)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Action completed before a certain future time |

**Examples:**
- *I will have finished the project by Friday.*
- *I won't be here this time tomorrow. I'll have already left.*

---

#### 25. Future Perfect Continuous (will have been doing)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Form** | will + have + been + verb-ing |
| **Use** | Emphasizes duration of an action up to a specific future time |

**Examples:**
- *By next year, I will have been working here for ten years.*
- *She will have been studying for six hours by the time the exam ends.*

---

#### 26. Be to + Infinitive; Be about to + Infinitive

##### Be to + Infinitive

Used for **formal plans, official arrangements, commands, and scheduled events**. Common in news reports and formal writing.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Official arrangements** | *The president **is to visit** France next week.* |
| **Commands / instructions** | *You **are to report** to the manager immediately.* |
| **Scheduled events** | *The meeting **is to begin** at 10 AM.* |
| **Destiny / inevitability** | *They **were never to meet** again.* |
| **In if-clauses** (formal conditions) | *If we **are to succeed**, we must work harder.* |

> **Negative form:** *be + not + to + infinitive*
> - *You **are not to tell** anyone about this.*

##### Be about to + Infinitive

Used for the **immediate future** - something that will happen very soon.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Immediate future** | *The train **is about to leave**. Hurry!* |
| **Near future** | *She **was about to call** you when you arrived.* |
| **With *just*** (very immediate) | *I **am just about to** start dinner.* |

> **Past form:** *was/were about to*
> - *I **was about to** leave when the phone rang.*

##### Comparison Table

| Structure | Meaning | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| *is to do* | Formal plan or command | *The CEO **is to resign** next month.* |
| *is about to do* | Immediate future (any moment) | *The movie **is about to start**.* |
| *is going to do* | Intention or prediction | *I **am going to study** tonight.* |
| *will do* | Spontaneous decision or prediction | *I **will call** you later.* |

---

#### 27. Other Ways of Talking About the Future

| Structure | Use | Example |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| **Be due to** | Scheduled / expected at a specific time | *The train **is due to arrive** at 6 PM.* |
| **Be set to** | About to start (often positive expectation) | *The company **is set to launch** a new product.* |
| **Be on the verge of** | Very close to happening | *She **is on the verge of tears**.* |
| **Be on the point of** | Very close to happening | *We **were on the point of giving up**.* |
| **Be likely/unlikely to** | Probability | *It **is likely to rain** later.* / *She **is unlikely to agree**.* |
| **Be bound to** | Almost certain to happen | *You **are bound to succeed** if you try.* |
| **Be sure/certain to** | Definite | *He **is sure to win** the race.* |

**Examples:**
- *The flight **is due to depart** at 8:30.*
- *The company **is set to double** its profits.*
- *They **were on the verge of signing** the contract.*
- *She **was on the point of calling** the police.*
- *It **is bound to cause** problems.*

---

#### 28. The Future Seen from the Past

When talking about a past time, we use special structures to refer to actions that were **future from that past perspective**.

| Structure | Meaning | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **would + infinitive** | Future from the past (past of *will*) | *He said he **would call** later.* |
| **was/were going to** | Past intention that may not have happened | *I **was going to call** you, but I forgot.* |
| **was/were to** | Planned future from the past (formal) | *They **were to meet** at noon, but he never showed up.* |
| **was/were about to** | Immediate future from the past | *I **was about to leave** when you arrived.* |
| **was/were on the verge of** | Very close to happening from the past | *She **was on the verge of winning** when she tripped.* |

##### Detailed Examples

| Structure | Example | Note |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **would** | *He promised he **would come**.* | Past of *will* |
| **was/were going to** | *We **were going to go** to the beach, but it rained.* | Unrealized plan |
| **was/were to** | *She **was to become** a famous scientist.* | Destiny from the past |
| **was/were about to** | *I **was about to eat** when the phone rang.* | Interrupted immediate action |
| **Past continuous** | *They **were meeting** at 5 PM, but he was late.* | Past arrangement |
| **Past perfect continuous** | *He **had been going to** call her for weeks.* | Duration of intention |

##### Important Distinction

| Past Future Form | Implication |
|------------------|-------------|
| *He **said he would come**.* | Neutral statement about a past prediction/promise |
| *He **was going to come**.* | Implies he didn't come (unrealized intention) |
| *He **was to come** at 5 PM.* | Formal arrangement (may or may not have happened) |
| *He **was about to come** when...* | Action was interrupted |

### E. Future Time Clauses & Conditionals (The "Present Future" Rule)

**Important rule:** Do not use *will* in subordinate clauses introduced by time or condition words. Use the present simple instead.

**Subordinating conjunctions affected:** *when, if, while, before, after, as soon as, until, unless.*

| ❌ Incorrect | ✓ Correct |
|-------------|-----------|
| *I'll call you when I **will arrive**.* | *I'll call you when I **arrive**.* |
| *If it **will rain**, we'll stay home.* | *If it **rains**, we'll stay home.* |
| *Before I **will leave**, I'll say goodbye.* | *Before I **leave**, I'll say goodbye.* |
| *I hope to see you before I **will leave**.* | *I hope to see you before I **leave**.* |
| *If we **won't hurry**, we'll be late.* | *If we **don't hurry**, we'll be late.* |

**Additional examples:**
- *I'll call you when I've finished.* (present perfect, not future)
- *When it rains, the roads get wet.* (general truth - zero conditional)
- *When you are in London again, come and see us.*
- *As soon as he arrives, we will start.*

|--------------|------|-------------|---------|

| Present Continuous | am/is/are + V-ing | Now, temporary | *She is working now.* |
| Past Simple | V2 | Completed past | *She worked yesterday.* |
| Past Continuous | was/were + V-ing | In progress at past time | *She was working at 5 PM.* |
| Past Perfect | had + V3 | Before another past | *She had worked before I arrived.* |
| Past Perfect Continuous | had + been + V-ing | Duration before past | *She had been working for hours.* |

| Future Continuous | will + be + V-ing | In progress at future time | *She will be working at 5 PM.* |
| Future Perfect | will + have + V3 | Completed before future | *She will have worked by 5 PM.* |
