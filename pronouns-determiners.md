## 16. Pronouns and Determiners

**Pronouns** are words that replace nouns or noun phrases. **Determiners** are words that come before a noun or pronoun to help specify or identify it, limiting its reference.

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 19. Adjectives and Adverbs](./adjectives-adverbs.md)* for relative pronouns vs. relative adverbs and interrogative pronouns vs. interrogative adverbs; *[Section 15. Articles and Nouns](./articles-nouns.md)* for articles; and *[Section 17. Clause Concepts](./clause-concepts.md)* and *[Section 18. Relative Clauses](./relative-clauses.md)* for adjective clauses and adverbial clauses.

---

### A. Pronouns

#### 1. Personal Pronouns

Personal pronouns refer to specific people or things and change form based on their grammatical function (subject or object) and number (singular or plural).

##### Subject Pronouns (Used as the subject of a verb)

| Pronoun | Example |
|---------|---------|
| *I* | *I* went to the store. |
| *you* | *You* are studying grammar. |
| *he* | *He* is tall. |
| *she* | *She* sings beautifully. |
| *it* | *It* is raining. |
| *we* | *We* will arrive soon. |
| *they* | *They* are playing outside. |

##### Object Pronouns (Used as direct object, indirect object, or object of a preposition)

| Pronoun | Example |
|---------|---------|
| *me* | Give the book to *me*. / She told *me* a story. |
| *you* | I saw *you*. |
| *him* | I saw *him*. |
| *her* | I called *her*. |
| *it* | I fixed *it*. |
| *us* | They invited *us*. |
| *them* | Call *them* later. |

---

#### 2. Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns show ownership and **stand alone** (they replace a noun phrase).

| Pronoun | Meaning | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| *mine* | my book/thing | *That book is **mine**.* |
| *yours* | your book/thing | *Is this pen **yours**?* |
| *his* | his book/thing | *The blue car is **his**.* |
| *hers* | her book/thing | *The responsibility is **hers**.* |
| *ours* | our book/thing | *The decision is **ours**.* |
| *theirs* | their book/thing | *The profits are **theirs**.* |

> **Related forms:** *of mine, my own* (e.g., *a friend of mine*)

---

#### 3. Reflexive Pronouns

Reflexive pronouns are used when the **subject and object** of a verb are the same person or thing. They direct the action back to the subject.

| Pronoun | Example |
|---------|---------|
| *myself* | *I hurt **myself**.* |
| *yourself* | *You should prepare **yourself**.* |
| *himself* | *He blamed **himself**.* |
| *herself* | *She taught **herself**.* |
| *itself* | *The cat licked **itself**.* |
| *ourselves* | *We enjoyed **ourselves**.* |
| *yourselves* | *You all should behave **yourselves**.* |
| *themselves* | *They introduced **themselves**.* |

**Expressions with Reflexive Pronouns:**

| Expression | Meaning | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| *on my own / by myself* | alone, independently | *I live **on my own**.* |
| *for myself* | for my own benefit | *I bought this **for myself**.* |

---

#### 4. Interrogative Pronouns

Interrogative pronouns are used to ask questions.

| Pronoun | Use | Example |
|---------|-----|---------|
| *who* | Subject (for people) | *Who* is at the door? |
| *whom* | Object (for people - formal) | *Whom* did you call? |
| *whose* | Possessive (for people) | *Whose* is this jacket? |
| *what* | Things or actions | *What* do you want? |
| *which* | Choosing from a limited set | *Which* is faster? |

> **Note:** *Whom* is less common in modern informal English and is often replaced by *who*.

---

#### 5. Indefinite Pronouns

Indefinite pronouns refer to **non-specific** people, places, or things. They are generally treated as **singular** and take singular verbs.

> **Note:** *somewhere, anywhere, nowhere, everywhere* are NOT pronouns. They are adverbs of place. Therefore, they are omitted in this section.

| Category | Pronouns |
|----------|----------|
| **People** | *someone, somebody, anyone, anybody, no one, nobody, everyone, everybody* |
| **Things** | *something, anything, nothing, everything* |

**Examples:**

| Pronoun | Example |
|---------|---------|
| *someone / somebody* | *Someone* is knocking. |
| *anyone / anybody* | *Anyone* can do this. (questions/negatives) |
| *no one / nobody* | *Nobody* was present. |
| *everyone / everybody* | *Everyone* arrived on time. |
| *something* | *Something* smells good. |
| *anything* | *Anything* can happen. (questions/negatives) |
| *nothing* | *Nothing* is impossible. |
| *everything* | *Everything* is ready. |

> **Pronoun agreement:** Indefinite pronouns ending in *-one*, *-body*, and *-thing* are singular. Use *their* (or *his/her*) to refer back to them:  
> *Everyone should bring **their** book.*

---

#### 6. Dummy (Expletive) Pronouns

Dummy pronouns act as grammatical subjects or objects but do **not** refer to anything specific. They fill a necessary slot in the sentence structure. English has two main dummy pronouns: ***it*** and ***there***.

---

##### A. Introductory *It* (Dummy *It*)

***It*** is used as an empty subject to talk about time, weather, distance, general situations, and to introduce opinions or evaluations. It does **not** refer to a concrete object.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Weather** | *It* is raining. / *It* is sunny today. |
| **Time** | *It* is 3 o'clock. / *It* is getting late. |
| **Distance** | *It* is far from here to the station. |
| **General situations** | *It* is noisy in here. / *It* was quiet in the library. |
| **Opinions / Evaluations** (*It + be + adjective + to-infinitive / that-clause*) | *It* is important to study. / *It* is clear that he is right. |
| **Placeholder (forward-referencing)** | *It* seems that he is late. / I find *it* difficult to concentrate. |

---

##### B. Existential *There* (Dummy *There*)

***There*** is used to introduce the **existence** or **presence** of something. It is often called "existential *there*." It does **not** refer to a place (unlike the adverb *there*).

**Structure:** ***There + be + noun phrase (+ location/time)***

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Present** | *There is / There are* | *There is* a book on the table. / *There are* many reasons. |
| **Past** | *There was / There were* | *There was* a party last night. / *There were* fifty guests. |
| **Present Perfect** | *There has been / There have been* | *There has been* a mistake. / *There have been* several accidents. |
| **Past Perfect** | *There had been* | *There had been* warnings before the storm. |
| **Future** | *There will be* | *There will be* a meeting tomorrow. |
| **With Modals** | *There + modal + be* | *There must be* an explanation. / *There should be* enough food. |
| **With *Going to*** | *There is going to be* | *There is going to be* a wedding in June. |
| **With *Seem / Appear*** | *There seems to be / There appear to be* | *There seems to be* a misunderstanding. |

**Subject-Verb Agreement with *There*:**

| Rule | Correct | Incorrect |
|------|---------|-----------|
| Singular noun -> *There is* | *There is a cat in the garden.* | *There are a cat...* |
| Plural noun -> *There are* | *There are two cats in the garden.* | *There is two cats...* |
| First item determines verb in lists | *There is a pen, two books, and a notebook.* | (informal) *There are a pen...* |
| Informal speech: *There's* + plural (common but non-standard) | *There's some cookies on the plate.* (informal) | *There is some cookies...* (formal: *There are*) |

---

##### C. *It Is* vs. *There Is* — Key Distinction

This is one of the most common learner errors. Understand the difference:

| | ***It is...*** (Introductory *It*) | ***There is...*** (Existential *There*) |
|--|-------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Function** | Refers to a **known or previously identified** subject, or introduces a **general situation/opinion** | Introduces something **new** — states that something **exists** or is **present** |
| **The noun is...** | Already understood from context, or forward-referenced by *that/to* clause | **New information** — indefinite (cannot use *the* or a proper noun) |
| **Typical context** | Weather, time, distance, opinions, evaluations | Listing, locating, describing situations |

**Key Rule:** Use *there* when you are **introducing something for the first time**. Use *it* when the thing is **already known** or you are describing a **general state.**

| Correct | Incorrect | Explanation |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| ***There is** a park near my house.* | ***It is** a park near my house.* | Introducing the park for the first time -> *there* |
| ***It is** beautiful.* (the park already mentioned) | ***There is** beautiful.* | Referring back to a known subject -> *it* |
| ***There is** a letter for you.* | ***It is** a letter for you.* | Introducing new information -> *there* |
| ***It is** cold today.* | ***There is** cold today.* | General state (weather) -> *it*, not *there* |

**Compare in Context:**

| Dialogue | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| A: *"What's in the box?"* B: *"**There is** a watch."* | Introducing new information (existence) |
| B: *"**It is** a Rolex."* | Referring back to the *watch* already introduced |

---

##### D. Definite vs. Indefinite Noun Restriction

***There*** structures can only introduce **indefinite** nouns. You cannot use *there* with a definite noun phrase (using *the, my, this, that*, proper nouns).

| Correct | Incorrect | Rule |
|---------|-----------|------|
| ***There is** a book on the table.* | ***There is** the book on the table.* | *The book* is definite — cannot follow *there* |
| ***There is** my friend at the door.* | — | Use: *My friend is at the door.* |
| ***There was** an accident.* | ***There was** the accident.* | *The accident* is definite |

**Exception:** *There is* + definite noun is allowed **only** in answers when listing or reminding someone:

| Context | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Reminding / listing known items | *"What can we do today?"* -> *"Well, **there is the museum**, **there is the park**..."* |

---

##### E. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| ***It has** a book on the table.* | ***There is** a book on the table.* | Use *there is/are* for existence, not *it has* |
| ***There is** raining.* | ***It is** raining.* | Weather -> *it*, not *there* |
| ***It is** a meeting at 3 p.m.* | ***There is** a meeting at 3 p.m.* | Introducing new information -> *there* |
| ***There are** a cat in the room.* | ***There is** a cat in the room.* | Singular noun -> singular verb |
| ***There is** the park near my house.* | ***There is a** park near my house.* | *There* must introduce indefinite nouns |
| ***There is** three problems.* (wrong in formal English) | ***There are** three problems.* | Plural noun -> plural verb (*There's* is informal) |
| ***There has** a problem.* | ***There is** a problem.* or ***There** has **been** a problem.* | *There* + *be*, not *there* + *have* |

#### 7. Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstrative pronouns point to specific things and **stand alone** (they replace a noun).

| Pronoun | Distance | Number | Example |
|---------|----------|--------|---------|
| *this* | near | singular | ***This** is my favorite song.* |
| *that* | far | singular | ***That** was a great movie.* |
| *these* | near | plural | ***These** are delicious.* |
| *those* | far | plural | ***Those** belong to me.* |

> **Compare with Demonstrative Determiners:** *This book* (determiner) vs. *This is mine* (pronoun)

---

#### 8. Reciprocal Pronouns

Reciprocal pronouns express a mutual relationship or action between two or more people.

| Pronoun | Use | Example |
|---------|-----|---------|
| *each other* | two people/things | *They hugged **each other**.* |
| *one another* | three or more | *The team members supported **one another**.* |

> **Note:** In modern English, *each other* and *one another* are often used interchangeably.

---

#### 9. One and Ones (Substitute Pronouns)

*One* and *ones* are used to replace a countable noun that has already been mentioned or is understood from context. They avoid repetition.

| Pronoun | Use | Example |
|---------|-----|---------|
| *one* | singular | *I need a pen. Do you have **one**?* |
| *ones* | plural | *I like these shoes, not those **ones**.* |

**With adjectives:** *one/ones* can follow an adjective.

| Without *one/ones* (repetitive) | With *one/ones* (natural) |
|--------------------------------|---------------------------|
| *I'd like the blue **cup**, not the red **cup**.* | *I'd like the blue **one**, not the red **one**.* |
| *These apples are fresh. Those **apples** are not.* | *These apples are fresh. Those **ones** are not.* |

**With determiners:** *one/ones* can follow *this, that, which, another, the other, either, neither*.

| Example |
|---------|
| *Which **one** do you prefer?* |
| *I'll take this **one**.* |
| *I don't like this dress. Show me another **one**.* |

> **Note:** Do not use *one/ones* after possessive determiners (*my, your, his, her, etc.*) without an adjective.
> - Correct: *Is this your coat? - Yes, it's **mine**.* (Not: *Yes, it's my one.*)
> - Correct: *Is this your new coat? - Yes, it's my new **one**.* (with adjective)

---

#### 10. So and Not as Substitutes (for Clauses)

*So* and *not* can replace whole clauses (usually after certain verbs like *think, believe, hope, expect, suppose, guess, be afraid*).

| Usage | Example |
|-------|---------|
| **Affirmative substitute** (using *so*) | *"Will she come?" - "I think **so**."* (= I think she will come) |
| **Negative substitute** (using *not*) | *"Is it raining?" - "I hope **not**."* (= I hope it is not raining) |

**Common verbs used with *so* and *not*:**

| Verb | Affirmative | Negative |
|------|-------------|----------|
| *think* | *I think **so**.* | *I don't think **so**.* / *I think **not**.* (less common) |
| *believe* | *I believe **so**.* | *I don't believe **so**.* |
| *hope* | *I hope **so**.* | *I hope **not**.* (not *I don't hope so*) |
| *expect* | *I expect **so**.* | *I expect **not**.* / *I don't expect **so**.* |
| *suppose* | *I suppose **so**.* | *I don't suppose **so**.* / *I suppose **not**.* |
| *guess* (informal) | *I guess **so**.* | *I guess **not**.* |
| *be afraid* | *I'm afraid **so**.* | *I'm afraid **not**.* |

> **Important:** *I hope not* and *I'm afraid not* are common. *I don't hope so* is incorrect.

---

#### 11. Relative Pronouns

Relative pronouns introduce **adjective clauses** (relative clauses) and connect them to a noun or pronoun. For detailed coverage, see **[Section 18. Relative Clauses](./relative-clauses.md)**.

| Pronoun | Use | Example |
|---------|-----|---------|
| *who* | people (subject) | *The man **who** called is my uncle.* |
| *whom* | people (object - formal) | *The woman **whom** I met is a doctor.* |
| *whose* | people/things (possession) | *The student **whose** phone rang was embarrassed.* |
| *which* | things/animals | *The book, **which** I read yesterday, was great.* |
| *that* | people or things (restrictive) | *The car **that** I bought is red.* |

> **Distinction:** *That* is used for restrictive clauses (essential information). *Which* is often used for non-restrictive clauses (extra information, with commas).

---

#### 12. Interrogative Pronouns vs. Relative Pronouns

| Aspect | Interrogative Pronouns | Relative Pronouns |
|--------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **Function** | Ask a **question** | Introduce an **adjective clause** (modify a noun) |
| **Sentence Type** | Questions (interrogative sentences) | Complex sentences (main clause + relative clause) |
| **Refer back to a noun?** | No - they ask for unknown information | Yes - they refer to a noun mentioned earlier (antecedent) |
| **Common words** | *who, whom, whose, what, which* | *who, whom, whose, which, that* |

| Pronoun | As Interrogative Pronoun (asks a question) | As Relative Pronoun (introduces adjective clause) |
|---------|---------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| *who* | ***Who** called?* | *The man **who** called is my uncle.* |
| *whom* | ***Whom** did you see?* | *The woman **whom** I met is a doctor.* |
| *whose* | ***Whose** is this jacket?* | *The student **whose** phone rang was embarrassed.* |
| *which* | ***Which** is faster?* | *The book, **which** I read yesterday, was great.* |
| *what* | ***What** do you want?* | ❌ Not used as relative pronoun (use *that* or *which*) |
| *that* | ❌ Not used as interrogative pronoun | *The car **that** I bought is red.* |

---

### B. Determiners

Determiners are words placed before nouns (or noun phrases) to clarify meaning, specify quantity, or indicate possession. They signal that a noun is coming.

#### 1. Articles

Articles are the most basic determiners in English.

| Determiner | Use | Example |
|------------|-----|---------|
| *a* / *an* | Indefinite (non-specific, singular countable) | *I need **a** pen.* / *She ate **an** apple.* |
| *the* | Definite (specific, any noun type) | ***The** book on the table is mine.* |

> **Zero article** (no article) is also important: *I like coffee.* (general - see **[Section 15. Articles and Nouns](./articles-nouns.md)** for details.)

---

#### 2. Possessive Determiners

Possessive determiners (also called possessive adjectives) come **before a noun** to show ownership.

| Determiner | Meaning | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| *my* | belonging to me | *This is **my** book.* |
| *your* | belonging to you | *Is that **your** car?* |
| *his* | belonging to him | *That is **his** house.* |
| *her* | belonging to her | *I like **her** dress.* |
| *its* | belonging to it | *The dog wagged **its** tail.* |
| *our* | belonging to us | ***Our** team won.* |
| *their* | belonging to them | *They sold **their** house.* |

---

#### 3. Demonstrative Determiners

Demonstrative determiners come **before a noun** to point to specific things.

| Determiner | Distance | Number | Example |
|------------|----------|--------|---------|
| *this* | near | singular | ***This** book is interesting.* |
| *that* | far | singular | ***That** car is fast.* |
| *these* | near | plural | ***These** cookies are delicious.* |
| *those* | far | plural | ***Those** shoes are expensive.* |

---

#### 4. Basic Determiners (Quantifiers)

| Determiner | Use | Example |
|------------|-----|---------|
| *some* | Positive statements; offers/requests | *I have **some** ideas.* / *Would you like **some** help?* |
| *any* | Negative statements; questions | *I don't have **any** money.* / *Do you have **any** questions?* |
| *no* | Before nouns to negate | *There is **no** milk left.* / *I have **no** intention of going.* |
| *none* | Alone (replaces *no + noun*) | *How many cookies? - **None**.* / ***None** of the students passed.* |
| *much* | Uncountable nouns (questions/negatives) | *Do you have **much** time?* / *I don't eat **much** sugar.* |
| *many* | Plural countable nouns (questions/negatives) | *Are there **many** people?* / *He doesn't have **many** friends.* |
| *little* | Uncountable nouns ("not much") | *I have **little** hope.* (negative) / *Just a **little** sugar.* (positive) |
| *few* | Plural countable nouns ("not many") | ***Few** people understand.* (negative) / *A **few** interesting people.* (positive) |
| *a lot of* | Both countable & uncountable (large quantity) | *We have **a lot of** work.* / ***A lot of** students attended.* |
| *plenty of* | Both countable & uncountable (more than enough) | *There's **plenty of** food.* / *She has **plenty of** experience.* |

#### 4a. *Little, A Little, Few, A Few, Less, Fewer* (Full Contrast)

These quantifiers are among the most commonly confused in English. They express small quantities, but the choice depends on **countable vs. uncountable** nouns and **positive vs. negative** meaning.

---

##### A. Overview Table

| Quantifier | Noun Type | Meaning | Connotation |
|------------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| ***a little*** | Uncountable | Some; a small amount | **Positive** (enough for something) |
| ***little*** | Uncountable | Not much; almost none | **Negative** (insufficient) |
| ***a few*** | Plural countable | Some; a small number | **Positive** (enough for something) |
| ***few*** | Plural countable | Not many; almost none | **Negative** (insufficient) |
| ***less*** | Uncountable | A smaller amount (comparative of *little*) | Neutral |
| ***fewer*** | Plural countable | A smaller number (comparative of *few*) | Neutral |

---

##### B. *Little* vs. *A Little* (Uncountable Nouns)

Both are used with uncountable nouns (*time, money, water, information, hope*), but the **meaning** is different.

| Quantifier | Meaning | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| ***a little*** | Some; a small but sufficient amount (**positive**) | *I have **a little** money, so I can buy lunch.* |
| ***little*** | Not much; almost none — insufficient (**negative**) | *I have **little** money. I can't afford anything.* |

**More examples:**

| Positive (*a little*) | Negative (*little*) |
|-----------------------|---------------------|
| *There is **a little** milk left. Let's share it.* | *There is **little** milk left. We need to buy more.* |
| *She spoke **a little** English, enough to order food.* | *She spoke **little** English, so communication was hard.* |
| *We have **a little** time before the train leaves.* | *We have **little** time. Hurry up!* |

> **Key Tip:** If you can add *"enough to do something"* after the sentence, use ***a little***. If the sentence implies *"not enough,"* use ***little***.

---

##### C. *Few* vs. *A Few* (Plural Countable Nouns)

Both are used with plural countable nouns (*friends, books, people, ideas, days*), but the **meaning** is different.

| Quantifier | Meaning | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| ***a few*** | Some; a small but sufficient number (**positive**) | *I have **a few** friends in this city, so I'm not lonely.* |
| ***few*** | Not many; almost none — insufficient (**negative**) | *I have **few** friends. I feel very isolated.* |

**More examples:**

| Positive (*a few*) | Negative (*few*) |
|--------------------|------------------|
| *We have **a few** good ideas to explore.* | *We have **few** good ideas. Most are impractical.* |
| *There are **a few** cookies left. Want one?* | *There are **few** cookies left. Not enough for everyone.* |
| *He made **a few** minor mistakes.* (acceptable) | *He made **few** mistakes.* (almost perfect — formal) |

> **Key Tip:** If you can add *"enough to be useful/positive"* after the sentence, use ***a few***. If the sentence implies *"not enough"* or *"almost none,"* use ***few***.

> **Note:** In formal English, *few* with no article can sound positive when it means "almost none in a good way": *He is a man of **few** words.* (not negative — means he is concise)

---

##### D. *Less* vs. *Fewer* (Comparative Forms)

*Less* and *fewer* are the comparative forms used to compare quantities. The traditional rule is strict, but modern usage varies.

---

**The Prescriptive Rule (Formal English):**

| Quantifier | Noun Type | Meaning | Example |
|------------|-----------|---------|---------|
| ***less*** | Uncountable nouns | A smaller amount | *There is **less** traffic today.* / *I drink **less** coffee now.* |
| ***fewer*** | Plural countable nouns | A smaller number | *There are **fewer** cars on the road.* / *I buy **fewer** books now.* |

**Examples:**

| Correct | Incorrect | Rule |
|---------|-----------|------|
| *There is **less** water in the lake this year.* | *There is **fewer** water...* | *Water* is uncountable -> *less* |
| *There are **fewer** students this semester.* | *There are **less** students...* | *Students* are countable -> *fewer* |
| *I have **less** free time than before.* | *I have **fewer** free time...* | *Time* is uncountable -> *less* |
| *She made **fewer** mistakes than last time.* | *She made **less** mistakes...* | *Mistakes* are countable -> *fewer* |

---

**The Reality (Modern Usage):**

In everyday spoken and informal written English, ***less*** is often used with countable nouns, especially in certain contexts. This is now so common that some grammarians consider it acceptable in all but the most formal writing.

| Context | *Less* + Countable (Common/Informal) | *Fewer* + Countable (Formal/Traditional) |
|---------|--------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Time, money, distance, weight | *It costs **less** than ten dollars.* | *It costs fewer than ten dollars.* (rare) |
| After *one less* | *I have **one less** problem now.* | *I have one fewer problem now.* (stiff) |
| In everyday speech | *There were **less** people at the party.* | *There were **fewer** people at the party.* |
| On signs (supermarkets) | *"10 items or **less**"* (very common) | *"10 items or **fewer**"* (grammatically correct) |

**Guidelines for learners:**

| Context | Recommendation |
|---------|----------------|
| **Formal writing (academic, business, exams)** | Always use *fewer* for countable nouns |
| **Informal speech and writing** | *Less* + countable is widely accepted |
| **When in doubt** | Use *fewer* for plurals, *less* for uncountable — you'll never be wrong |
| **After numbers and measurements** | *Less* is natural in all registers: *less than 20 minutes / less than 5 miles / less than $50* |

---

##### E. *Less... than* and *Fewer... than* in Comparisons

> **Structure:** *less/fewer + noun + than*

| Sentence | Noun Type | Correct Quantifier |
|----------|-----------|--------------------|
| *I have **less** patience **than** you.* | *Patience* (uncountable) | *less* |
| *We need **fewer** volunteers **than** last year.* | *Volunteers* (countable) | *fewer* |
| *This recipe uses **less** sugar **than** that one.* | *Sugar* (uncountable) | *less* |
| *He invited **fewer** guests **than** she did.* | *Guests* (countable) | *fewer* |
| *The new model has **fewer** problems **than** the old one.* | *Problems* (countable) | *fewer* |
| *She earns **less** money **than** her husband.* | *Money* (uncountable) | *less* |

```bash
Is the noun countable (can you count it: books, people, cars)?
├── YES (countable)
│ ├── Positive meaning ("some, enough")? -> a few
│ ├── Negative meaning ("not many, almost none")? -> few
│ └── Comparing quantities? -> fewer (... than)
│
└── NO (uncountable: water, time, money, information)
├── Positive meaning ("some, enough")? -> a little
├── Negative meaning ("not much, almost none")? -> little
└── Comparing quantities? -> less (... than)

```
---

##### G. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| *I have **few** time.* | *I have **little** time.* | *Time* is uncountable -> *little*, not *few* |
| *She has **little** friends.* | *She has **few** friends.* | *Friends* are countable -> *few*, not *little* |
| *There are **less** cars today.* (formal) | *There are **fewer** cars today.* | Countable noun -> *fewer* (formal) |
| *I drink **fewer** coffee now.* | *I drink **less** coffee now.* | Uncountable noun -> *less* |
| *We have **a few** hope.* | *We have **a little** hope.* | *Hope* is uncountable -> *a little* |
| *He gave me **a little** suggestions.* | *He gave me **a few** suggestions.* | *Suggestions* are countable -> *a few* |
| *I have **a little** money, so I can't buy anything.* | *I have **little** money, so I can't buy anything.* | Negative meaning ("not enough") -> *little*, not *a little* |
| *I have **few** ideas. Want to hear them?* | *I have **a few** ideas. Want to hear them?* | Positive meaning ("some") -> *a few*, not *few* |

#### 5. Quantity/Proportion Determiners

| Determiner | Meaning | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| *all / all of* | Entire quantity or group | ***All** students must attend.* / ***All of** the water was gone.* |
| *most / most of* | Majority (more than half) | ***Most** people agree.* / ***Most of** the guests have left.* |
| *none of* | Zero proportion | ***None of** the options were suitable.* |
| *each / each of* | Every individual item (singular) | ***Each** child received a prize.* / ***Each of** the participants was given a certificate.* |
| *every* | All individual members (singular, emphasizes distribution) | ***Every** student has a textbook.* |
| *both / both of* | Two things together | ***Both** sisters are doctors.* / ***Both of** my parents are teachers.* |
| *either / either of* | One of two options | *You can choose **either** path.* / ***Either of** these solutions will work.* |
| *neither / neither of* | Not either (negates both options) | ***Neither** answer is correct.* / ***Neither of** the two teams won.* |

---

#### 6. Interrogative Determiners (Question Determiners)

Interrogative determiners ask questions and come **before a noun**.

| Determiner | Use | Example |
|------------|-----|---------|
| *which* | choosing from a limited set | ***Which** movie do you want to see?* |
| *what* | open-ended choice | ***What** color is your car?* |
| *whose* | asking about possession | ***Whose** phone is this?* |

> **Contrast with Interrogative Pronouns:** ***Which** is yours?* (pronoun) vs. ***Which** book is yours?* (determiner)

---

#### 7. Whole / Every / All (Nuances)

| Determiner | Meaning | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| *all* | Entire quantity or group (general, plural/uncountable) | ***All** the cookies were eaten.* / ***All** the information was helpful.* |
| *every* | Each individual member (singular, emphasizes distribution) | ***Every** student received an award.* / ***Every** page is important.* |
| *whole* | Entire thing (often with *the, a, his*, etc.) | *He ate **the whole** pizza.* / *She read **the whole** book.* / ***The whole** family went.* |

---

#### 8. Determiners: Summary of Quantifiers

| | Countable (Singular) | Countable (Plural) | Uncountable |
|---|---------------------|--------------------|--------------|
| **large quantity** | - | *many, a lot of, plenty of* | *much, a lot of, plenty of* |
| **small quantity** | - | *a few, few* | *a little, little* |
| **comparative (smaller quantity)** | - | *fewer* | *less* |
| **sufficient quantity** | - | *enough* | *enough* |
| **zero quantity** | *no, none of* | *no, none of, not any* | *no, none of, not any* |

### C. Quick Reference: Pronouns vs. Determiners

| Function | Pronouns (Stand Alone) | Determiners (Before Nouns) |
|----------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| Possession | *mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs* | *my, your, his, her, its, our, their* |
| Demonstration | *this, that, these, those* | *this, that, these, those* |
| Interrogative | *who, whom, what, which, whose* | *which, what, whose* |
| Quantity (positive) | *some, none* | *some, no* |
| Quantity (questions/negatives) | *none* | *any, much, many* |
| Proportion | *all, most, none, each, both, either, neither* | *all, most, no, each, every, both, either, neither* |
| Indefinite people/things | *someone, anybody, nothing, etc.* | (Not applicable - these are pronouns) |
| Relative | *who, whom, whose, which, that* | (Not applicable - these are pronouns) |
| Reciprocal | *each other, one another* | (Not applicable - these are pronouns) |
| Substitution | *one, ones, so, not* | (Not applicable - these are pronouns) |
| Articles | (Not applicable) | *a, an, the* |

### D. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| *Me and John went.* | *John and I went.* | Subject pronoun (*I*), not object (*me*); put others first |
| *Between you and **I**.* | *Between you and **me**.* | Prepositions take object pronouns (*me, him, her*) |
| *The dog wagged **it's** tail.* | *The dog wagged **its** tail.* | *Its* = possessive (no apostrophe); *It's* = *it is* |
| *Everyone should bring **his** book.* | *Everyone should bring **their** book.* | Singular *they* for gender neutrality |
| *Who did you see? - **Me**.* | *Who did you see? - **I** did.* | Formal answer uses subject pronoun |
| *I hurt **me**.* | *I hurt **myself**.* | Reflexive pronoun needed when subject and object are same |
| *Is this your coat? - Yes, it's **my one**.* | *Yes, it's **mine**.* | Don't use *one* after possessive determiners |
| *"Will she come?" - "I hope **so**."* (correct) | - | Affirmative substitute uses *so* |
| *"Is it raining?" - "I don't hope **so**."* | *I hope **not**.* | Negative substitute for *hope* is *not*, not *don't hope so* |
| *Which **one** do you want?* (correct) | - | *One* can follow *which* |
| *There are **less** people here.* (formal) | *There are **fewer** people here.* | Countable noun -> *fewer* (formal); *less* is common informally |
| *I have **fewer** patience than you.* | *I have **less** patience than you.* | *Patience* is uncountable -> *less* |
| *I have **a little** coins in my pocket.* | *I have **a few** coins in my pocket.* | *Coins* are countable -> *a few* |
| *She feels lonely because she has **a few** friends.* | *She feels lonely because she has **few** friends.* | Negative meaning ("not many") -> *few*, not *a few* |
| *I have **few** friends.* (negative meaning) | *I have **a few** friends.* (positive meaning) | *Few* = not many; *a few* = some |
