## 1.3 Numbers, Dates, and Time

This section covers the correct ways to write and express numbers, dates, and time in English, including common variations between British and American English.

#### 3. Rules for Writing Numbers

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Spell out numbers one through nine** | *She has **five** brothers.* / *I need **three** eggs.* |
| **Use digits for numbers 10 and above** | *He has **15** cousins.* / *The meeting lasted **20** minutes.* |
| **Spell out numbers at the beginning of a sentence** | ***Twenty-five** people attended.* (Not: *25 people attended*) |
| **Be consistent within a sentence** | *She bought 5 apples and 12 oranges.* (Not mixing: *five apples and 12 oranges*) |
| **Use digits for ages, dates, time, percentages, decimals, measurements** | *He is **8** years old.* / *It costs **50%** more.* / ***2.5** kilometers* |
| **Large numbers** (use commas as thousands separators) | *1,000,000* (one million) / *10,500* (ten thousand five hundred) |
| **Decimals** (use a period, not a comma) | *3.14* / *0.5* (not *3,14* or *0,5*); (three point one four, or three and fourteen hundredths) / (zero point five, or one half, or five tenths)
 |
| **Percentages** | *25%* (or *25 percent* in formal writing) |

#### 5. Roman Numerals

| Roman | Number | Common Use |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| I | 1 | Clock faces, outlines, movie sequels (Rocky II) |
| IV | 4 | Clock faces (IIII is also common), movie sequels (Rocky IV) |
| V | 5 | Clock faces, outlines, Super Bowl numbers (Super Bowl V) |
| IX | 9 | Movie sequels (e.g., Star Wars Episode IX), outlines |
| X | 10 | Clock faces, outlines, X-ray (named for X-shaped rays) |
| XL | 40 | Historical dates (e.g., 1940 = MCMXL) |
| L | 50 | Outlines, historical dates (e.g., Louis XIV) |
| XC | 90 | Years (e.g., 1990 = MCMXC) |
| C | 100 | Centuries (21st century = XXI), years (e.g., 1900 = MCM) |
| CD | 400 | Historical dates (e.g., 400 AD = CD) |
| D | 500 | Historical dates, formal outlines |
| CM | 900 | Years (e.g., 1900 = MCM, 900 = CM) |
| M | 1,000 | Years (e.g., 2000 = MM), book chapters, movie copyright dates |

> **Notes:**   
    - **No zero:** Roman numerals have no symbol for zero.  
    - **Additive principle:** III = 3 (I + I + I), VI = 6 (5 + 1).  
    - **Subtractive principle:** IV = 4 (5 − 1), IX = 9 (10 − 1).  
    - **Only subtract powers of ten (I, X, C)** before the next two higher numerals:    
    ✓ IV (4), IX (9), XL (40), XC (90), CD (400), CM (900)    
    ✗ IL (49 — incorrect; should be XLIX).

### B. Dates

#### 1. Writing Dates (Format)

| Format | Example | Region |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Month Day, Year** | *December 25, 2024* | American English (US) |
| **Day Month Year** | *25 December 2024* | British English (UK) |
| **Numerical (US)** | *12/25/2024* | Month/Day/Year |
| **Numerical (UK)** | *25/12/2024* | Day/Month/Year |
| **International (ISO)** | *2024-12-25* | Year-Month-Day (recommended for clarity) |

> **Warning:** *02/03/2024* means **February 3, 2024** in the US but **2 March 2024** in the UK. Always clarify or use the ISO format.

#### 2. Saying Dates

| Written | Spoken (US) | Spoken (UK) |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| December 25, 2024 | *December twenty-fifth, twenty twenty-four* | *The twenty-fifth of December, twenty twenty-four* |
| 25 December 2024 | *December twenty-fifth, twenty twenty-four* | *The twenty-fifth of December, twenty twenty-four* |
| 01/01/2024 | *January first, twenty twenty-four* | *The first of January, twenty twenty-four* |

#### 3. Ordinal Numbers in Dates

In spoken English, dates use ordinal numbers (1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, etc.). In written English, ordinals are often omitted in numerical formats.

| Written | Spoken |
|---------|--------|
| *July 4* | *July **fourth*** |
| *4 July* | *The **fourth** of July* |
| *April 1* | *April **first*** |

#### 4. Years

| Year | Spoken Form |
|------|--------------|
| 1066 | *ten sixty-six* |
| 1776 | *seventeen seventy-six* |
| 1900 | *nineteen hundred* |
| 1901 | *nineteen oh-one* |
| 1945 | *nineteen forty-five* |
| 2000 | *two thousand* |
| 2001 | *two thousand one* or *twenty oh-one* |
| 2024 | *twenty twenty-four* |
| 2100 | *twenty-one hundred* |

#### 5. Centuries

| Century | Years | Written Form |
|---------|-------|--------------|
| 1st century | 1-100 | *the first century* |
| 19th century | 1801-1900 | *the nineteenth century* |
| 20th century | 1901-2000 | *the twentieth century* |
| 21st century | 2001-2100 | *the twenty-first century* |

> **Note:** Do not capitalize centuries unless part of a title: *the **twentieth century*** (not *Twentieth Century*).

#### 6. Decades

| Decade | Written Form |
|--------|--------------|
| 1980-1989 | *the 1980s* or *the eighties* |
| 1990s | *the 1990s* or *the nineties* |

> **Note:** Do not use an apostrophe for plural decades: *the 1990s* (not *the 1990's*).

#### 7. Prepositions with Dates

| Preposition | Use | Example |
|-------------|-----|---------|
| **on** | Specific dates (US) | *The meeting is **on** December 5th.* |
| **in** | Months, years, seasons | *It happened **in** December.* / ***in** 2024* / ***in** summer* |
| **at** | Specific times (not dates) | *We arrived **at** 5 PM.* |

> **British English:** *on* is often omitted before dates: *The meeting is December 5th.* (without *on*)

#### 8. Common Date Phrases

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| *today* | the current day |
| *yesterday* | the day before today |
| *tomorrow* | the day after today |
| *the day before yesterday* | two days ago |
| *the day after tomorrow* | two days from now |
| *last week / month / year* | the previous week/month/year |
| *next week / month / year* | the following week/month/year |
| *this week / month / year* | the current week/month/year |
| *a week from today* | seven days from now |

### C. Time

#### 1. Clock Time Formats

| Format | Example | Region |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **12-hour clock** | *3:15 PM* | Common in US, UK, Canada, Australia (everyday use) |
| **24-hour clock** | *15:15* | Military, aviation, healthcare, many European countries |

#### 2. Saying the Time (12-hour clock)

| Written | Spoken |
|---------|--------|
| 9:00 | *nine o'clock* / *nine* / *nine AM* |
| 9:05 | *five past nine* (UK) / *nine oh-five* (US) |
| 9:10 | *ten past nine* / *nine ten* |
| 9:15 | *quarter past nine* / *nine fifteen* |
| 9:20 | *twenty past nine* / *nine twenty* |
| 9:30 | *half past nine* / *nine thirty* |
| 9:35 | *twenty-five to ten* (UK) / *nine thirty-five* (US) |
| 9:45 | *quarter to ten* / *nine forty-five* |
| 9:50 | *ten to ten* / *nine fifty* |
| 9:55 | *five to ten* / *nine fifty-five* |

##### 2.1 24-hour clock

In everyday conversation, **24-hour time** is usually converted to 12-hour time. However, in **military, aviation, public transit, and formal schedules**, you may hear it spoken as follows:

| Written | Spoken (Formal/Military) | Spoken (Casual Conversion) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 15:00 | fifteen hundred hours | 3:00 / three o'clock (PM) |
| 15:05 | fifteen zero five hours | five past three (UK) / three oh-five (US) |
| 15:10 | fifteen ten hours | ten past three / three ten |
| 15:15 | fifteen fifteen hours | quarter past three / three fifteen |
| 15:20 | fifteen twenty hours | twenty past three / three twenty |
| 15:25 | fifteen twenty-five hours | twenty-five past three / three twenty-five |
| 15:30 | fifteen thirty hours | half past three / three thirty |
| 15:35 | fifteen thirty-five hours | twenty-five to four (UK) / three thirty-five (US) |
| 15:40 | fifteen forty hours | twenty to four (UK) / three forty (US) |
| 15:45 | fifteen forty-five hours | quarter to four / three forty-five |
| 15:50 | fifteen fifty hours | ten to four (UK) / three fifty (US) |
| 15:55 | fifteen fifty-five hours | five to four (UK) / three fifty-five (US) |

| Rule | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Add "hundred" for exact hours** | 15:00 = "fifteen hundred hours" |
| **Say each digit after the hour** | 15:25 = "fifteen twenty-five hours" |
| **Use "zero" for 0–9 minutes** | 15:05 = "fifteen zero five hours" |
| **Midnight** | 00:00 = "zero hundred hours" |
| **Noon** | 12:00 = "twelve hundred hours" |

#### 3. AM and PM

| Abbreviation | Meaning | Time Range |
|--------------|---------|------------|
| **AM** | *Ante Meridiem* (before noon) | Midnight (12:00) to Noon (11:59) |
| **PM** | *Post Meridiem* (after noon) | Noon (12:00) to Midnight (11:59) |

> **Note:** *12:00 AM* = midnight. *12:00 PM* = noon. To avoid confusion, use *midnight* or *noon* instead of *12:00 AM/PM*.

| 24-Hour | 12-Hour | Spoken (Casual) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 00:00 | 12:00 AM | midnight |
| 01:00 | 1:00 AM | one in the morning |
| 12:00 | 12:00 PM | noon / twelve o'clock |
| 13:00 | 1:00 PM | one o'clock |
| 15:00 | 3:00 PM | three o'clock |
| 18:30 | 6:30 PM | half past six |
| 21:45 | 9:45 PM | quarter to ten |
| 23:59 | 11:59 PM | one minute to midnight |

#### 4. Prepositions with Time

| Preposition | Use | Example |
|-------------|-----|---------|
| **at** | Specific clock times | *The meeting starts **at** 3 PM.* |
| **in** | Parts of the day (except *night*) | *I work **in** the morning.* / ***in** the afternoon* / ***in** the evening* |
| **at** | With *night* | *I sleep **at** night.* |
| **on** | Days and dates | *She arrived **on** Monday.* |
| **in** | Months, years, seasons | *He was born **in** December.* |
| **by** | Deadline (no later than) | *Please finish **by** 5 PM.* |
| **until / till** | Up to a point | *We waited **until** midnight.* |
| **since** | Starting point (with present perfect) | *I have been here **since** 8 AM.* |
| **for** | Duration | *We talked **for** two hours.* |

### E. Common Errors

| Error | Correct |
|-------|---------|
| *The 1990's* (apostrophe) | *The 1990s* ✓ |
| *It's 5:00 in the morning.* (saying *in* with time) | *It's 5:00 **in the morning**.* (acceptable) / *It's 5:00 AM.* (preferred) |
| *December 31th* | *December 31st* (thirty-**first**) |
| *July 22rd* | *July 22nd* (twenty-**second**) |
| *I will meet you in Monday.* | *I will meet you **on** Monday.* |
| *She was born at December.* | *She was born **in** December.* |
| *The meeting is at 5 PM in the evening.* | *The meeting is at 5 PM.* (PM already means evening) |
