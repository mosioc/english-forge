## 27. Semantics: Word and Sentence Meaning

**Semantics** is the study of **meaning** in language - both at the word level (lexical semantics) and the sentence level (compositional semantics). While grammar tells you what is *correct*, semantics tells you what is *meant*.

---

### A. Lexical Relations: How Words Relate to Each Other

Words do not exist in isolation. They form networks of relationships that structure the vocabulary of English.

#### 1. Synonymy (Same Meaning)

**Definition:** Two or more words with the same or very similar meaning.

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Absolute Synonyms** | Completely interchangeable in all contexts | Very rare; possibly *begin/commence* or *buy/purchase* in some contexts |
| **Near Synonyms (Cognitive Synonyms)** | Similar core meaning but differ in register, collocation, connotation, or dialect | *big/large/great, little/small/tiny, smart/intelligent/clever* |

**Why Absolute Synonymy Is So Rare:**

| Near Synonyms | Same Core Meaning | Difference |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------|
| *big / large* | Of considerable size | *Big* is more informal; *large* is more formal (compare: *a big mistake* vs. *a large mistake*) |
| *smart / intelligent / clever* | Having mental ability | *Smart* is more common in AmE; *clever* implies quick-wittedness; *intelligent* is more formal |
| *buy / purchase* | Acquire by paying money | *Purchase* is more formal; you *buy time* but don't *purchase time* |
| *begin / start / commence* | Initiate an action | *Commence* is very formal; you *start a car* but rarely *begin a car* |
| *friend / pal / buddy / mate* | A person you like | *Pal/buddy* (informal), *mate* (BrE/AusE) |
| *slim / thin / skinny / slender* | Not fat | *Slim/slender* = positive; *thin* = neutral; *skinny* = often negative |

**Collocational Differences (A Key Source of Non-Synonymy):**

| Word Pair | ✓ Natural Collocation | ✗ Unnatural Collocation |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------------|
| *strong / powerful* | *strong coffee* / *powerful engine* | ~~*powerful coffee*~~ / ~~*strong engine*~~ |
| *high / tall* | *high mountain* / *tall person* | ~~*tall mountain*~~ / ~~*high person*~~ |
| *do / make* | *do homework* / *make a mistake* | ~~*make homework*~~ / ~~*do a mistake*~~ |
| *fast / quick* | *fast car* / *quick decision* | ~~*quick car*~~ (colloquial?) / ~~*fast decision*~~ |

#### 2. Antonymy (Opposite Meaning)

**Definition:** Words with opposite or contrasting meanings.

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Gradable Antonyms** | Opposite ends of a scale; can be modified by *very*; one term is unmarked (neutral question form) | *hot/cold, big/small, tall/short, fast/slow, young/old, happy/sad* |
| **Complementary Antonyms** | True opposites; no middle ground; one entails the negation of the other | *alive/dead, true/false, on/off, present/absent, win/lose, pass/fail* |
| **Relational Antonyms (Converses)** | Describe the same relationship from opposite perspectives | *buy/sell, give/receive, parent/child, teacher/student, above/below, before/after, lend/borrow* |
| **Directional Antonyms** | Opposite directions or movements | *up/down, left/right, forward/backward, arrive/depart, ascend/descend, enter/exit* |

**Gradable Antonyms - The Marked/Unmarked Distinction:**

In gradable pairs, one term is "unmarked" - it serves as the neutral question form and does not presuppose a position on the scale.

| Unmarked (Neutral) | Marked (Presupposes) |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| *How tall is he?* (neutral - he could be short) | *How short is he?* (presupposes he is short) |
| *How old is she?* (neutral) | *How young is she?* (presupposes she is young) |
| *How big is it?* (neutral) | *How small is it?* (presupposes it is small) |
| *How fast is it?* (neutral) | *How slow is it?* (presupposes it is slow) |

**Complementary Antonyms - Test:**

If the negation of one entails the other, they are complementary.
- X is dead -> X is not alive ✓
- X is not alive -> X is dead ✓ (complementary)
- X is hot -> X is not cold ✓
- X is not cold -> X is hot ✗ (could be warm) - gradable, not complementary

**Relational Antonyms - Pairs That Imply Each Other:**

| One Perspective | The Other Perspective |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| *She sold the car to him.* | *He bought the car from her.* |
| *John is Mary's husband.* | *Mary is John's wife.* |
| *The picture is above the sofa.* | *The sofa is below the picture.* |

#### 3. Hyponymy and Hypernymy (Hierarchy of Meaning)

**Definition:** A **hypernym** (superordinate) is a general term. A **hyponym** (subordinate) is a specific member of that category.

| Hypernym (General) | Hyponyms (Specific) |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| *animal* | *dog, cat, horse, bird, fish, insect* |
| *dog* | *poodle, bulldog, labrador, spaniel* |
| *vehicle* | *car, truck, bus, motorcycle, bicycle* |
| *furniture* | *chair, table, sofa, bed, desk* |
| *emotion* | *joy, anger, sadness, fear, surprise* |
| *say* | *whisper, shout, murmur, yell, mumble* |
| *walk* | *stroll, stride, march, tiptoe, stumble* |
| *red* | *scarlet, crimson, ruby, vermilion, maroon* |

**Test for Hyponymy:**

"A is a type of B" or "A is a B."
- *A poodle is a type of dog.* ✓ -> *poodle* is a hyponym of *dog*
- *A dog is a type of animal.* ✓ -> *dog* is a hyponym of *animal*
- *A car is a type of vehicle.* ✓
- ~~*A vehicle is a type of car.*~~ ✗ (vehicle is the hypernym, not hyponym)

**Co-Hyponyms:** Words at the same level of the hierarchy.
- *dog, cat, horse* are co-hyponyms of *animal*
- *stroll, stride, march* are co-hyponyms of *walk*

**Transitivity of Hyponymy:**
If A is a hyponym of B, and B is a hyponym of C, then A is a hyponym of C.
- *poodle -> dog -> animal -> living thing*

#### 4. Meronymy (Part-Whole Relationships)

**Definition:** A **meronym** is a part of a **holonym** (the whole).

| Holonym (Whole) | Meronyms (Parts) |
|------------------|-------------------|
| *car* | *engine, wheel, door, window, seat* |
| *body* | *head, arm, leg, heart, brain* |
| *book* | *chapter, page, paragraph, cover* |
| *tree* | *root, trunk, branch, leaf* |
| *house* | *room, roof, wall, floor, window* |
| *computer* | *screen, keyboard, processor, memory* |

**Test for Meronymy:**

"A is a part of B."
- *An engine is a part of a car.* ✓
- *A wheel is a part of a car.* ✓
- ~~*A car is a part of an engine.*~~ ✗

**Holonymy Test (Reverse):**

"B has A as a part."
- *A car has an engine.* ✓

**Hyponymy vs. Meronymy:**

| Relation | Example | Test |
|----------|---------|------|
| **Hyponymy** (is a type of) | *dog -> animal* | *A dog is a type of animal.* ✓ |
| **Meronymy** (is a part of) | *engine -> car* | *An engine is a part of a car.* ✓ |

#### 5. Polysemy vs. Homonymy

These are often confused. Both involve one form having multiple meanings.

##### Polysemy (Related Meanings)

**Definition:** One word with multiple, **historically or conceptually related** meanings.

| Word | Related Meanings | Connection |
|------|------------------|------------|
| *head* | body part / leader / top of something | All share the concept of "top or foremost part" |
| *foot* | body part / bottom of mountain / unit of measurement | The foot is at the bottom; the unit derives from foot length |
| *run* | move quickly / operate (a machine) / manage (a company) / flow (water) | All share a sense of "continuous motion or operation" |
| *mouth* | body part / entrance to a cave or river | Both are openings |
| *paper* | material / newspaper / academic article / document | All originally from papyrus/paper material |
| *bright* | shining light / intelligent | Metaphor: intelligence as light |

##### Homonymy (Unrelated Meanings)

**Definition:** Two **different words** that happen to sound (and sometimes look) the same, with historically **unrelated** meanings.

| Homonyms | Meaning 1 | Meaning 2 | Origin |
|----------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| *bank* (1) | Financial institution | | From Italian *banca* (bench) |
| *bank* (2) | Side of a river | | From Old Norse *bakki* (ridge) |
| *bat* (1) | Flying mammal | | From Old Norse *leðrblaka* |
| *bat* (2) | Sports equipment | | From Old English *batt* (club) |
| *ring* (1) | Circular jewelry | | From Old English *hring* |
| *ring* (2) | Sound of a bell | | From Old English *hringan* (echo) |
| *match* (1) | Stick for making fire | | From Old French *mesche* |
| *match* (2) | Game / contest | | From Old English *gemæcca* (companion) |
| *bear* (1) | Animal | | From Old English *bera* |
| *bear* (2) | Carry / endure | | From Old English *beran* |

**How to Distinguish:**

| Criterion | Polysemy | Homonymy |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Etymology** | Same historical origin | Different historical origins |
| **Meaning relationship** | Meanings are connected (metaphor, metonymy) | Meanings are unrelated |
| **Dictionary entry** | One entry with multiple numbered senses | Separate entries |
| **Intuition** | Native speakers sense a connection | Native speakers see them as different words |

**Test:** If you can explain how the meanings are related, it's polysemy. If they are connected only by chance, it's homonymy.

| Word | Test | Verdict |
|------|------|---------|
| *mole* (animal) / *mole* (spy) | The spy meaning comes from the animal's burrowing behavior | Polysemy |
| *mole* (animal) / *mole* (skin growth) | Unrelated; the skin meaning is from Old English *māl* (spot) | Homonymy |

### B. Thematic Roles (Theta Roles)

#### 1. What Are Thematic Roles?

**Thematic roles** (also called **theta roles** or **semantic roles**) describe the relationship between a verb and its arguments - who does what to whom.

Understanding thematic roles explains:
- Why certain verbs take certain patterns
- How active and passive sentences are related
- Why some sentences are grammatical and others are not

#### 2. Core Thematic Roles

| Role | Definition | Example | Question Answered |
|------|------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Agent** | The doer; the entity that deliberately performs the action | ***John** opened the door.* | Who did it? |
| **Patient (Theme)** | The entity that undergoes the action or is affected by it | *John opened **the door**.* | What was acted upon? |
| **Experiencer** | The entity that experiences a sensation, emotion, or perception | ***She** felt happy. / **I** heard a noise.* | Who felt/perceived it? |
| **Instrument** | The tool or means used to perform the action | *He cut the bread **with a knife**.* | What was used? |
| **Location** | Where the action takes place | *They met **in the park**.* | Where? |
| **Goal** | The endpoint or destination of an action | *She walked **to the store**. / He gave it **to me**.* | To where/whom? |
| **Source** | The starting point or origin of an action | *She came **from Paris**. / I got it **from her**.* | From where/whom? |
| **Beneficiary** | The entity for whose benefit the action is performed | *He baked a cake **for her**.* | For whom? |
| **Recipient** | The entity that receives something | *She gave the keys **to John**.* | To whom? |
| **Stimulus** | What causes the experience | *The noise frightened **them**. / She likes **music**.* | What caused the experience? |
| **Causer** | The non-deliberate cause of an event | ***The wind** blew the door open.* | What caused it (without intention)? |

#### 3. Thematic Roles in Active and Passive Sentences

Thematic roles stay the same when a sentence moves from active to passive voice. The **grammatical roles** (subject, object) change, but the **thematic roles** (agent, patient) remain constant.

| Active Voice | Passive Voice |
|--------------|---------------|
| *John (Agent, Subject) opened the door (Patient, Object).* | *The door (Patient, Subject) was opened by John (Agent, Oblique).* |
| *She (Agent, Subject) wrote the letter (Patient, Object).* | *The letter (Patient, Subject) was written by her (Agent, Oblique).* |
| *The chef (Agent, Subject) prepared the meal (Patient, Object).* | *The meal (Patient, Subject) was prepared by the chef (Agent, Oblique).* |

**Key Insight:** The "by-phrase" in passive sentences always marks the Agent. The subject of a passive sentence is always a Patient or Recipient.

> **See also:** *[Section 8. Passive Voice](./passive-voice.md)*

#### 4. How Thematic Roles Explain Verb Patterns

The same verb can assign different thematic roles, and that produces different meanings or patterns.

**Example: *Open* (Two Frames)**

| Pattern | Agent | Patient | Instrument | Example |
|---------|-------|---------|------------|---------|
| **Transitive (Agentive)** | John | the door | (with a key) | *John opened the door with a key.* |
| **Ergative/Inchoative** | - | the door | - | *The door opened.* |
| **Instrument as Subject** | - | the door | The key | *The key opened the door.* |

**Example: *Break* (Multiple Frames)**

| Pattern | Agent / Causer | Patient | Instrument | Example |
|---------|----------------|---------|------------|---------|
| **Transitive (Agentive)** | John | the window | with a rock | *John broke the window with a rock.* |
| **Transitive (Causative)** | The storm | the window | - | *The storm broke the window.* |
| **Ergative/Inchoative** | - | the window | - | *The window broke.* |
| **Instrument as Subject** | - | the window | A rock | *A rock broke the window.* |

**Example: *Give* (Ditransitive)**

| Frame | Agent | Recipient | Patient | Example |
|-------|-------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **V + IO + DO** | John | Mary (IO) | a book (DO) | *John gave Mary a book.* |
| **V + DO + to + Recipient** | John | Mary | a book | *John gave a book to Mary.* |
| **Passive (Recipient as Subject)** | (by John) | Mary | a book | *Mary was given a book (by John).* |
| **Passive (Patient as Subject)** | (by John) | Mary | a book | *A book was given to Mary (by John).* |

> **See also:** *[Section 14. Verb Complementation](./ing-and-to-forms-verbals.md)* and *[Section 1.5 Word Order (SVO)](./word-order-svo.md)*

#### 5. The Theta Criterion (Why Some Sentences Are Ungrammatical)

The **Theta Criterion** states that:
- Each argument must be assigned **exactly one** thematic role
- Each thematic role must be assigned to **exactly one** argument

| ✓ Grammatical | ✗ Ungrammatical | Explanation |
|----------------|------------------|-------------|
| *John ate the apple.* | *John ate.* (when *ate* is intended as transitive) | *Eat* (transitive) requires a Patient, which is missing |
| *She put the book on the table.* | *She put the book.* | *Put* requires a Goal (Location) |
| *He laughed.* | *He laughed the joke.* | *Laugh* (intransitive) cannot assign a role to an object |
| *It rained.* | *The sky rained.* | *Rain* does not assign an Agent role |
| *She slept.* | *She slept a sleep.* | Unnecessary repetition of meaning |

**Common Learner Errors Explained by Thematic Roles:**

| Error | Role Violation | Correction |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| *I explained her the problem.* | *Explain* assigns Goal as *to + NP*, not as indirect object | *I explained the problem **to** her.* |
| *He suggested me to go.* | *Suggest* takes a Patient (the idea), not a Recipient as indirect object | *He suggested **that I go**.* |
| *She described me the scene.* | *Describe* assigns Recipient as *to + NP* | *She described the scene **to** me.* |
| *The book was arrived.* | *Arrive* is unaccusative (Patient subject) - cannot be passivized | *The book arrived.* |
| *He happened an accident.* | *Happen* assigns Patient as subject, no Agent | *An accident happened **to him**.* |

#### 6. Common Verb Patterns Mapped to Thematic Roles

| Pattern | Thematic Roles | Example Verbs |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| **Intransitive** | Agent | *run, walk, swim, dance, sleep, laugh, work* |
| **Unaccusative (Patient-Subject)** | Patient | *arrive, fall, die, happen, appear, disappear* |
| **Transitive** | Agent + Patient | *hit, build, read, write, eat, open* |
| **Experiencer-Stimulus** | Experiencer + Stimulus | *love, hate, fear, enjoy, hear, see* |
| **Stimulus-Experiencer** | Stimulus + Experiencer | *frighten, surprise, please, worry, amaze* |
| **Ditransitive (Double Object)** | Agent + Recipient + Patient | *give, send, offer, lend, tell, show* |
| **Causative with Location** | Agent + Patient + Location | *put, place, position, stand, sit, lay* |

### C. Sense and Reference

#### 1. Denotation and Connotation

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| **Denotation** | The literal, dictionary definition of a word | *Woman* = adult human female |
| **Connotation** | The associated, emotional, or cultural meaning | *Woman* may connote warmth, nurturing, strength (varies by culture/context) |

**Words with Similar Denotation, Different Connotation:**

| Denotation | Positive Connotation | Neutral Connotation | Negative Connotation |
|------------|---------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| *Not fat* | *slender, slim, svelte* | *thin* | *skinny, scrawny, bony* |
| *Economical* | *thrifty, frugal* | *budget-conscious* | *cheap, stingy, miserly* |
| *Self-confident* | *assertive, confident* | *self-assured* | *arrogant, pushy, cocky* |
| *Curious* | *inquisitive, inquiring* | *questioning* | *nosy, prying, snooping* |
| *Determined* | *persistent, tenacious* | *determined* | *stubborn, obstinate, pig-headed* |
| *Not modern* | *vintage, classic, antique* | *old* | *outdated, obsolete, decrepit* |
| *Careful* | *meticulous, thorough* | *cautious* | *pedantic, nitpicking, fussy* |

#### 2. Sense vs. Reference (Intension vs. Extension)

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| **Sense (Intension)** | The internal meaning of a word - the concept it expresses | *The President of the United States* = the chief executive officer of the US government |
| **Reference (Extension)** | The actual entity in the world the expression points to | *The President of the United States* (said in 2024) = Joe Biden |

| Expression | Sense | Reference (2024) | Reference (2019) |
|------------|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| *The President of the United States* | Chief executive of the US | Joe Biden | Donald Trump |
| *The capital of France* | The seat of government of France | Paris | Paris |
| *The tallest building in the world* | The human-made structure with the greatest height | Burj Khalifa | Burj Khalifa |

**Same Reference, Different Sense:**

| Expression 1 | Expression 2 | Same Reference? | Different Sense? |
|--------------|--------------|-----------------|------------------|
| *The morning star* | *The evening star* | ✓ Yes (both refer to the planet Venus) | ✓ Yes (one is seen in morning, one in evening) |
| *Clark Kent* | *Superman* | ✓ Yes (same person) | ✓ Yes (different identities/presentations) |
| *The author of Hamlet* | *William Shakespeare* | ✓ Yes | ✓ Yes (one describes, one names) |
| *My mother* | *The woman who gave birth to me* | ✓ Yes (same person) | ✓ Yes (one is relational, one is descriptive) |

#### 3. Definite vs. Indefinite Reference

Reference can be **definite** (the hearer can identify the specific referent) or **indefinite** (the hearer cannot identify a specific referent).

| Type | Expression | Example | Condition |
|------|------------|---------|-----------|
| **Definite** | *the book, my car, this house, that idea, she* | *I read **the book** you recommended.* | Hearer knows which one |
| **Indefinite** | *a book, some books, any book, something* | *I read **a book** last night.* | Hearer does not know which one |
| **Generic** | *the tiger, a tiger, tigers* (without specific referent) | ***Tigers** are endangered.* | Refers to the whole class |

### D. Entailment and Presupposition

> **Note:** Presupposition is covered in detail in the Pragmatics section. Here we focus specifically on **entailment** and how it contrasts with presupposition.

#### 1. Entailment

**Definition:** A proposition A **entails** proposition B if, whenever A is true, B must also be true.

| Proposition A | Entailment B | Reason |
|---------------|--------------|--------|
| *John killed the spider.* | *The spider died.* | Killing entails dying |
| *She is a spinster.* | *She is a woman.* | *Spinster* means "unmarried woman" |
| *He solved all the problems.* | *He solved at least one problem.* | All entails some |
| *They have three children.* | *They have at least one child.* | Three entails at least one |
| *It's a red car.* | *It's a car.* | Red car is a type of car |
| *She bought a poodle.* | *She bought a dog.* | *Poodle* is a hyponym of *dog* |

#### 2. Entailment vs. Presupposition

| Feature | Entailment | Presupposition |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| **Survives negation?** | ✗ No | ✓ Yes |
| **Survives questioning?** | ✗ Usually not | ✓ Often |
| **Cancellable?** | ✓ Often | ✗ Less easily |

**Comparison:**

| Statement | Entailment | Presupposition |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| *John stopped smoking.* | John does not smoke now | John used to smoke |
| *John didn't stop smoking.* | John does smoke now | John used to smoke |
| *Did John stop smoking?* | Neither (question) | John used to smoke |
| *The King of France is bald.* | There is a King, he is bald | There is a King of France |
| *The King of France is not bald.* | There is a King, he is not bald | There is a King of France |

**Test for Entailment vs. Presupposition:**

Apply the **negation test**. If the inference survives when you negate the sentence, it's a presupposition. If it does not, it's an entailment.

| Original Sentence | Inference | Negated Sentence | Inference Survives? | Verdict |
|-------------------|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|---------|
| *She regrets leaving.* | She left | *She doesn't regret leaving.* | ✓ Yes | **Presupposition** |
| *She saw a dog.* | She saw an animal | *She didn't see a dog.* | ✗ No | **Entailment** (hyponymy) |
| *He managed to escape.* | He escaped | *He didn't manage to escape.* | ✗ No (he may have failed) | **Entailment** |
| *He managed to escape.* | He tried to escape | *He didn't manage to escape.* | ✓ Yes (he tried but failed) | **Presupposition** |
