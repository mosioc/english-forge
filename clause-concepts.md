## 17. Clause Concepts

A **clause** is a group of words containing a subject and a verb. It is the fundamental unit of a sentence that expresses either a complete thought (independent clause) or a part of a thought (dependent/subordinate clause).

> **Examples:**
> - *"She sings."* (Subject: *She*, Verb: *sings* - a complete thought -> independent clause)
> - *"Because she sings."* (Subject: *she*, Verb: *sings* - incomplete thought -> dependent clause)

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 18. Conjunctions](./conjunctions.md)* for subordinating conjunctions and *[Section 16. Pronouns and Determiners](./pronouns-determiners.md)* for relative pronouns.

---

### A. Types of Clauses

#### 1. Independent (Main) Clause

A clause that can stand alone as a complete sentence because it expresses a complete thought.

- ***The cat slept** on the mat.* (This part can be a sentence by itself.)
- *She is reading a book.*
- *They arrived late.*

#### 2. Dependent (Subordinate) Clause

A clause that **cannot** stand alone as a complete sentence. It relies on an independent clause to make sense. These clauses often begin with a **subordinating conjunction** (*because, although, if, when*, etc.) or a **relative pronoun** (*who, which, that*, etc.) or a **relative adverb** (*where, when, why*).

- ***When the cat slept** on the mat, the dog barked.* (*When the cat slept on the mat* is a subordinate clause.)
- *She stayed home **because she was sick**.*
- *The book **that I borrowed** is excellent.*

---

### B. Functional Classification of Subordinate Clauses

Subordinate clauses function like specific parts of speech within a larger sentence.

#### 1. Adjective Clauses (Relative Clauses)

These clauses function like **adjectives**, modifying a noun or pronoun. They typically begin with **relative pronouns** (*who, whom, whose, which, that*) or **relative adverbs** (*where, when, why*). They answer questions like *"Which one?"* or *"What kind?"*

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| *The book **that I am reading** is fascinating.* | Modifies *book* - which book? |
| *The person **who called you** left a message.* | Modifies *person* - which person? |
| *This is the town **where I grew up**.* | Modifies *town* - which town? |
| *The man **whose car was stolen** reported it.* | Modifies *man* - which man? |

> **Note:** Adjective clauses are also called **relative clauses**. For full details, see **[Section 18. Relative Clauses](./relative-clauses.md)**.

---

#### 2. Adverb Clauses (Adverbial Clauses)

These clauses function like **adverbs**, modifying a verb, adjective, or another adverb. They answer questions like *"When?" "Where?" "Why?" "How?" "To what extent?"* or *"Under what condition?"* They usually begin with **subordinating conjunctions** (*because, since, although, if, when, while, after, before, so that, unless*, etc.).

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| ***Because it was raining**, we stayed inside.* | Modifies *stayed* - why? |
| *I will go **wherever you go**.* | Modifies *go* - where? |
| *Call me **when you arrive**.* | Modifies *call* - when? |
| *He ran faster **than I did**.* | Modifies *faster* - comparison |
| *She studied hard **so that she could pass**.* | Modifies *studied* - purpose |
| ***Although he was tired**, he kept working.* | Modifies *kept* - concession |

---

#### 3. Noun Clauses

These clauses function like **nouns**. They can act as the subject, object, or complement of a sentence. They often begin with words like *that, what, whatever, who, whoever, whom, whomever, which, whichever, when, where, why, how, whether, if*.

| Function | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Subject** | ***What you said** surprised me.* |
| **Direct Object** | *I know **that he is honest**.* |
| **Subject Complement** | *The problem is **how we will solve it**.* |
| **Object of a Preposition** | *Give it to **whoever needs it**.* |
| **Appositive** | *The fact **that he lied** bothered me.* |
| **Adjective Complement** | *I am happy **that you came**.* |

**Examples of Noun Clauses with Different Introducers:**

| Introducer | Example |
|------------|---------|
| *that* | *I believe **that she is right**.* |
| *what* | ***What I need** is a break.* |
| *who/whom* | *I know **who did it**.* |
| *whose* | *I wonder **whose bag this is**.* |
| *which* | *Tell me **which you prefer**.* |
| *when* | *I remember **when we met**.* |
| *where* | *Do you know **where she lives**?* |
| *why* | *I understand **why you're upset**.* |
| *how* | *Show me **how it works**.* |
| *whether/if* | *I wonder **whether she'll come**.* |

---

#### 4. Relative Adverbs (Important Note)

**Relative adverbs** (*where, when, why*) introduce adjective clauses (relative clauses) that modify a noun of **place**, **time**, or **reason**. They replace a **preposition + which**.

| Relative Adverb | Refers to | Replaces | Example |
|----------------|-----------|----------|---------|
| ***where*** | place | *in/at/on which* | *This is the house **where** I grew up.* (= in which) |
| ***when*** | time | *in/on/at which* | *I remember the day **when** we first met.* (= on which) |
| ***why*** | reason | *for which* | *That's the reason **why** I left.* (= for which) |

> **Important:** Relative adverbs are **never pronouns**. They are always adverbs. They introduce a full clause (subject + verb) that modifies a noun.

**Examples in Context:**

| Example | Function |
|---------|----------|
| *This is the restaurant **where** we had our first date.* | Modifies *restaurant* (place) |
| *Do you remember the summer **when** we went to Spain?* | Modifies *summer* (time) |
| *Nobody knows the reason **why** he resigned.* | Modifies *reason* (reason) |

**Comparison: Relative Adverb vs. Relative Pronoun**

| Clause Type | Word | Function | Example |
|-------------|------|----------|---------|
| **Relative Adverb** | *where* | Refers to a place (adverb) | *The house **where** I live is old.* |
| **Relative Pronoun** | *which* | Refers to a thing (pronoun) | *The house **which** I bought is old.* |

> **Note:** In informal English, *that* is often used instead of relative adverbs, and prepositions can be moved to the end of the clause.
> - Formal: *The day **on which** we met was rainy.* / *The day **when** we met was rainy.*
> - Informal: *The day **that** we met was rainy.* / *The day we met was rainy.*

### C. Specialized Clause and Sentence Structures

#### 1. Participle Clauses

These are **reduced clauses** that function like adjectives or adverbs. They start with a **present participle** (*-ing*) or a **past participle** (*-ed / -en*). They are essentially shortened versions of adverb or adjective clauses.

| Type | Example | Full Version |
|------|---------|--------------|
| **Present Participle (adverbial)** | ***Walking down the street**, I saw an old friend.* | *As I was walking down the street...* |
| **Present Participle (adverbial - perfect)** | ***Having finished the work**, she went home.* | *After she had finished the work...* |
| **Present Participle (adjectival)** | *The man **sitting over there** is my uncle.* | *The man **who is sitting** over there...* |
| **Past Participle (adjectival)** | *The car **damaged in the accident** needs repair.* | *The car **that was damaged** in the accident...* |
| **Past Participle (adverbial)** | ***Seen from the hilltop**, the valley looked beautiful.* | *When it was viewed from the hilltop...* |

**Important Rules for Participle Clauses:**

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| The subject of the participle clause must be the same as the main clause | ✗ *Walking home, the rain started.* -> ✓ *Walking home, I got wet.* |
| For two actions by the same subject, use *having + V3* for the first action | ***Having finished** his homework, he went to bed.* |
| Negative form: *not* before the participle | ***Not knowing** the answer, she guessed.* |

---

#### 2. Cleft Sentences

Cleft sentences are used for **emphasis**. They rearrange a basic sentence to highlight a particular piece of information.

**Basic Sentence:** *John broke the window yesterday.*

| Type | Structure | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **It-cleft (emphasizing subject)** | *It + be + emphasized element + that/who + rest* | *It was **John** who broke the window yesterday.* |
| **It-cleft (emphasizing object)** | *It + be + emphasized element + that/who + rest* | *It was **the window** that John broke yesterday.* |
| **It-cleft (emphasizing time)** | *It + be + emphasized element + that/who + rest* | *It was **yesterday** that John broke the window.* |
| **It-cleft (emphasizing place)** | *It + be + emphasized element + that/who + rest* | *It was **in the garage** that John broke the window.* |
| **What-cleft (emphasizing action)** | *What + subject + do + be + emphasized action* | *What John **did** was break the window yesterday.* |
| **What-cleft (emphasizing object)** | *What + subject + verb + be + emphasized object* | *What John **broke** was the window yesterday.* |
| **All-cleft** | *All + subject + do + be + emphasized element* | *All John **did** was break the window.* |

> For full details on cleft sentences and inversion, see **[Section 12. Emphatic Structures (Cleft Sentences)](./emphatic-structures-cleft-sentences.md)**.

---

### D. Reduced Relative Clauses

Relative clauses can be reduced (shortened) when the relative pronoun is the subject of the clause.

| Full Relative Clause | Reduced Form | Condition |
|---------------------|--------------|-----------|
| *The man **who is sitting** there is my uncle.* | *The man **sitting** there is my uncle.* | Active verb -> use *-ing* |
| *The book **that was written** by her is famous.* | *The book **written** by her is famous.* | Passive verb -> use *-ed/-en* |
| *The first person **who arrives** will get a prize.* | *The first person **to arrive** will get a prize.* | With ordinal/superlative -> use *to* infinitive |

> **Note:** Reduced relative clauses cannot be used when the relative clause has a different subject from the main clause or when the verb is in a continuous form with a different meaning.

### E. Subordinating Conjunctions (for Adverb Clauses)

| Function | Conjunctions |
|----------|--------------|
| **Time** | *when, while, after, before, until, till, as soon as, since, once, as* |
| **Reason/Cause** | *because, since, as, inasmuch as* |
| **Purpose** | *so that, in order that, lest* (formal) |
| **Result** | *so... that, such... that* |
| **Condition** | *if, unless, provided that, providing that, as long as, so long as, on condition that* |
| **Concession** | *although, even though, though, whereas, while* |
| **Comparison** | *than, as... as, just as* |
| **Place** | *where, wherever, everywhere* |
| **Manner** | *as, as if, as though, like* (informal) |

**Examples:**

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *although* | *Although it was raining, we went out.* |
| *wherever* | *She follows him wherever he goes.* |
| *as if* | *He talks as if he knew everything.* |
| *lest* (formal) | *He wore a coat lest he catch a cold.* |

### F. Distinguishing Clause Types (Quick Reference)

| Question to Ask | Clause Type |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Does it stand alone as a complete sentence? | **Independent** |
| Does it modify a noun (answer *which one?*)? | **Adjective (Relative)** |
| Does it modify a verb (answer *when? where? why? how?*)? | **Adverb** |
| Does it act as a subject, object, or complement? | **Noun** |
| Is it a reduced form starting with *-ing* or *-ed*? | **Participle** |
| Is it rearranged for emphasis (*it was... that*)? | **Cleft** |

### G. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| *Walking home, the rain started.* | *Walking home, **I got wet as** the rain started.* | Participle clause subject must match main clause |
| *The book which I read it was great.* | *The book which I read was great.* | No repeated pronoun in relative clause |
| *I know what does he want.* | *I know what he wants.* | Noun clause uses statement word order (no inversion) |
| *Although it was raining, but we went out.* | *Although it was raining, we went out.* or *It was raining, but we went out.* | Don't use both *although* and *but* |
| *The man who he called me is my uncle.* | *The man who called me is my uncle.* | Relative pronoun is the subject - no extra pronoun |
| *Because of he was late, we missed the bus.* | *Because he was late, we missed the bus.* | *Because of* + noun; *because* + clause |
| *This is the house where I lived in.* | *This is the house **where** I lived.* or *This is the house I lived **in**.* | Don't use both *where* and a preposition |

> For a full treatment of *although, despite,* and *in spite of*, including structural rules and common errors, see **[Section 20. Conjunctions, C.1](./conjunctions.md#c1-although-despite-in-spite-of-full-contrast)**.

### I. Relative Adverbs: Complete Summary

Relative adverbs introduce adjective clauses that modify a noun of **place**, **time**, or **reason**.

| Relative Adverb | Refers to | Replaces | Function in Clause | Example |
|----------------|-----------|----------|--------------------|---------|
| ***where*** | place | *in/at/on which* | Adverb of place | *This is the café **where** I met her.* |
| ***when*** | time | *in/on/at which* | Adverb of time | *That was the year **when** I graduated.* |
| ***why*** | reason | *for which* | Adverb of reason | *Tell me the reason **why** you called.* |

#### Key Characteristics of Relative Adverbs

| Characteristic | Explanation |
|----------------|-------------|
| **They are never pronouns** | They are always adverbs |
| **They introduce a full clause** | The clause has its own subject and verb |
| **They modify a noun** | The noun refers to a place, time, or reason |
| **They cannot be omitted** | Unlike relative pronouns (*which/that*) which can sometimes be omitted |

#### Examples in Context

| Example | Relative Adverb | Noun Modified |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| *This is the village **where** I was born.* | *where* | *village* (place) |
| *Do you remember the moment **when** we first met?* | *when* | *moment* (time) |
| *That's the reason **why** she left.* | *why* | *reason* (reason) |

#### Informal Alternatives to Relative Adverbs

In informal English, *that* can replace relative adverbs, or the relative adverb can be omitted entirely.

| Formal (Relative Adverb) | Informal (with *that*) | Informal (omitted) |
|--------------------------|------------------------|--------------------|
| *the day **when** we met* | *the day **that** we met* | *the day we met* |
| *the house **where** I live* | *the house **that** I live in* | *the house I live in* |
| *the reason **why** I came* | *the reason **that** I came* | *the reason I came* |

> **Note:** *Why* is often omitted: *That's the reason I came.* (no relative word needed)

#### Relative Adverb vs. Relative Pronoun (Important Distinction)

| | Relative Adverb | Relative Pronoun |
|--|-----------------|------------------|
| **Words** | *where, when, why* | *who, whom, whose, which, that* |
| **Function in clause** | Adverb (modifies the verb) | Pronoun (subject or object) |
| **Example** | *the house **where** I live* -> *I live **there*** | *the house **which** I bought* -> *I bought **it*** |
| **Can be omitted?** | No (but can be replaced by *that* in informal English) | Yes (when object of the clause) |

**Comparison Example:**

| Clause | Type | Analysis |
|--------|------|----------|
| *the house **where** I live* | Relative Adverb | *where* = adverb (I live **there**) |
| *the house **which** I bought* | Relative Pronoun | *which* = object (I bought **it**) |

> **Memory Trick:** If you can replace the relative word with *there*, *then*, or *because*, it's a **relative adverb**.
> - *the house **where** I grew up* -> *I grew up **there*** ✓ (relative adverb)
> - *the house **that** I bought* -> *I bought **it*** ✗ (not *there*) -> relative pronoun
