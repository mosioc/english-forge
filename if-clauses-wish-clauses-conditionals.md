## 7. If-Clauses, Wish-Clauses, and Conditionals

This section covers the full range of conditional structures in English, from real situations to hypothetical scenarios, as well as expressions of wish, regret, and desire for change. It also includes advanced conditional variants, formal alternatives, and useful phrases for expressing conditions and exceptions.

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 6.2 Subjunctive Mood](./subjunctive-mood.md)* for the past subjunctive (*were*) and *[Section 7.1 Unreal Time](./unreal-time.md)* for related hypothetical structures.

---

### A. Basic Conditional Patterns

English has four main conditional patterns, each expressing a different degree of reality or possibility.

#### 1. Zero Conditional: `if + present simple, present simple`

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Facts, general truths, rules (always true) |
| **Meaning** | 100% certainty - when the condition happens, the result always happens |

**Examples:**
- *If you heat water, it boils.*
- *If people eat too much, they get sick.*
- *If it rains, the ground gets wet.*
- *Ice melts if you heat it.*

> **Note:** The order of clauses can be reversed without changing meaning: *Water boils if you heat it.*

---

#### 2. First Conditional: `if + present simple, will + base verb`

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Real/possible future situations |
| **Meaning** | Likely or probable - if the condition is met, the result is likely |

**Examples:**
- *If it rains, we will stay home.*
- *If you study hard, you will pass the exam.*
- *If she calls, I will tell her the news.*
- *I will be late if I miss the bus.*

**Variations (other modal verbs in the result clause):**

| Modal | Example | Meaning |
|-------|---------|---------|
| *can* | *If you finish early, you **can** leave.* | permission/possibility |
| *may* | *If it's sunny, we **may** go to the beach.* | possibility |
| *might* | *If he comes, he **might** bring food.* | weaker possibility |
| *should* | *If you see her, you **should** say hello.* | advice |
| *must* | *If you feel sick, you **must** see a doctor.* | obligation |
| *imperative* | *If you see John, **tell** him to call me.* | command/request |

---

#### 3. Second Conditional: `if + past simple, would + base verb`

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Unreal or imaginary present/future situations |
| **Meaning** | Unlikely or impossible - the condition is not true now |

**Examples:**
- *If I had more money, I would travel more.* (I don't have more money)
- *If she were taller, she would play basketball.* (She is not taller)
- *If I were you, I would apologize.* (I am not you)
- *If we lived in Paris, we would speak French.* (We don't live in Paris)

> **Important:** With *I/he/she/it*, use **were** (not *was*) in formal or careful English. In informal English, *was* is sometimes used, but *were* is preferred in writing.

**Variations (other modal verbs in the result clause):**

| Modal | Example | Meaning |
|-------|---------|---------|
| *could* | *If I had more time, I **could** learn guitar.* | ability/possibility |
| *might* | *If she tried harder, she **might** succeed.* | weaker possibility |
| *should* | *If he came, we **should** welcome him.* | advice/obligation |

---

#### 4. Third Conditional: `if + past perfect, would + have + past participle`

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Use** | Unreal past / regret about the past |
| **Meaning** | Impossible to change - the condition did not happen, so the result did not happen |

**Examples:**
- *If I had known, I would have called you.* (I didn't know, so I didn't call)
- *If they had left earlier, they would have caught the train.* (They didn't leave earlier, so they missed the train)
- *You would have been happier if you had stayed home.*
- *If she had studied, she would have passed the exam.* (She didn't study, so she failed)

> **Note:** Both orders are grammatically correct. The *if*-clause can come first or second.

**Variations (other modal verbs in the result clause):**

| Modal | Example | Meaning |
|-------|---------|---------|
| *could* | *If I had saved more, I **could have bought** a car.* | ability/possibility |
| *might* | *If it hadn't rained, we **might have gone** out.* | weaker possibility |
| *should* | *If you had seen her, you **should have said** hello.* | advice/regret |

---

### B. Wish-Clauses

We use *wish* to express regrets, unreal situations, or things we want to change.

#### 1. `wish + past simple` - Unreal or Imaginary Present

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Meaning** | I am unhappy about the present; I want something to be different now |
| **Similar to** | Second conditional |

**Examples:**
- *I wish I had more time.* (I don't have more time)
- *I wish I were taller.* (I'm not taller)
- *I wish you didn't smoke.* (You smoke)
- *I wish she lived closer.* (She doesn't live closer)
- *We wish it weren't raining.* (It is raining)

> **Note:** Use *were* instead of *was* for all persons in formal English: *I wish I **were** rich.*

---

#### 2. `wish + past perfect` - Regrets About the Past

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Meaning** | I regret something in the past |
| **Similar to** | Third conditional |

**Examples:**
- *I wish I had studied harder.* (I didn't study harder)
- *I wish we had left earlier.* (We didn't leave earlier)
- *I wish she had told me.* (She didn't tell me)
- *He wishes he hadn't said that.* (He said it)
- *They wish they had bought the house.* (They didn't buy it)

---

#### 3. `wish + would + base verb` - Annoyances About Others' Behavior / Desire for Future Change

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Meaning** | I want something or someone to change in the future |
| **Important** | Use *wish + would* only for other people, not for ourselves |

**Examples:**
- *I wish you would stop shouting.*
- *I wish they would arrive on time.*
- *I wish it would stop raining.*
- *I wish my neighbor would turn down the music.*
- *She wishes he would call more often.*

| ❌ Incorrect | ✓ Correct |
|--------------|-----------|
| *I wish I would study more.* | *I wish I studied more.* (use *wish + past simple*) |
| *I wish I would be richer.* | *I wish I were richer.* |

---

#### 4. Summary: Wish Structures

| Time Focus | Structure | Example |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| **Present** (unreal) | *wish + past simple* | *I wish I had a car.* |
| **Present** (with *were*) | *wish + were* | *I wish I were taller.* |
| **Past** (regret) | *wish + past perfect* | *I wish I had studied.* |
| **Future** (annoyance/desire for change) | *wish + would* (others only) | *I wish you would listen.* |

---

### C. Mixed Conditionals

Mixed conditionals are used when the two clauses belong to different time frames - one past and one present (or future).

#### 1. Past Cause -> Present Result

> **Structure:** `if + past perfect, would + base verb`

This pattern describes how a past event affects the present situation.

| Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| *If I had studied harder, I would be a doctor now.* | Past: I didn't study. Present: I am not a doctor. |
| *If she had taken the job, she would live in London now.* | Past: She didn't take the job. Present: She doesn't live in London. |
| *If they hadn't missed the flight, they would be here already.* | Past: They missed the flight. Present: They are not here. |

#### 2. Present Condition -> Past Result

> **Structure:** `if + past simple, would have + past participle`

This pattern describes how a present state or habit would have changed a past outcome.

| Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| *If I were smarter, I would have passed the exam.* | Present: I am not smarter. Past: I didn't pass. |
| *If she wasn't afraid of flying, she would have visited us.* | Present: She is afraid of flying. Past: She didn't visit. |
| *If he weren't so lazy, he would have finished the project.* | Present: He is lazy. Past: He didn't finish. |

### D. Conditional Alternatives and Formal Variants

#### 1. *As Long As* / *So Long As* / *Provided (That)* / *Providing (That)*

These phrases mean "on the condition that" or "only if."

| Phrase | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *as long as* | *You can stay here **as long as** you pay the rent.* |
| *so long as* | *I'll help you **so long as** you promise to study.* |
| *provided (that)* | *You may leave early **provided that** you finish the report.* |
| *providing (that)* | *We will go **providing** the weather is good.* |
| *on condition that* | *She'll join the team **on condition that** she receives training first.* |

> **Note:** These are more formal than *if* and are often used in contracts, rules, and official statements.

---

#### 2. *If It Wasn't / Weren't For* and *But For*

These phrases express that something prevents a negative situation ("without this").

| Structure | Time Reference | Example |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| *If it weren't for + noun* | Present/Future | *If it weren't for your help, I'd still be stuck.* (= Without your help) |
| *If it wasn't for + noun* | Present (informal) | *If it wasn't for the rain, we'd go out.* |
| *But for + noun* | Past or Present | *But for your advice, I would have failed.* (= Without your advice) |
| *If it hadn't been for + noun* | Past | *If it hadn't been for her support, I couldn't have survived.* |

**Examples:**
- *If it weren't for his money, she wouldn't be with him.*
- *But for the traffic, we would have arrived on time.*
- *If it hadn't been for the seatbelt, he would have died.*

---

#### 3. *In Case* and *In Case Of*

These express precaution or preparation for a possible event.

| Structure | Use | Example |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| *in case + present simple* | Preparation for a possible future event | *Take an umbrella **in case it rains**.* |
| *in case + past simple* | Past precaution | *I packed extra food **in case we got hungry**.* |
| *in case of + noun* | Formal instructions/warnings | ***In case of emergency**, call 911.* |

> **Important distinction:** *In case* ≠ *if*. *In case* is about preparation; *if* is about condition.
> - *I'll buy pizza **if** you're hungry.* (I will buy pizza only when you are hungry)
> - *I'll buy pizza **in case** you're hungry.* (I will buy pizza as a precaution, whether you are hungry or not)

---

### E. Hypothetical and Stylistic Variants

#### 1. *If (Someone) Happen To* (Polite/Uncertain)

Expresses a possibility that may occur accidentally or unexpectedly.

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *if + subject + happen to + V1* | *If you **happen to see** Mark, tell him I'm looking for him.* |
| *if + subject + should + V1* (similar) | *If you **should see** Mark, tell him I'm looking for him.* |

**Examples:**
- *If I happen to forget, please remind me.*
- *If you should change your mind, let me know.*

---

#### 2. *If (Someone) Were To* (Formal Hypothetical)

A more formal or hypothetical alternative to the second conditional; emphasizes something unlikely or imagined.

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *if + subject + were to + V1* | *If he **were to resign**, we'd need a new manager.* |
| *if + subject + were to + V1* (past) | *If I **were to win** the lottery, I'd travel the world.* |

**Examples:**
- *If she were to arrive late, we would start without her.*
- *If they were to ask, I would say yes.*

---

### F. Negative and Exception Conditions

#### 1. *Unless* (= If Not)

Used for negative conditions.

| Structure | Example | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| *unless + clause* | *I won't go **unless you come** with me.* | If you don't come, I won't go |
| *unless + clause* | *You won't pass **unless you study**.* | If you don't study, you won't pass |

> **Important:** Do not use another negative word with *unless* (no double negatives).
> - ❌ *I won't go unless you don't come.*
> - ✓ *I won't go unless you come.*

---

#### 2. *If So* and *If Not*

Used to refer back to a situation already mentioned.

| Phrase | Use | Example |
|--------|-----|---------|
| *if so* | Refers to an affirmative situation | *Are you tired? **If so**, take a break.* |
| *if not* | Refers to a negative situation | *Are you coming? **If not**, please let me know.* |

**Examples:**
- *Did she call? If so, I'll call her back.*
- *Will you be at the meeting? If not, please send your report in advance.*

---

#### 3. *Otherwise*

Shows the result if a condition is not met ("if not").

| Structure | Example | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| *otherwise + clause* | *Work hard, **otherwise** you'll fail.* | If you don't work hard, you'll fail |
| *otherwise + clause* | *Hurry up, **otherwise** we'll be late.* | If we don't hurry, we'll be late |

---

### G. Other Conditional Patterns

#### 1. *Even If*

Emphasizes that the result will happen regardless of the condition.

| Structure | Example | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| *even if + clause, main clause* | *Even if it rains, I will go.* | Rain will not stop me |
| *even if + clause, main clause* | *She won't apologize, even if she is wrong.* | Her being wrong won't change her behavior |

> **Compare:** *If it rains, I will cancel the picnic.* (Condition matters) vs. *Even if it rains, I will go.* (Condition does not matter)

---

#### 2. *Only If*

Emphasizes that the condition is the only way the result will happen.

| Structure | Example | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| *only if + clause* | *I will go **only if** you come with me.* | I won't go unless you come |
| *only if + clause* (inversion for emphasis) | *Only if you apologize will I forgive you.* | Emphasis on the condition |

---

#### 3. *Supposing / Suppose / What If*

Used to introduce hypothetical scenarios, often in spoken English.

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *Suppose + clause* | *Suppose you won the lottery. What would you do?* |
| *Supposing + clause* | *Supposing the train is late, what will we do?* |
| *What if + clause* | *What if she doesn't come?* |

---

#### 4. *Imagine* (Hypothetical)

Used to ask someone to consider a hypothetical situation.

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *Imagine + clause* | *Imagine you had one million dollars. How would you spend it?* |
| *Imagine if + clause* | *Imagine if dinosaurs still existed.* |

---

### H. Inversion in Conditionals (Formal)

In formal English, we can omit *if* and invert the subject and auxiliary verb.

| Conditional | Normal Form | Inverted Form (Formal) |
|-------------|-------------|------------------------|
| **First** (with *should*) | *If you should need help, call me.* | *Should you need help, call me.* |
| **Second** (with *were*) | *If I were you, I would go.* | *Were I you, I would go.* |
| **Second** (with *were to*) | *If he were to arrive late, we would start without him.* | *Were he to arrive late, we would start without him.* |
| **Third** (with *had*) | *If I had known, I would have called.* | *Had I known, I would have called.* |
| **Third** (negative) | *If she hadn't helped, I would have failed.* | *Had she not helped, I would have failed.* |

**Examples:**
- *Had I seen her, I would have said hello.*
- *Were they to change their minds, we would be happy.*
- *Should you require further assistance, please contact us.*

> **Note:** This inversion is formal and more common in writing than in speech.

### I. Common Errors and Corrections

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| *If I will see her, I will tell her.* | *If I **see** her, I will tell her.* | No *will* in *if*-clause (first conditional) |
| *If I would have known, I would have called.* | *If I **had known**, I would have called.* | Use past perfect in *if*-clause (third conditional) |
| *If she was here, she would help.* (informal) | *If she **were** here, she would help.* (formal) | Use *were* for all persons in formal second conditional |
| *I wish I would be taller.* | *I wish I **were** taller.* | *Wish* + past simple for present unreal |
| *I wish I would have studied.* | *I wish I **had studied**.* | *Wish* + past perfect for past regret |
| *Unless you don't hurry, you'll be late.* | *Unless you **hurry**, you'll be late.* | *Unless* already means "if not" - no double negative |
| *I'll take my umbrella if it rains.* (not precaution) | *I'll take my umbrella **in case** it rains.* | *In case* = precaution; *if* = condition |
