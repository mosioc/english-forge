## 7. If-Clauses, Wish-Clauses; Conditionals

This section covers the full range of conditional structures in English, from real situations to hypothetical scenarios, as well as expressions of wish, regret, and desire for change. It also includes advanced conditional variants, formal alternatives, and useful phrases for expressing conditions and exceptions.

### A. Basic Conditional Patterns

English has four main conditional patterns, each expressing a different degree of reality or possibility.

**1. Zero Conditional: `if + present simple, present simple`**  
*Use:* Facts, general truths, rules (always true)  
- *If you heat water, it boils.*  
- *If people eat too much, they get sick.*

**2. First Conditional: `if + present simple, will + base verb`**  
*Use:* Real/possible future situations  
- *If it rains, we will stay home.*  
- *If you study hard, you will pass the exam.*

**3. Second Conditional: `if + past simple, would + base verb`**  
*Use:* Unreal or imaginary present situations  
*Note:* With *I/he/she/it*, use **were** (not *was*) in formal or careful English.  
- *If I had more money, I would travel more.*  
- *If she were taller, she would play basketball.*  
- *If I were you, I would apologize.*

**4. Third Conditional: `if + past perfect, would + have + past participle`**  
*Use:* Unreal past / regret about the past  
- *If I had known, I would have called you.*  
- *If they had left earlier, they would have caught the train.*  
- *You would have been happier if you had stayed home.* (Or: *If you had stayed home, you would have been happier.*)  
→ Both orders are grammatically correct.

### B. Wish-Clauses

We use *wish* to express regrets, unreal situations, or things we want to change.

**1. `wish + past simple`** - Unreal or imaginary present  
*Meaning:* I am unhappy about the present; I want something to be different now. (Similar to the second conditional.)  
- *I wish I had more time.* (I don’t have more time.)  
- *I wish I were taller.* (I’m not taller.)  
- *I wish you didn’t smoke.* (You smoke.)

**2. `wish + past perfect`** - Regrets about the past  
*Meaning:* I regret something in the past. (Same meaning as the third conditional.)  
- *I wish I had studied harder.* (I didn’t study harder.)  
- *I wish we had left earlier.* (We didn’t leave earlier.)  
- *I wish she had told me.* (She didn’t tell me.)

**3. `wish + would + base verb`** - Annoyances about others’ behavior / desire for future change  
*Meaning:* I want something or someone to change in the future.  
*Important:* Use *wish + would* only for other people, not for ourselves.  
- *I wish you would stop shouting.*  
- *I wish they would arrive on time.*  
- *I wish it would stop raining.*  
- Incorrect: *I wish I would study more.* ✘  
- Correct: *I wish I studied more.* (use *wish + past simple*)

### C. Mixed Conditionals

Used when the two clauses belong to different time frames - one past and one present (or future).

- **Past cause → present result:** `if + past perfect, would + base verb`  
  - *If I had studied harder, I would be a doctor now.* (Past situation affects current consequence.)

- **Present condition → past result:** `if + past simple, would + have + past participle`  
  - *If I were smarter, I would have passed the exam.* (Present condition influences a past result.)

### D. Conditional Alternatives and Formal Variants

**1. As Long As**  
*Meaning:* “On the condition that” or “only if.” Expresses that something must continue or be true for another thing to happen.  
- *You can stay here as long as you pay the rent.*  
- *I’ll help you as long as you promise to study.*

**2. Provided (That) / On Condition (That)**  
*Use:* Formal ways to say “if” or “only if,” common in rules, instructions, and contracts.  
- *You may leave early provided (that) you finish the report.*  
- *She’ll join the team on condition that she receives training first.*

**3. If It Wasn’t / Weren’t For**  
*Meaning:* Expresses that something prevents a negative situation (“without this”).  
- *If it weren’t for your help, I’d still be stuck.*  
- *If it wasn’t for the rain, we’d have gone out.*

**4. But For / If It Hadn’t Been For**  
*Use:* More formal version of “If it weren’t for,” often used for past conditions.  
- *But for your advice, I would have failed.*  
- *If it hadn’t been for her support, I couldn’t have survived.*

### E. Hypothetical Variants

**1. If (Someone) Happen To**  
*Use:* Expresses a possibility that may occur accidentally or unexpectedly; polite or indirect situations.  
- *If you happen to see Mark, tell him I’m looking for him.*  
- *If I happen to forget, please remind me.*

**2. If (Someone) Were To**  
*Use:* A more formal or hypothetical alternative to the second conditional; emphasizes something unlikely or imagined.  
- *If he were to resign, we’d need a new manager.*  
- *If I were to win the lottery, I’d travel the world.*

### F. Negative and Exception Conditions

**1. Unless** - “If not”  
*Use:* For negative conditions.  
- *I won’t go unless you come with me.* (= If you don’t come, I won’t go.)  
- *You won’t pass unless you study.*

**2. If So**  
*Use:* Refers back to a situation already mentioned; conditional confirmation based on a previous sentence.  
- *Are you tired? If so, take a break.*  
- *Did she call? If so, I’ll call her back.*

**3. Otherwise** - “If not”  
*Use:* Shows consequences; the result if a condition is not met.  
- *Work hard, otherwise you’ll fail.* (= If you don’t work hard, you’ll fail.)

**4. In Case / In Case Of**  
*Use:* Precaution or preparation for a possible event.  
- *Take an umbrella in case it rains.*  
- *In case of emergency, call 911.* (Prepositional form used for instructions/signs.)
