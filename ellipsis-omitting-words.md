## 14.1 Ellipsis (Omitting Words)

Ellipsis (from Greek *elleipsis*, meaning "omission") is the removal of words from a sentence because they are understood from the context. Using ellipsis makes speech and writing more concise and natural, avoiding unnecessary repetition.

> **Note:** Do not confuse **ellipsis** (omission of words) with **ellipsis marks** (...), which indicate omitted text or a pause. This section focuses on grammatical ellipsis.

---

### A. What Is Ellipsis?

Ellipsis allows us to leave out words that are clearly understood without changing the meaning of the sentence.

| Without Ellipsis (Full Form) | With Ellipsis (Natural) |
|------------------------------|-------------------------|
| *She speaks French, and he speaks French too.* | *She speaks French, and he **does** too.* |
| *I wanted to leave, but I couldn't leave.* | *I wanted to leave, but I couldn't **Ø**.* |
| *John ate an apple, and Mary ate an apple too.* | *John ate an apple, and Mary **did** too.* |

> **Symbol:** Linguists often use **Ø** (zero symbol) to mark an omitted word.

---

### B. Types of Ellipsis

---

#### 1. Verb Phrase Ellipsis (VPE)

The most common type. We omit a verb or verb phrase after an auxiliary or modal verb.

> **Structure:** *Auxiliary/Modal + Ø (verb phrase understood)*

| Full Form | Ellipsis |
|-----------|----------|
| *She can swim, and I can swim too.* | *She can swim, and I **can** too.* |
| *He hasn't finished, but she has finished.* | *He hasn't finished, but she **has**.* |
| *I don't like coffee, but she does like coffee.* | *I don't like coffee, but she **does**.* |
| *Are you coming? I am coming.* | *Are you coming? I **am**.* |
| *He said he would call, but he didn't call.* | *He said he would call, but he **didn't**.* |

**With multiple auxiliaries:**

| Full Form | Ellipsis |
|-----------|----------|
| *She should have been working, but she wasn't working.* | *She should have been working, but she **wasn't**.* |
| *He could have told me, but he didn't tell me.* | *He could have told me, but he **didn't**.* |

---

#### 2. To-Infinitive Ellipsis

We can omit the verb after *to* when the meaning is clear. We keep *to* but drop the rest.

> **Structure:** *to + Ø (verb phrase understood)*

| Full Form | Ellipsis |
|-----------|----------|
| *I wanted to go, but I forgot to go.* | *I wanted to go, but I forgot **to**.* |
| *She asked him to leave, and he agreed to leave.* | *She asked him to leave, and he agreed **to**.* |
| *You can stay if you want to stay.* | *You can stay if you want **to**.* |
| *He didn't come, though he had promised to come.* | *He didn't come, though he had promised **to**.* |

> **Note:** Do not omit *to* - keep it as a marker of the omitted infinitive.

| Correct | Incorrect |
|---------|-----------|
| *I wanted to go, but I forgot **to**.* | *I wanted to go, but I forgot **Ø**.* ✘ |

---

#### 3. Noun Phrase Ellipsis

We omit a noun after an adjective, determiner, or possessive when the noun is understood.

> **Structure:** *Determiner/Adjective/Possessive + Ø (noun understood)*

| Full Form | Ellipsis |
|-----------|----------|
| *I like the red car, not the blue car.* | *I like the red car, not the blue **Ø**.* |
| *She has two dogs: a big dog and a small dog.* | *She has two dogs: a big **Ø** and a small **Ø**.* |
| *Is this your book or my book?* | *Is this your book or **mine**?* (pronoun form) |
| *These apples are fresh, but those apples are not.* | *These apples are fresh, but those **Ø** are not.* |

**With numbers and quantifiers:**

| Full Form | Ellipsis |
|-----------|----------|
| *I need three tickets. Do you need three tickets too?* | *I need three tickets. Do you need **three** too?* |
| *She bought some apples, and I bought some apples too.* | *She bought some apples, and I bought **some** too.* |

---

#### 4. Clausal Ellipsis (Answer Ellipsis)

In responses, we omit everything except the essential new information. This is extremely common in conversation.

| Question | Full Answer | Ellipsis Answer |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| *Where are you going?* | *I am going to the store.* | *To the store.* |
| *Who called?* | *John called.* | *John.* |
| *Are you tired?* | *Yes, I am tired.* | *Yes.* |
| *What did you eat?* | *I ate a sandwich.* | *A sandwich.* |
| *Can you help me?* | *Yes, I can help you.* | *Sure.* |

---

#### 5. Comparative Ellipsis

In comparative sentences, we omit repeated elements after *than* or *as...as*.

| Full Form | Ellipsis |
|-----------|----------|
| *She is taller than I am tall.* | *She is taller than I **am**.* |
| *He runs faster than I run.* | *He runs faster than **I**.* |
| *You have more money than I have money.* | *You have more money than **I**.* |
| *This book is as interesting as that book is interesting.* | *This book is as interesting as **that one**.* |

---

#### 6. Coordination Ellipsis (Gapping)

When two clauses are joined by *and, but, or*, we can omit repeated words from the second clause. This is called **gapping**.

> **Structure:** *Clause 1 + conjunction + Clause 2 (with repeated verb omitted)*

| Full Form | Ellipsis (Gapping) |
|-----------|--------------------|
| *John eats rice, and Mary eats beans.* | *John eats rice, and Mary **Ø** beans.* |
| *She wanted to leave, but I wanted to stay.* | *She wanted to leave, but I **Ø** to stay.* |
| *He can speak French, and she can speak German.* | *He can speak French, and she **Ø** German.* |
| *Some went north, and others went south.* | *Some went north, and others **Ø** south.* |

---

#### 7. Stripping (Bare Ellipsis)

A more extreme form where only one word or phrase remains, often with *too, either, also, as well*.

| Full Form | Ellipsis (Stripping) |
|-----------|----------------------|
| *John likes pizza, and Mary likes pizza too.* | *John likes pizza, and **Mary too**.* |
| *She didn't see him, and I didn't see him either.* | *She didn't see him, and **me neither**.* |
| *He went home, and she went home also.* | *He went home, and **she also**.* |

---

#### 8. Sluicing

We omit everything after a *wh-* word in a question when the meaning is clear.

> **Structure:** *Wh-word + Ø (rest of clause understood)*

| Full Form | Ellipsis (Sluicing) |
|-----------|---------------------|
| *Someone called, but I don't know who called.* | *Someone called, but I don't know **who**.* |
| *He went somewhere, but I don't know where he went.* | *He went somewhere, but I don't know **where**.* |
| *She said something, but I can't remember what she said.* | *She said something, but I can't remember **what**.* |
| *There's a problem, but I'm not sure how to fix it.* | *There's a problem, but I'm not sure **how**.* |

---

### C. Ellipsis in Different Contexts

#### 1. In Lists

| With Ellipsis | Full Form |
|---------------|-----------|
| *I like coffee, tea, and juice.* | *I like coffee, I like tea, and I like juice.* |
| *He came, saw, and conquered.* | *He came, he saw, and he conquered.* |

#### 2. In Instructions and Headlines

Ellipsis is very common in instructions, headlines, and notes where brevity is important.

| Context | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Instructions** | *Insert coin, then press button.* (Omit *you should*) |
| **Headlines** | *Man bites dog.* (Omit *a*) |
| **Notes** | *Meeting at 3 PM.* (Omit *The* and *is*) |
| **Signs** | *No smoking.* (Omit *There is*) |

#### 3. In Informal Speech

Spoken English uses ellipsis frequently, especially at the beginning of sentences.

| Informal Ellipsis | Full Form |
|-------------------|-----------|
| *Coming?* | *Are you coming?* |
| *Don't know.* | *I don't know.* |
| *See you later.* | *I will see you later.* |
| *Tired?* | *Are you tired?* |
| *Good idea.* | *That is a good idea.* |

---

### D. Ellipsis Marks (...) vs. Grammatical Ellipsis

It is important to distinguish between these two concepts.

| Concept | Meaning | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Grammatical ellipsis** | Omitting understood words | *She can swim, and I can too.* |
| **Ellipsis marks (...)** | Three dots indicating omitted text or a pause | *"He said... something about leaving."* |

> **Ellipsis marks uses:**
> - Omitted words in a quotation: *"The report stated that... the results were inconclusive."*
> - A pause or hesitation: *"I'm not sure... maybe we should wait."*
> - A trailing off of thought: *"I was thinking that maybe..."*

---

### E. When NOT to Use Ellipsis

Ellipsis can cause ambiguity if the omitted words are not clear from context.

| Unclear (Avoid) | Clear (Better) |
|-----------------|----------------|
| *He loves her more than me.* (Ambiguous: more than he loves me? or more than I love her?) | *He loves her more than he loves me.* / *He loves her more than I do.* |
| *She knows you better than me.* | *She knows you better than she knows me.* / *She knows you better than I do.* |

> **Tip:** When ellipsis creates ambiguity, use the full form instead.

| Type of Ellipsis | Structure | Example |
|------------------|-----------|---------|
| **Verb phrase ellipsis** | *Auxiliary + Ø* | *She can swim, and I **can** too.* |
| **To-infinitive ellipsis** | *to + Ø* | *I wanted to go, but I forgot **to**.* |
| **Noun phrase ellipsis** | *Det/Adj + Ø* | *I like the red car, not the blue **Ø**.* |

| **Sluicing** | *Wh-word + Ø* | *Someone called, but I don't know **who**.* |

---

### G. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| *I wanted to go, but I forgot.* (with *to* meaning omitted) | *I wanted to go, but I forgot **to**.* ✓ |
| *She is taller than me.* (informal, but ambiguous in formal writing) | *She is taller than **I**.* (formal) or *...than **I am**.* |
| *He likes her more than me.* (ambiguous) | Clarify: *...more than **he likes me**.* or *...more than **I do**.* |
| *Can you come? - I can.* (missing *come*) | *Can you come? - I **can**.* ✓ (correct with ellipsis) |
| Using ellipsis when context is unclear | Use full form instead |
