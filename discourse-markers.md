## 20.1 Discourse Markers

Discourse markers are words or phrases that help organize speech and writing, connect ideas, and guide the listener or reader through the flow of communication. They do not change the meaning of a sentence but signal the speaker's attitude, indicate transitions, or manage the conversation.

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 20. Conjunctions](./conjunctions.md)* for connecting clauses and *[Section 6. Modal Verbs](./modal-verbs.md)* for expressing certainty and opinion.

---

### A. What Are Discourse Markers?

Discourse markers (also called linking words or transition signals) are like **signposts** in language. They tell the listener or reader:
- Where the conversation is going
- How ideas relate to each other
- What the speaker thinks about the information

| Without Discourse Markers | With Discourse Markers |
|---------------------------|------------------------|
| *He is late. We should start.* | ***Well**, he is late. **Anyway**, we should start.* |
| *I like coffee. I drink it every day.* | ***To be honest**, I like coffee. **In fact**, I drink it every day.* |

> **Key Features:**
> - They are often optional (the sentence still makes sense without them)
> - They are usually separated by commas
> - They are more common in speech than formal writing
> - They do not change the grammatical structure of the sentence

---

### B. Types of Discourse Markers

---

#### 1. Adding Information

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *and* | *She likes reading, **and** she enjoys writing.* |
| *also* | *He is intelligent. **Also**, he is very kind.* |
| *furthermore* (formal) | *The proposal is expensive. **Furthermore**, it is impractical.* |
| *moreover* (formal) | *The product is popular. **Moreover**, it is profitable.* |
| *in addition* | *In addition to his job, he volunteers at a shelter.* |
| *besides* | *I don't want to go. **Besides**, I'm too tired.* |
| *as well as* | *She sings **as well as** plays the piano.* |

---

#### 2. Contrasting Ideas

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *but* | *I wanted to go, **but** I was too busy.* |
| *however* | *The weather was bad. **However**, we still enjoyed the trip.* |
| *nevertheless* | *The task was difficult. **Nevertheless**, she succeeded.* |
| *nonetheless* | *He was tired. **Nonetheless**, he kept working.* |
| *although / though* | *Although it rained, we went for a walk.* |
| *even though* | *Even though he studied hard, he failed.* |
| *whereas* | *Some people love coffee, **whereas** others prefer tea.* |
| *while* | *While I understand your point, I disagree.* |
| *on the other hand* | *This car is fast. **On the other hand**, it is very expensive.* |
| *in contrast* | *In contrast to his brother, he is very shy.* |
| *conversely* | *Eating healthy is good for you. **Conversely**, junk food is harmful.* |

---

#### 3. Showing Cause and Effect / Result

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *so* | *It was raining, **so** we stayed home.* |
| *therefore* | *He didn't study. **Therefore**, he failed the exam.* |
| *consequently* | *She missed the bus. **Consequently**, she was late.* |
| *thus* | *The evidence was clear; **thus**, the jury found him guilty.* |
| *hence* | *The company lost money; **hence**, it had to close.* |
| *as a result* | *He trained every day. **As a result**, he won the competition.* |
| *because of this* | *The roads were icy. **Because of this**, schools were closed.* |

---

#### 4. Ordering and Sequencing

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *first / firstly / first of all* | *First, preheat the oven.* |
| *second / secondly* | *Second, mix the ingredients.* |
| *third / thirdly* | *Third, bake for 20 minutes.* |
| *next* | *Next, add the sugar.* |
| *then* | *Then, stir until smooth.* |
| *after that* | *After that, let it cool.* |
| *finally / lastly* | *Finally, serve warm.* |
| *to begin with* | *To begin with, let me introduce myself.* |
| *in the first place* | *Why did you agree in the first place?* |

---

#### 5. Giving Opinions

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *in my opinion* | *In my opinion, this is the best option.* |
| *I think* | *I think we should wait.* |
| *I believe* | *I believe he is telling the truth.* |
| *it seems to me* | *It seems to me that you are right.* |
| *from my perspective* | *From my perspective, the plan is flawed.* |
| *personally* | *Personally, I prefer tea to coffee.* |
| *to be honest* | *To be honest, I don't like it.* |
| *if you ask me* (informal) | *If you ask me, he's making a mistake.* |
| *as far as I'm concerned* | *As far as I'm concerned, you can go.* |

---

#### 6. Expressing Certainty or Doubt

| Certainty | Doubt / Possibility |
|-----------|---------------------|
| *certainly* - *She is certainly the best.* | *perhaps* - *Perhaps we should leave.* |
| *definitely* - *I will definitely be there.* | *maybe* - *Maybe it will rain.* |
| *undoubtedly* - *He is undoubtedly guilty.* | *probably* - *He is probably late.* |
| *of course* - *Of course I'll help.* | *possibly* - *Possibly, we can try again.* |
| *without a doubt* - *Without a doubt, she is talented.* | *it depends* - *It depends on the weather.* |

---

#### 7. Clarifying and Explaining

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *in other words* | *He is frugal. **In other words**, he doesn't like to spend money.* |
| *that is* | *He will arrive on Friday, **that is**, if the flight is on time.* |
| *namely* | *Three people were chosen, **namely** John, Sarah, and Tom.* |
| *i.e.* (Latin: *id est*) | *He enjoys outdoor activities, **i.e.**, hiking and camping.* |
| *e.g.* (Latin: *exempli gratia*) | *He likes citrus fruits, **e.g.**, oranges and lemons.* |
| *to put it differently* | *To put it differently, we need more time.* |
| *to clarify* | *To clarify, I was not blaming you.* |

---

#### 8. Giving Examples

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *for example* | *Many animals are endangered. **For example**, the giant panda.* |
| *for instance* | *He has many hobbies. **For instance**, he plays guitar.* |
| *such as* | *He enjoys sports **such as** soccer and basketball.* |
| *including* | *The price includes breakfast, **including** coffee and juice.* |
| *like* (informal) | *He loves fruits **like** apples and bananas.* |

---

#### 9. Summarizing and Concluding

| Marker | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *in conclusion* | *In conclusion, we must act now.* |
| *to sum up* | *To sum up, the evidence is clear.* |
| *in summary* | *In summary, the project was a success.* |
| *overall* | *Overall, it was a good experience.* |
| *in short* | *In short, we need more money.* |
| *in a nutshell* (informal) | *In a nutshell, he quit.* |
| *finally* | *Finally, I'd like to thank everyone.* |
| *ultimately* | *Ultimately, the choice is yours.* |

---

#### 10. Managing Conversation (Spoken Discourse Markers)

These are very common in spoken English to manage the flow of conversation.

| Marker | Use | Example |
|--------|-----|---------|
| *well* | Hesitation, thinking, or introducing a response | *"What do you think?" - "**Well**, I'm not sure."* |
| *so* | Drawing a conclusion or changing topic | *"**So**, what do we do now?"* |
| *okay / alright* | Agreement, transition, or checking understanding | *"**Okay**, let's start."* |
| *right* | Acknowledgment or agreement | *"**Right**, I see what you mean."* |
| *now* | Introducing a new topic or step | *"**Now**, let's move on to the next point."* |
| *actually* | Correcting or introducing new information | *"I **actually** prefer tea to coffee."* |
| *anyway* | Returning to the main topic or ending a topic | *"**Anyway**, that's not important."* |
| *basically* | Summarizing or simplifying | *"**Basically**, we need more time."* |
| *honestly* | Emphasizing sincerity | *"**Honestly**, I don't care."* |
| *frankly* | Being direct or blunt | *"**Frankly**, that's a terrible idea."* |
| *you know* | Filler, assuming shared knowledge | *"It's, **you know**, complicated."* |
| *I mean* | Clarifying or rephrasing | *"He's nice. **I mean**, he's not perfect, but nice."* |
| *look* / *listen* | Getting attention or expressing frustration | *"**Look**, I've told you before."* |

---

### C. Punctuation with Discourse Markers

| Position | Punctuation Rule | Example |
|----------|------------------|---------|
| **At beginning of sentence** | Usually followed by a comma | ***However**, we decided to go.* |
| **In the middle of a sentence** | Set off by commas on both sides | *This plan, **however**, is too expensive.* |
| **At end of sentence** | Preceded by a comma | *He is late, **obviously**.* |

> **Exception:** Short markers like *so, then, well, now, anyway* may not need a comma in informal writing.

---

### D. Formal vs. Informal Discourse Markers

| Formal (Writing) | Informal (Speech) |
|------------------|-------------------|
| *furthermore, moreover* | *and, also, plus* |
| *nevertheless, nonetheless* | *but, though, still* |
| *consequently, therefore, thus* | *so, that's why* |
| *in conclusion, to summarize* | *anyway, so, basically* |
| *however* | *but, though* |
| *in my opinion* | *I think, honestly* |

**Examples:**
- **Formal:** *The evidence is insufficient. **Furthermore**, the methodology is flawed.*
- **Informal:** *The evidence isn't enough. **Plus**, the method is bad.*

---

### E. Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|------------|
| *I like coffee. **But** I don't like tea.* (Starting sentence with *but* in formal writing) | *I like coffee, **but** I don't like tea.* (or use *however*) |
| *However I like coffee.* (missing comma after *however*) | ***However**, I like coffee.* ✓ |
| *He is late, **however** he called.* (using *however* as *but* without proper punctuation) | *He is late; **however**, he called.* / *He is late, **but** he called.* |
| Overusing *actually*, *basically*, *you know*, *like*, *I mean* in formal writing | Use sparingly or not at all |
| *Firstly... secondly... thirdly...* (can sound repetitive) | Use *first... second... third* or *firstly... secondly... finally* |

### G. Example Paragraph (Markers Highlighted)

> ***First of all**, let me explain the problem. **In my opinion**, we need more staff. **However**, our budget is limited. **For example**, we cannot afford to hire three new people right now. **Nevertheless**, we can hire at least one person. **In addition**, we could offer overtime to current employees. **To sum up**, we have options, but we need to act quickly. **So**, what do you think?**
