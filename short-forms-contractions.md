## 3. Short Forms (Contractions)

Short forms (or contractions) are used in spoken English and informal writing (e.g., a letter or message to a friend). When we write short forms, we use an apostrophe (’ or ') to replace the missing letter(s):

- *I am* → *I'm*
- *you have* → *you've*
- *did not* → *didn't*

---

### A. Affirmative Short Forms

| Contraction | Full Form | Examples |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| `'m` | am | *I'm* tired. |
| `'s` | is **or** has | *She's* ill. (= She is ill)<br>*She's* gone away. (= She has gone) |
| `'re` | are | *You're* late. / *We're* ready. |
| `'ve` | have | *I've* finished. / *They've* arrived. |
| `'ll` | will | *I'll* help. / *He'll* call. |
| `'d` | would **or** had | *I'd* see a doctor. (= I would see)<br>*I'd* never seen her. (= I had never seen) |

#### Special Cases

| Contraction | Full Form | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| *let's* | let us | *Let's* go now. (= Let us go) |

#### Ambiguity Notes

- **`'s`** can mean *is* or *has* - context determines the meaning.
- **`'d`** can mean *would* or *had* - context determines the meaning.

---

### B. Common Uses of Affirmative Contractions

Contractions are commonly used after:

**Question Words**

| Contraction | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| *who's* | who is / who has | *Who's* at the door? |
| *what's* | what is / what has | *What's* the matter? |
| *where's* | where is / where has | *Where's* my phone? |
| *when's* | when is / when has | *When's* the meeting? |
| *why's* | why is / why has | *Why's* he crying? |
| *how's* | how is / how has | *How's* your mother? |

**That, There, Here**

| Contraction | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| *that's* | that is / that has | *That's* my car. |
| *there's* | there is / there has | *There's* a solution. |
| *here's* | here is / here has | *Here's* your coffee. |

**Nouns** (when the noun is the subject performing an action or being described)

- *Katherine's* going to be late. (= Katherine is)
- *The dog's* barking loudly. (= The dog is)

---

### C. Prohibited Use: End of a Sentence

Contractions (especially `'m`, `'s`, `'re`, `'ve`, `'ll`, `'d`) should **not** be used when the verb form stands alone at the end of a sentence for emphasis or as a direct answer.

| Correct | Incorrect |
|---------|-----------|
| *Are you coming? - Yes, I am.* | *Yes, I'm.* ✘ |
| *Where is she? - I don't know where she is.* | *I don't know where she's.* ✘ |
| *Who is that? - That is John.* | *That's John.* ✘ (in direct response to "Who is that?") |

### E. Two Ways to Form Negative Contractions with *is* and *are*

For *he/she/it is not* and *you/we/they are not*, there are two common forms:

**Option 1:** Negative contraction of *is* or *are*

| Contraction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *he isn't / she isn't / it isn't* | *He isn't* coming to the party. |
| *you aren't / we aren't / they aren't* | *They aren't* aware of the changes. |

**Option 2:** Affirmative contraction + *not*

| Contraction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *he's not / she's not / it's not* | *He's not* feeling well. |
| *you're not / we're not / they're not* | *We're not* sure about the timing. |

> Both forms are equally correct and commonly used.
