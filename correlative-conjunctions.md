## 20.2 Correlative Conjunctions (Paired Conjunctions)

Correlative conjunctions are **paired conjunctions** that work together to join words, phrases, or clauses of equal grammatical rank. They always appear in pairs.

Unlike coordinating conjunctions (which use a single word), correlative conjunctions frame the connected elements with two parts.

| Pair | Function |
|------|----------|
| *both... and...* | Inclusion (adds two elements) |
| *either... or...* | Choice between two alternatives |
| *neither... nor...* | Negation of both alternatives |
| *not only... but also...* | Addition with emphasis |
| *whether... or...* | Alternatives (often in embedded questions) |
| *rather... than...* | Preference |
| *scarcely... when...* | Immediate sequence |
| *no sooner... than...* | Immediate sequence |
| *the more... the more...* | Proportional relationship |
| *as... as...* | Equality |
| *such... that...* | Result |
| *so... that...* | Result |

---

### 1. Both... And...

**Function:** Emphasizes that two things are included together. Stronger than simple *and*.

**Structure:** *both + [element A] + and + [element B]*

| Joined Elements | Example |
|-----------------|---------|
| Nouns | *Both **my mother** and **my father** are teachers.* |
| Adjectives | *She is both **intelligent** and **hardworking**.* |
| Verbs | *He both **sings** and **dances**.* |
| Prepositional Phrases | *The problem exists both **in cities** and **in rural areas**.* |
| Clauses | *Both **what he said** and **how he said it** offended me.* |

**Key Rule:** The elements after *both* and *and* must be parallel in structure.

| ✓ Correct (Parallel) | ✗ Incorrect (Not Parallel) |
|-----------------------|-----------------------------|
| *She is both smart and kind.* (adj + adj) | *She is both smart and a kind person.* (adj + noun phrase) |
| *I both like and respect him.* (verb + verb) | *I both like him and respect him.* (verb+obj + verb+obj) |

**Subject-Verb Agreement:** *Both + A + and + B* always takes a **plural** verb.
- *Both **the teacher and the student are** present.*
- *Both **he and I are** responsible.*

---

### 2. Either... Or...

**Function:** Presents a choice between two alternatives (only one is true or possible).

**Structure:** *either + [element A] + or + [element B]*

| Joined Elements | Example |
|-----------------|---------|
| Nouns | *Either **coffee** or **tea** is fine.* |
| Adjectives | *The film was either **boring** or **confusing**.* |
| Verbs | *We can either **walk** or **take** a taxi.* |
| Clauses | *Either **you apologize**, or **I'm leaving**.* |
| Prepositional Phrases | *She's either **at home** or **at work**.* |

**Key Rule:** Maintain parallel structure.

| ✓ Correct | ✗ Incorrect |
|------------|--------------|
| *We'll either drive or fly.* (verb + verb) | *We'll either drive or take a flight.* (verb + verb phrase) |
| *You can have either tea or coffee.* (noun + noun) | *You can either have tea or coffee.* (verb phrase + noun — misplaced *either*) |

**Subject-Verb Agreement:** The verb agrees with the element **closest** to it (proximity rule).

| Example | Rule |
|---------|------|
| *Either **the teacher or the students are** responsible.* | *Students* (plural) is closer -> plural verb |
| *Either **the students or the teacher is** responsible.* | *Teacher* (singular) is closer -> singular verb |
| *Either **he or I am** going.* | *I* is closer -> *am* |

**Position of *Either*:** Place *either* directly before the first element being contrasted.

| ✓ Natural | ✗ Awkward (Misplaced) |
|------------|------------------------|
| *She is either French or Belgian.* | *Either she is French or she is Belgian.* (longer but grammatically correct) |
| *I'll see either the red one or the blue one.* | *I'll either see the red one or the blue one.* (X) |

---

### 3. Neither... Nor...

**Function:** Negates both of two alternatives. The negative form of *both... and...*.

**Structure:** *neither + [element A] + nor + [element B]*

| Joined Elements | Example |
|-----------------|---------|
| Nouns | *Neither **the movie** nor **the book** was good.* |
| Adjectives | *She is neither **rich** nor **famous**.* |
| Verbs | *He neither **called** nor **texted**.* |
| Clauses | *Neither **did he apologize**, nor **did he explain**.* (Note: inversion in formal use) |
| Prepositional Phrases | *It's neither **on the table** nor **in the drawer**.* |

**Key Rules:**

#### Subject-Verb Agreement
The verb agrees with the element **closest** to it.

| Example | Verb |
|---------|------|
| *Neither **the manager nor the employees were** informed.* | Plural (*employees* is closer) |
| *Neither **the employees nor the manager was** informed.* | Singular (*manager* is closer) |
| *Neither **he nor I am** happy about this.* | Singular (*I* -> *am*) |

#### No Double Negative
*Neither... nor...* is already negative. Do not use *not* with it.

| ✓ Correct | ✗ Incorrect |
|------------|--------------|
| *I have neither the time nor the money.* | *I don't have neither the time nor the money.* |
| *She likes neither coffee nor tea.* | *She doesn't like neither coffee nor tea.* |

#### Parallel Structure
| ✓ Correct | ✗ Incorrect |
|------------|--------------|
| *He is neither intelligent nor hardworking.* | *He is neither intelligent nor a hard worker.* |
| *She neither called nor texted.* | *She neither called nor sent a text.* |

#### Formal Inversion with Clauses
When *neither* and *nor* connect clauses in formal English, the clause after *nor* uses inversion.

- *The government did not respond, **nor did they** acknowledge the problem.*
- *He doesn't smoke, **nor does he** drink.*

---

### 4. Not Only... But Also...

**Function:** Emphasizes that a second point is equally or more important than the first. Stronger than *both... and...*.

**Structure:** *not only + [element A] + but also + [element B]*

Variations: *not only... but...*, *not only... but... as well*, *not only... also...*

| Joined Elements | Example |
|-----------------|---------|
| Nouns | *She speaks **not only English** but also **French**.* |
| Adjectives | *He is **not only intelligent** but also **kind**.* |
| Verbs | *She **not only sings** but also **dances**.* |
| Prepositional Phrases | *The problem exists **not only in cities** but also **in towns**.* |
| Clauses | ***Not only did he finish** early, but he also **helped** others.* |

#### Inversion Rule (Formal/Emphatic)
When *not only* begins a clause, the subject and auxiliary verb **must invert**.

| Normal Order | Inversion with *Not Only* |
|--------------|---------------------------|
| *She not only won the race, she also broke the record.* | ***Not only did she win** the race, but she also broke the record.* |
| *They not only completed the project, they finished early.* | ***Not only did they complete** the project, but they also finished early.* |
| *He is not only talented, he is hardworking.* | ***Not only is he** talented, (but) he is also hardworking.* |

**Inversion Formula:** *Not only + auxiliary/modal + subject + main verb...*

| Original Verb | Inversion Example |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Present Simple (*she likes*) | *Not only **does she like*** |
| Past Simple (*they went*) | *Not only **did they go*** |
| *is/are/was/were* | *Not only **is he / are they / was she**...* |
| Present Perfect (*has seen*) | *Not only **has she seen*** |
| Modals (*can, will, should*) | *Not only **can he / will they / should we**...* |

**Key Rule:** Maintain parallel structure after *not only* and *but also*.

| ✓ Correct | ✗ Incorrect |
|------------|--------------|
| *She is not only smart but also funny.* | *She is not only smart but also she is funny.* |

---

### 5. Whether... Or...

**Function:** Presents two alternatives, often in embedded questions or when the outcome doesn't matter.

| Usage | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Embedded Question | *I don't know **whether he's coming or not**.* |
| No Difference | ***Whether you like it or not**, we're going.* |
| With *or not* | *She asked **whether or not** I was free.* / *She asked **whether** I was free **or not**.* |
| Implied Alternative | *I'm going **whether you come or not**.* |

**Note:** *Whether* (not *if*) is required before *or not* and after prepositions.
- *It depends on **whether** she arrives on time.*
- *I'm worried about **whether** we'll finish.*

---

### 6. Rather... Than...

**Function:** Expresses a preference for one thing over another.

**Structure:** *rather + [bare infinitive] + than + [bare infinitive]*

| Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| *I would **rather die than betray** my friends.* | Strong preference |
| *She'd **rather walk than take** the bus.* | Preference |
| *They chose **rather to resign than to compromise**.* | Formal, with *to*-infinitives |

**Parallel Structure:** Both halves must match.
| ✓ Correct | ✗ Incorrect |
|------------|--------------|
| *I'd rather read than watch TV.* | *I'd rather read than watching TV.* |

---

### 7. No Sooner... Than...

**Function:** Emphasizes that one event happened immediately after another.

**Structure:** *no sooner + [past perfect] + than + [past simple]*

| Example |
|---------|
| ***No sooner had I sat down than** the phone rang.* |
| ***No sooner had she left than** he arrived.* |
| ***No sooner had we finished eating than** the fire alarm went off.* |

**Inversion Rule:** *No sooner* at the beginning always triggers **subject-auxiliary inversion**.

| ✓ Correct (Inverted) | ✗ Incorrect |
|-----------------------|--------------|
| *No sooner had I arrived than it started.* | *No sooner I had arrived than it started.* |

**Alternative Position:** No inversion when *no sooner* is in the middle.
- *I **had no sooner** arrived than it started to rain.*

---

### 8. Scarcely... When... / Hardly... When... / Barely... When...

**Function:** Same as *no sooner... than* — emphasizes immediate sequence. More formal.

**Structure:** *scarcely/hardly/barely + [past perfect] + when + [past simple]*

| Example |
|---------|
| ***Scarcely had I sat down when** the phone rang.* |
| ***Hardly had she walked in when** everyone applauded.* |
| ***Barely had we started when** the power went out.* |

**Inversion Rule:** Same as *no sooner*. Inversion required at the beginning.

| ✓ Correct | ✗ Incorrect |
|------------|--------------|
| *Hardly had he spoken when the crowd cheered.* | *Hardly he had spoken when the crowd cheered.* |

---

### 9. The More... The More... (Proportional Comparison)

**Function:** Shows that two things increase or decrease together in proportion.

**Structure:** *the + comparative + subject + verb, the + comparative + subject + verb*

| Example |
|---------|
| ***The more I learn, the more** I realize how little I know.* |
| ***The sooner we leave, the better**.* |
| ***The harder you work, the luckier** you get.* |
| ***The less you spend, the more** you save.* |
| ***The bigger they are, the harder** they fall.* |

**Pattern Variations:**

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| *The more... the more...* | *The more you practice, the more you improve.* |
| *The more... the less...* | *The more you worry, the less you sleep.* |
| *The less... the more...* | *The less you stress, the more you enjoy.* |
| *The + -er... the + -er...* | *The faster you drive, the riskier it gets.* |
| *The more... the + -er* | *The more you study, the smarter you become.* |

**Shortened Form:** The verb *be* is often omitted in proverbs and fixed expressions.
- *The bigger, the better.* (= The bigger it is, the better it is)
- *The more, the merrier.* (= The more people there are, the merrier we'll be)

---

### 10. As... As... (Equality)

**Function:** Shows that two things are equal in some way.

**Structure:** *as + [adjective/adverb] + as + [noun/pronoun/clause]*

| Example | Type |
|---------|------|
| *She is **as tall as** her brother.* | Equality (adj) |
| *He runs **as fast as** I do.* | Equality (adv) |
| *There were **as many as** 500 people.* | Quantity |
| *It's not **as difficult as** it looks.* | Negative equality |

---

### 11. Such... That... / So... That... (Result)

**Function:** Expresses cause and result with emphasis on the degree.

#### Such... That...

**Structure:** *such + [noun phrase] + that + [result clause]*

| Example |
|---------|
| *It was **such a beautiful day that** we decided to go to the beach.* |
| *She showed **such courage that** everyone admired her.* |
| *They were **such good friends that** they told each other everything.* |

#### So... That...

**Structure:** *so + [adjective/adverb] + that + [result clause]*

| Example |
|---------|
| *I was **so tired that** I fell asleep immediately.* |
| *She spoke **so quickly that** I couldn't understand her.* |
| *There were **so many people that** we couldn't get in.* |

---

### Common Errors

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| *Both he likes coffee and tea.* | *He likes **both** coffee **and** tea.* | *Both* must be directly before the first element |
| *Either you can have cake or ice cream.* | *You can have **either** cake **or** ice cream.* | *Either* must be directly before the first option |
| *Neither she didn't call nor text.* | *She **neither** called **nor** texted.* | *Neither... nor* is already negative (no *didn't*) |
| *Not only she sings, but she also dances.* | ***Not only does she sing**, but she also dances.* | Inversion required when *not only* starts a clause |
| *Neither the students nor the teacher were happy.* | *Neither the students nor **the teacher was** happy.* | Verb agrees with closest element (*teacher*) |
| *Both my father and my mother is coming.* | *Both my father and my mother **are** coming.* | *Both... and* always takes plural verb |
| *No sooner I had left when it started.* | ***No sooner had I left than** it started.* | Inversion required; use *than*, not *when* |
| *She is as tall than me.* | *She is as tall **as** me.* | Equality uses *as... as*, never *as... than* |
| *The more I practice, I get better.* | *The more I practice, **the better** I get.* | Second clause must also begin with *the + comparative* |
