## 13.1 Negative Structures

Negative structures are used to deny, contradict, or express the opposite of a positive statement. English forms negatives primarily by adding **not** after an auxiliary verb, but there are several other ways to express negation.

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 13. Questions](./questions.md)* for question formation and *[Section 3. Short Forms](./short-forms-contractions.md)* for negative contractions.

---

### A. Basic Negative Formation

#### 1. With Auxiliary Verbs (be, have, do, modals)

The basic rule is to add **not** after the auxiliary verb.

| Tense/Auxiliary | Affirmative | Negative |
|-----------------|-------------|----------|
| *be* (present) | *She **is** ready.* | *She **is not** ready.* |
| *be* (past) | *They **were** happy.* | *They **were not** happy.* |
| *have* (present perfect) | *I **have** finished.* | *I **have not** finished.* |
| *have* (past perfect) | *He **had** eaten.* | *He **had not** eaten.* |
| *will* (future) | *We **will** come.* | *We **will not** come.* |
| *can* (ability) | *She **can** swim.* | *She **cannot** swim.* |
| *should* (advice) | *You **should** go.* | *You **should not** go.* |

#### 2. Without an Auxiliary Verb (Present and Past Simple)

When there is no auxiliary verb, use **do not / does not** (present) or **did not** (past) before the base verb.

| Tense | Affirmative | Negative |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Present Simple (I/you/we/they) | *I **like** coffee.* | *I **do not like** coffee.* |
| Present Simple (he/she/it) | *She **likes** coffee.* | *She **does not like** coffee.* |
| Past Simple (all persons) | *They **went** home.* | *They **did not go** home.* |

> **Important:** The main verb returns to its **base form** after *do not / does not / did not*.

| Incorrect | Correct |
|-----------|---------|
| *She does not **likes** coffee.* | *She does not **like** coffee.* ✓ |
| *They did not **went** home.* | *They did not **go** home.* ✓ |

### B. Negative Contractions (Short Forms)

Negative contractions are very common in spoken and informal written English.

| Full Form | Contraction | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| *is not* | *isn't* | *He **isn't** here.* |
| *are not* | *aren't* | *They **aren't** ready.* |
| *was not* | *wasn't* | *She **wasn't** there.* |
| *were not* | *weren't* | *We **weren't** late.* |
| *do not* | *don't* | *I **don't** know.* |
| *does not* | *doesn't* | *She **doesn't** like it.* |
| *did not* | *didn't* | *He **didn't** call.* |
| *have not* | *haven't* | *We **haven't** seen it.* |
| *has not* | *hasn't* | *She **hasn't** finished.* |
| *had not* | *hadn't* | *They **hadn't** arrived.* |
| *will not* | *won't* | *I **won't** forget.* |
| *shall not* | *shan't* | *We **shan't** be late.* (BrE, rare) |
| *cannot* | *can't* | *She **can't** swim.* |
| *could not* | *couldn't* | *He **couldn't** come.* |
| *should not* | *shouldn't* | *You **shouldn't** smoke.* |
| *would not* | *wouldn't* | *They **wouldn't** agree.* |
| *might not* | *mightn't* | *It **mightn't** be true.* (rare) |
| *must not* | *mustn't* | *You **mustn't** tell anyone.* |

> **Special case:** *cannot* is written as one word (not *can not*). The contraction is *can't*.

### C. Negative Adverbs and Determiners

#### 1. *No* as a Determiner

Use *no* before nouns to mean "not any."

| Affirmative | Negative with *no* |
|-------------|--------------------|
| *I have **some** money.* | *I have **no** money.* |
| *There **are** problems.* | *There **are no** problems.* |
| *She has **a** car.* | *She has **no** car.* |

> **Note:** *No* makes the sentence negative without needing *not*: *I have **no** money.* (Not: *I don't have no money.* - that's a double negative)

#### 2. *None* as a Pronoun

Use *none* alone to mean "not any" or "not one."

| Example |
|---------|
| *How many tickets are left? **None**.* |
| *None of the students passed the exam.* |
| *I asked for help, but **none** came.* |

#### 3. *Nothing, Nobody, No one, Nowhere*

| Word | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| *nothing* | not anything | *I saw **nothing**.* |
| *nobody / no one* | not anyone | *Nobody came to the party.* |
| *nowhere* | not anywhere | *He had **nowhere** to go.* |

#### 4. *Never*

*Never* means "not ever" (at no time). It is placed before the main verb.

| Affirmative | Negative with *never* |
|-------------|----------------------|
| *I have **ever** been there.* | *I have **never** been there.* |
| *She **always** arrives on time.* | *She **never** arrives on time.* |

#### 5. *Neither* and *Nor*

Use *neither* and *nor* to connect two negative ideas.

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| *neither... nor...* | *He **neither** called **nor** sent a message.* |
| *nor* (with inversion) | *She doesn't like coffee, **nor does** she like tea.* |
| *neither* (alone, meaning "also not") | *"I don't understand." - "**Neither** do I."* |

### D. Negative Questions

Negative questions are used for confirmation, surprise, or rhetorical effect.

#### 1. Formation

| Affirmative Question | Negative Question |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| *Do you like coffee?* | *Don't you like coffee?* |
| *Is she coming?* | *Isn't she coming?* |
| *Have you finished?* | *Haven't you finished?* |
| *Can he swim?* | *Can't he swim?* |

#### 2. Uses of Negative Questions

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Seeking confirmation** (expecting "yes") | *Don't you want to come?* (I think you do want to come) |
| **Expressing surprise** | *Didn't you hear the news?* (I'm surprised you didn't) |
| **Rhetorical** (not expecting an answer) | *Isn't that a beautiful view?* |
| **Making invitations or offers** | *Wouldn't you like some tea?* |

#### 3. Answering Negative Questions

Answering negative questions can be confusing. **Yes** = affirmative (positive), **No** = negative.

| Question | Meaning of "Yes" | Meaning of "No" |
|----------|------------------|-----------------|
| *Don't you like coffee?* | *Yes* = I do like coffee | *No* = I don't like coffee |
| *Isn't she here?* | *Yes* = she is here | *No* = she is not here |

> **Tip:** To avoid confusion, answer with a full sentence: *Yes, I do.* / *No, I don't.*

### E. Double Negatives

In standard English, **double negatives are incorrect**. Two negatives cancel each other out and create a positive meaning (or make the sentence ungrammatical).

#### 1. What Is a Double Negative?

Using two negative words in the same clause to express a single negative idea.

| Incorrect (Double Negative) | Correct (Single Negative) |
|-----------------------------|---------------------------|
| *I **don't have no** money.* | *I **don't have any** money.* / *I have **no** money.* |
| *She **can't find nothing**.* | *She **can't find anything**.* / *She finds **nothing**.* |
| *He **didn't go nowhere**.* | *He **didn't go anywhere**.* / *He went **nowhere**.* |
| *I **never saw nobody**.* | *I **never saw anybody**.* / *I saw **nobody**.* |

#### 2. When Double Negatives Are Acceptable (Very Rare)

In some dialects (e.g., African American Vernacular English, Cockney) and informal speech, double negatives are used for emphasis. However, they are **not acceptable in standard English** (academic, professional, or formal writing).

#### 3. Intentional Double Negatives (Rhetorical)

Sometimes a double negative is used deliberately to create a **weak positive** (litotes).

| Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| *She is **not unhappy**.* | She is happy (or at least not sad) |
| *It was **not impossible**.* | It was possible |
| *He is **not without** talent.* | He has some talent |
| *That's **not a bad** idea.* | It's a good idea |

### F. Negative Prefixes and Suffixes

Many words become negative by adding prefixes or suffixes.

#### 1. Negative Prefixes

| Prefix | Meaning | Examples |
|--------|---------|----------|
| *un-* | not | *unhappy, unable, uncertain, unfair* |
| *in-* | not | *invisible, incorrect, independent* |
| *im-* | not (before m/p) | *impossible, impolite, immature* |
| *il-* | not (before l) | *illegal, illogical, illegible* |
| *ir-* | not (before r) | *irregular, irresponsible, irrelevant* |
| *dis-* | not, opposite of | *disagree, disapprove, dishonest, dislike* |
| *non-* | not | *nonstop, nonfiction, nonviolent* |
| *mis-* | wrongly | *misunderstand, misplace, mistake* |
| *anti-* | against | *antisocial, antibiotic, antiwar* |
| *counter-* | against | *counterproductive, counteract* |

#### 2. Negative Suffix: *-less*

| Word | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| *hopeless* | without hope | *The situation is **hopeless**.* |
| *fearless* | without fear | *She is **fearless**.* |
| *endless* | without end | *The journey seemed **endless**.* |
| *useless* | without use | *This tool is **useless**.* |

### G. Negative Expressions with *Any* Words

After a negative verb, use *any, anything, anyone, anybody, anywhere* instead of *some, something, someone, somewhere*.

| Affirmative | Negative |
|-------------|----------|
| *I have **some** money.* | *I don't have **any** money.* |
| *She bought **something**.* | *She didn't buy **anything**.* |
| *Someone is here.* | *There isn't **anyone** here.* |
| *He went **somewhere**.* | *He didn't go **anywhere**.* |

> **Note:** The same rule applies in questions (whether negative or not): *Do you have **any** money?*

---

### H. *Not* vs. *No* (Key Distinction)

| Word | Part of Speech | Use | Example |
|------|----------------|-----|---------|
| *not* | Adverb | Negates a verb or clause | *She **is not** happy.* |
| *no* | Determiner | Negates a noun | *She has **no** friends.* |

**Compare:**
- *I have **not** eaten.* (negates the verb)
- *I have **no** food.* (negates the noun)

**Transformations:**

| Affirmative | Negative with *not* | Negative with *no* |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| *I have a car.* | *I **do not have** a car.* | *I have **no** car.* |
| *There is a problem.* | *There **is not** a problem.* | *There is **no** problem.* |

---

### I. *Hardly, Scarcely, Barely, Rarely, Seldom*

These are **semi-negative adverbs**. They have a negative meaning but are not followed by *not*.

| Adverb | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| *hardly* | almost not | *I **hardly** know him.* (I almost don't know him) |
| *scarcely* | almost not | *We **scarcely** had time to eat.* |
| *barely* | only just, almost not | *She **barely** passed the exam.* |
| *rarely* | not often | *He **rarely** goes out.* |
| *seldom* | not often | *They **seldom** complain.* |

> **Important:** Because these words are negative, they are not used with another negative word. Also, when they begin a sentence, **inversion** is required (see *[Section 11. Inversion](./inversion.md)*).

| Correct | Incorrect (Double Negative) |
|---------|----------------------------|
| *I **hardly** know him.* | *I **don't hardly** know him.* ✘ |
| *She **barely** passed.* | *She **didn't barely** pass.* ✘ |

**Inversion examples:**
- ***Hardly** had I arrived when the phone rang.*
- ***Rarely** do we see such talent.*
- ***Scarcely** had she finished when he interrupted.*

### J. Negative Commands (Imperatives)

To make a command negative, put **do not** (or **don't**) before the base verb.

| Affirmative Command | Negative Command |
|---------------------|------------------|
| *Sit down.* | *Do **not** sit down.* / ***Don't** sit down.* |
| *Open the door.* | *Do **not** open the door.* / ***Don't** open the door.* |
| *Be quiet.* | *Do **not** be quiet.* / ***Don't** be quiet.* |

> **Note:** The negative imperative uses *do not / don't* even with the verb *be*.

### L. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| *I don't have no money.* | *I **don't have any** money.* / *I have **no** money.* |
| *She doesn't like nothing.* | *She **doesn't like anything**.* / *She likes **nothing**.* |
| *I didn't see nobody.* | *I **didn't see anybody**.* / *I saw **nobody**.* |
| *He can't hardly walk.* | *He **can hardly** walk.* |
| *She doesn't never come.* | *She **never** comes.* / *She **doesn't ever** come.* |
| *I don't have some money.* | *I **don't have any** money.* |
| *She does not likes it.* | *She does not **like** it.* |
