### 1.7 Types of Verbs

Verbs in English fall into distinct categories based on their function in a sentence. Understanding these types is essential for mastering tense formation, sentence structure, and meaning.

---

#### A. Main Verbs vs. Auxiliary (Helping) Verbs

| Type | Function | Examples |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Main Verb** | Carries the core meaning of the action or state. Every complete sentence must have a main verb. | *She **plays** the piano.* / *They **ate** dinner.* / *I **am** happy.* |
| **Auxiliary Verb (Helping Verb)** | Helps the main verb express tense, mood, voice, or negation. Cannot stand alone as the main meaning-carrier (except *to be* and *to have*, which can also be main verbs). | *She **has** played.* (has = auxiliary, played = main) / *They **are** playing.* (are = auxiliary, playing = main) |

---

#### B. The Three Primary Auxiliary Verbs

These three verbs do the heavy lifting in English grammar. They can function as main verbs OR as auxiliaries.

##### 1. To Be (am, is, are, was, were, be, been, being)

| Role | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Main Verb** (Linking Verb) | Links subject to a description or state. | *She **is** a teacher.* / *I **was** tired.* |
| **Auxiliary Verb** | Forms continuous tenses and passive voice. | *She **is** playing.* (continuous) / *The cake **was** eaten.* (passive) |

##### 2. To Have (have, has, had)

| Role | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Main Verb** | Shows possession or experience. | *She **has** a car.* / *I **had** breakfast.* |
| **Auxiliary Verb** | Forms perfect tenses. | *She **has** played.* / *They **had** eaten.* |

##### 3. To Do (do, does, did)

| Role | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Main Verb** | Expresses performing an action or task. | *She **did** her homework.* / *They **do** yoga.* |
| **Auxiliary Verb** | Forms questions, negatives, and emphatic statements in simple tenses. | ***Does** she play?* / *She **didn't** play.* / *She **did** play!* (emphatic) |

---

#### C. Modal Auxiliary Verbs

Modal verbs express necessity, possibility, permission, ability, or obligation. They are always followed by a base verb (V1) and do not change form for person or number.

| Modal Verb | Primary Meaning(s) | Example |
|------------|-------------------|---------|
| **can** | Ability, permission, possibility | *She **can** swim.* / *Can I leave?* |
| **could** | Past ability, polite request, possibility | *She **could** swim as a child.* / *Could you help?* |
| **may** | Permission, possibility | *You **may** enter.* / *It **may** rain.* |
| **might** | Weak possibility | *She **might** come later.* |
| **must** | Obligation, strong certainty | *You **must** stop.* / *She **must** be tired.* |
| **shall** | Formal suggestion, future (chiefly British with *I/we*) | *Shall we dance?* |
| **should** | Advice, expectation | *You **should** rest.* |
| **will** | Future, willingness, prediction | *She **will** arrive soon.* |
| **would** | Polite request, hypothetical, past habit | *Would you help?* / *If I were you, I **would** go.* |
| **ought to** | Moral obligation, advice | *You **ought to** apologize.* |

> **Key Rules for Modal Verbs:**
> - Always followed by the base form (V1): *She can **play**.* (Not *She can plays* or *She can to play*)
> - No *-s* in third person: *She can go.* (Not *She cans go*)
> - Negatives formed directly: *She cannot (can't) go.*
> - Questions formed by inversion: *Can she go?*

---

#### D. Full Verbs by Function

Beyond the auxiliary/main distinction, verbs can be classified by what they do in a sentence.

##### 1. Action Verbs (Dynamic Verbs)

Express physical or mental actions. They can be used in all tenses, including continuous.

| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Physical Action** | run, eat, build, write, hit, dance |
| **Mental Action** | think, believe, consider, imagine, wonder |

- *She **runs** every morning.*
- *She **is running** right now.* (Continuous is fine)
- *I **thought** about it.*

##### 2. Linking Verbs (Stative/Non-Action)

Do not express action. They connect the subject to a word or phrase that describes, identifies, or classifies it. The complement can be a noun, adjective, or prepositional phrase. **Linking verbs are NOT used in continuous tenses.**

| Category | Examples | Sample Sentence |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Primary Linking Verb** | be (am, is, are, was, were, been, being) | *She **is** intelligent.* |
| **Sensory Perception** | look, smell, sound, taste, feel | *The cake **smells** good.* |
| **State/Existence** | seem, appear, become, remain, stay, prove, turn, grow | *He **seems** tired.* / *She **became** a doctor.* |

> **Linking vs. Action Test:** Substitute the verb with *am/is/are/was/were*. If the sentence still makes logical sense, the verb is linking.
> - *She looks happy.* -> *She is happy.* (Makes sense -> **linking**)
> - *She looks at the painting.* -> *She is at the painting.* (Doesn't make sense -> **action**)

> **Adjective Rule:** Linking verbs are followed by adjectives, not adverbs.
> - Correct: *I feel **bad**.* (Describes my emotional state)
> - Incorrect: *I feel **badly**.* (Describes my sense of touch as impaired)

##### 3. Stative Verbs (State Verbs or Non-Continuous Verbs)

Verbs that describe states, conditions, or situations rather than actions. They are **rarely or never used in continuous tenses**. Linking verbs are a subset of stative verbs, but the category is broader.

| Category | Examples | Correct Usage | Incorrect Usage |
|----------|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Emotions** | love, hate, like, dislike, prefer, want, fear | *I **love** this song.* | *I am loving this song.* (informal exception exists) |
| **Mental States** | know, believe, understand, remember, forget, mean, realize | *She **knows** the answer.* | *She is knowing the answer.* |
| **Possession** | have, own, possess, belong | *They **own** a house.* | *They are owning a house.* |
| **Senses/Perception** | see, hear, smell, taste, feel (perception, not action) | *I **see** the bird.* | *I am seeing the bird.* |
| **Measurement/Description** | cost, weigh, measure, contain, matter, seem | *It **costs** ten dollars.* | *It is costing ten dollars.* |

> **Exception:** Some stative verbs can be used in continuous form when they describe an action or a temporary state.
> - *I **have** a car.* (possession, stative) vs. *I **am having** breakfast.* (eating, action)
> - *I **think** it's good.* (opinion, stative) vs. *I **am thinking** about it.* (considering, action)
> - *She **looks** happy.* (appearance, linking) vs. *She **is looking** at the painting.* (action)

---

#### E. Transitive vs. Intransitive Verbs

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Transitive** | Requires a direct object to complete its meaning. The action transfers to something or someone. | *She **ate** the cake.* (Cake is the object) / *He **needs** help.* |
| **Intransitive** | Does not take a direct object. The action is complete on its own. | *She **arrived** late.* / *The baby **cried**.* |
| **Ditransitive** | Takes both a direct and an indirect object. | *She **gave** him a gift.* (Him = indirect, gift = direct) |
| **Ambitransitive** | Can be used both transitively and intransitively. | *She **ate**.* (Intransitive) / *She **ate** pizza.* (Transitive) |

---

#### F. Regular vs. Irregular Verbs

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|------|------------|----------|
| **Regular Verbs** | Form their past simple (V2) and past participle (V3) by adding *-ed* (or *-d*). | play -> played -> played / walk -> walked -> walked / love -> loved -> loved |
| **Irregular Verbs** | Form V2 and V3 in unpredictable ways; must be memorized. | eat -> ate -> eaten / go -> went -> gone / sing -> sang -> sung / cut -> cut -> cut |

---

#### G. Summary Table: Verb Types at a Glance

| Classification | Types |
|----------------|-------|
| **By Structure** | Main Verbs, Auxiliary Verbs (Primary: be/have/do), Modal Auxiliaries |
| **By Function** | Action (Dynamic), Linking, Stative |
| **By Object Requirement** | Transitive, Intransitive, Ditransitive, Ambitransitive |
| **By Formation** | Regular, Irregular |
