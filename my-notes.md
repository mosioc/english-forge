## types of words (parts of speech)

- nouns
    - name a person, place, thing, or idea (*dog, city, happiness*)
    - concrete: physical (*table, rain*)
    - abstract: concept (*love, freedom*)
    - countable: can count (*apple, chairs*)
    - uncountable: cannot count (*water, information*)
    - proper: specific (*Maria, London*)

- pronouns
    - replace a noun (*I, you, he, she, it*)
    - subject: *I, you, he, she, it, we, they*
    - object: *me, you, him, her, it, us, them*
    - possessive: *mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs*
    - reflexive: *myself, yourself, himself, herself*
    - demonstrative: *this, that, these, those*
    - indefinite: *someone, anything, no one, everything*
    - relative: *who, whom, whose, which, that*

- verbs
    - express action or state (*run, think, is, become*)
    - action: physical/mental (*run, eat, think*)
    - linking: connects subject to description (*is, seem, become*)
    - auxiliary: helps main verb (*be, have, do*)
    - modal: possibility/obligation (*can, must, should*)

- adjectives
    - describe a noun/pronoun (*big, beautiful, red*)
    - comparative: compare two (*bigger, more beautiful*)
    - superlative: compare three+ (*biggest, most beautiful*)
    - possessive: show ownership (*my, your, his, her*)

- adverbs
    - modify verbs/adjectives/adverbs (*quickly, very, often*)
    - manner: *quickly, carefully* (how?)
    - frequency: *always, often, never* (how often?)
    - time: *now, yesterday, soon* (when?)
    - place: *here, there, outside* (where?)
    - degree: *very, too, enough* (to what extent?)

- prepositions
    - show relationship (*in, on, at, under, before, with*)
    - time: *at, on, in, before, after, during, since, for*
    - place: *at, on, in, under, over, behind, between*
    - direction: *to, from, into, onto, towards, through*
    - key rule: verbs after prepositions -> *-ing* (*interested in learning*)

- conjunctions
    - connect words/clauses (*and, but, because*)
    - coordinating (FANBOYS): *for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so*
    - subordinating: *because, although, if, when, while, unless*
    - correlative: *both... and, either... or, neither... nor*

- articles / determiners
    - specify nouns (*a, an, the, this, my, some*)
    - definite: *the*
    - indefinite: *a, an*
    - quantifiers: *some, any, many, much, few, little*

- interjections
    - express emotion (*Oh!, Wow!, Ouch!, Hey!*)
    - surprise: *Oh!, Wow!* | pain: *Ouch!* | disgust: *Ew!*
    - joy: *Yay!* | realization: *Aha!* | mistake: *Oops!*

- [key distinctions]
    - adjective vs. adverb
        - adj describes noun; adv describes verb/adj/adv
        - *She is careful.* (adj) / *She drives carefully.* (adv)
    - preposition vs. conjunction
        - prep + noun; conj + clause
        - *after the game* (prep) / *after the game ended* (conj)
    - determiner vs. pronoun
        - det before noun; pron replaces noun
        - *This book* (det) / *This is mine* (pron)

## types of verbs 

- main verbs vs. auxiliary verbs
    - main verb: carries core meaning (*She **plays**.*)
    - auxiliary verb: helps express tense/voice/negation (*She **has** played.*)

- three primary auxiliaries
    - to be (*am, is, are, was, were, be, been, being*)
        - main: linking (*She **is** a teacher.*)
        - auxiliary: continuous & passive (*She **is** playing. / It **was** eaten.*)
    - to have (*have, has, had*)
        - main: possession (*She **has** a car.*)
        - auxiliary: perfect tenses (*She **has** finished.*)
    - to do (*do, does, did*)
        - main: perform action (*She **did** homework.*)
        - auxiliary: questions, negatives, emphasis (***Does** she play? / She **did** call!*)

- action, linking, stative
    - action (dynamic): physical/mental actions; continuous OK (*run, eat, think*)
    - linking: connect subject to description; no continuous (*be, seem, become, look, smell, taste*)
    - stative: states/emotions/possession; rarely continuous (*know, love, own, want*)
    - linking verb test: replace with *is/was*
        - *She looks happy.* -> *She is happy.* (linking ✓)
        - *She looks at it.* -> *She is at it.* (action ✗)
    - linking rule: followed by adjectives, not adverbs
        - *I feel bad.* (not *I feel badly.*)
    - linking ⊂ stative (linking is a subset of stative)

- stative -> action (meaning change)
    - *have*: possession -> experience (*I have a car. / I'm having lunch.*)
    - *think*: opinion -> process (*I think so. / I'm thinking.*)
    - *see*: perception -> meeting (*I see it. / I'm seeing her.*)
    - *taste*: flavor -> testing (*It tastes good. / He's tasting it.*)
    - *feel*: state -> touching (*I feel happy. / I'm feeling it.*)
    - *smell*: scent -> sniffing (*It smells good. / She's smelling it.*)
    - *look*: appearance -> directing eyes (*You look tired. / She's looking.*)
    - *weigh*: weight -> measuring (*It weighs 2 kg. / I'm weighing it.*)

- transitive vs. intransitive
    - transitive: needs direct object (*She ate **the cake**.*)
    - intransitive: no object (*She arrived.*)
    - ditransitive: two objects (*She gave **him** **a gift**.*)
    - intransitive verbs cannot be passive (*The child vanished.* NOT *was vanished*)

- regular vs. irregular
    - regular: V2/V3 + *-ed* (*play -> played -> played*)
    - irregular: unpredictable (*eat -> ate -> eaten / go -> went -> gone*)

- verb-forming
    - `-ize`/`-ise`, `-en`
- adverb-forming
    - `-ly`, `-ward(s)`
- negative prefixes
    - `un-`, `in-`/`im-`/`il-`/`ir-`, `dis-`, `non-`, `mis-`, `anti-`, `counter-`

## spelling rules

- *-y* -> *-ie* (before -s/-ed/-er/-est)
    - *baby -> babies, happy -> happier*
- keep *-y* before *-ing*
    - *trying, hurrying*
- keep *-y* after vowel
    - *plays, played, monkeys*
- drop *-e* before *-ing*
    - *hope -> hoping* (but: *be -> being*, *see -> seeing*)
- keep *-e* before *-ed*
    - *hope -> hoped*
- double final consonant (stressed, CVC)
    - *stop -> stopping, begin -> beginning*
- no doubling (unstressed or two consonants)
    - *visit -> visiting, help -> helping*
- BrE doubles *l*
    - *travelling* (AmE: *traveling*)

## capitalization

- proper nouns
    - *John, Paris, Monday, January, English*
- titles before names
    - *Dr. Smith, President Biden*
- titles alone = lowercase
    - *The doctor is here.*
- days, months, holidays
    - *Monday, July, Christmas*
- seasons = lowercase
    - *spring, summer* (unless in a title)
- *I* always capitalized
    - *My friend and I*

## numbers, dates & time

- numbers
    - spell out 1-9 (*five*), digits 10+ (*15*)
    - spell out at sentence start (*Twenty-five people came*)
- dates
    - US: *December 25, 2024* -> 12/25/2024
    - UK: *25 December 2024* -> 25/12/2024
- time
    - *quarter past / half past / quarter to* (UK)
    - *three fifteen / three thirty / three forty-five* (US)
    - *at* clock times (*at 5 PM*), *on* days (*on Monday*), *in* months (*in July*)

## tenses 

- normal  
    - simple   
        - *(present) v1, (past) v2, will + (future) v1*  
    - continuous   
        - *(be) + V-ing*  
    - perfect    
        - *has/had/have + past participle (v3)*  
    - perfect continuous  
        - *has/had/have + been + V-ing*  

- passive  
    - simple   
        - *(be) + past participle (v3)*  
    - continuous  
        - *(be) + being + past participle (v3)*  
    - perfect  
        - *have/has/had + been + past participle (v3)* 
    - modal  
        - *(modal verb) + (be) + past participle (v3)*  
    - modal perfect
        - *(modal verb) + have + been + past participle (v3)*

- key tense rules
    - state verbs (no continuous)
        - *know, believe, understand, belong, own, love, hate, want, need, seem*
    - state -> action (meaning change)
        - *think* (opinion) vs. *be thinking* (process)
        - *have* (possession) vs. *be having* (experience)
    - performatives
        - *I promise, I apologize, I declare, I suggest, I warn* (the words are the action)
    - used to (past habits & states)
        - *I used to smoke. She used to live in Paris.*
    - would (past habits only, NOT states)
        - *We would visit every summer.* (NOT *She would have long hair.*)
    - for (duration) vs. since (starting point)
        - *for two hours / since 2020*
    - when (past simple) vs. how long (present perfect)
        - *When did you arrive? / How long have you been here?*
    - have / have got (possession)
        - *I have a car. = I've got a car.* (BrE)
    - future from the past
        - *was going to / was about to / was to*

## tenses advanced (future)

- present tenses for the futures  
    - *present simple --> timetables, schedules*   
    - *present continuous --> arrangements*   
    - *(be) going to --> plans, intentions, predictions based on evidence*  
- will
    - *will --> spontaneous decisions, promises, predictions*
- future continuous
    - *will be + V-ing --> action in progress at a future time; polite inquiries*
        - *This time tomorrow I'll be flying.*
- future perfect
    - *will have + V3 --> completed before a future time*
        - *I'll have finished by Friday.*
- formal future structures
    - *be to + infinitive --> formal plans, commands*
        - *The president is to visit.*
    - *be about to + infinitive --> immediate future*
        - *The movie is about to start.*
    - *be due to / be set to / be bound to --> scheduled, expected, almost certain*
        - *The train is due at 6. It's bound to rain.*
- rule: no *will* in future time clauses
    - *I'll call you when I **arrive**.* (NOT *when I will arrive*)

## modals 

- structure
    - *subject + auxiliary verb (modal) + base verb (v1)*  
        - no *to*, no *-s*, no *do/does/did* for questions or negatives
- primary modals
    - *can* --> ability, possibility, informal permission
    - *can't* --> impossibility, disbelief
    - *could* --> past ability, polite permission, possibility, suggestion
    - *may* --> formal permission, possibility
    - *might* --> weak possibility
    - *must* --> strong obligation, strong certainty
    - *mustn't* --> prohibition
    - *should* --> advice, expectation
    - *ought to* --> moral obligation
    - *had better* --> strong advice with warning
    - *will* --> future, prediction, promise, spontaneous decision
    - *would* --> past habit, polite request, hypothetical
    - *shall* --> formal future (*I/we*), suggestion (*Shall we...?*)
    - *needn't* --> lack of necessity (formal/BrE)
    - *don't have to / don't need to* --> lack of necessity
- mustn't vs. don't have to
    - *You mustn't smoke here.* (prohibition)
    - *You don't have to come.* (no obligation)

## past modals (speculation & regret)

- *must have + V3* --> almost certain about the past
- *may/might/could have + V3* --> possible in the past
- *can't/couldn't have + V3* --> impossible in the past
- *should have + V3* --> regret / missed opportunity
- *shouldn't have + V3* --> criticism of a past action
- *needn't have + V3* --> done but unnecessary
- *didn't need to + V1* --> not done because unnecessary
- *would have + V3* --> hypothetical past (3rd conditional)

## word order (SVO)

- basic patterns
    - intransitive (no object)
        - *S + V* --> *The baby cried.*
    - transitive (direct object)
        - *S + V + DO* --> *She bought a car.*
    - ditransitive (two objects)
        - *S + V + IO + DO* --> *He gave me a gift.*
    - linking (subject complement)
        - *S + V + SC* --> *She is happy.*
    - object complement
        - *S + V + O + OC* --> *They painted the room blue.*
- two objects: both patterns
    - IO first (no preposition)
        - *V + IO + DO* --> *He gave me a book.*
    - DO first (with *to/for*)
        - *V + DO + to/for + IO* --> *He gave a book to me.*
- when to use the *to/for* pattern
    - IO is a long phrase
        - *I gave the documents to the woman who called.*
    - emphasize the IO
        - *She gave the award to him, not to his brother.*
    - verb requires *to*
        - *Explain the problem **to me**.* (NOT *Explain me the problem*)
    - both objects are pronouns
        - *Give it to me.* (NOT *Give me it*)
- verbs that require *to* (no V+IO+DO)
    - *explain, describe, suggest, recommend, say*
- place before time rule
    - place (Where?) -> time (When?)
        - *She went **to the park** (place) **yesterday** (time).*
    - exceptions: time first
        - time is the topic/introduces the sentence
        - time contrasts with another time
        - place is a long phrase
- manner + place + time
    - manner (How?) -> place (Where?) -> time (When?)
        - *She drove **carefully** (M) **to work** (P) **this morning** (T).*
- key rule: don't separate the verb from its object
    - ✓ *I read **the book** yesterday.*
    - ✗ *I read yesterday **the book**.*

## adverb placement

- frequency (*always, never*)
    - before main verb / after *be*
        - *She often visits. / He is always late.*
- manner (*carefully*)
    - after verb + object
        - *She opened the box carefully.*
- place (*here, outside*)
    - end
        - *Let's go outside.*
- time (*yesterday*)
    - end (or front for emphasis)
        - *I'll call you tomorrow.*
- certainty (*probably*)
    - mid (before main verb, after aux)
        - *She will definitely come.*
- viewpoint (*personally*)
    - beginning of clause
        - *Personally, I disagree.*

## sentence types by function

- declarative
    - give information
        - `.` (period)
        - *She works at a hospital.*
- interrogative
    - ask for information
        - `?` (question mark)
        - *Where does she live?*
- imperative
    - give command/request
        - `.` or `!`
        - *Sit down. / Don't touch that.*
- exclamative
    - express emotion
        - `!`
        - *What a beautiful day!*

## if-wish clauses and conditionals 

- if  
    - zero conditional  
        - *if + present simple, present simple*   
            - always true
    - first conditional  
        - *if + present simple, will + base verb*   
            - real future
    - second conditional  
        - *if + past simple, would + base verb*    
            - unreal present/future
    - third conditional  
        - *if + past perfect + would + have + past participle (v3)*   
            - unreal past

- wish  
    - present (unreal)
        - *wish + past simple*    
            - *I wish I had a car.*
    - present (formal)
        - *wish + were*
            - *I wish I were taller.*
    - past (regret)
        - *wish + past perfect*  
            - *I wish I had studied.*
    - future (annoyance/desire)
        - *wish + would + base verb*  (others only)
            - *I wish you would listen.*

- mixed conditional **(when the two clauses belong to different time frames - one past and one present (or future))**  
    - past cause --> present result
        - *if + past perfect, would + base verb*  
    - present condition --> past result
        - *if + past simple, would + have + past participle (v3)*  

- key alternatives
    - *as long as / provided (that)* --> only if
    - *unless* --> if not
    - *otherwise* --> if not (consequence)
    - *in case* --> as a precaution (≠ *if*)
    - *even if* --> regardless of the condition
    - *only if* --> the condition is the only way
    - *but for / if it weren't for* --> without this

- inversion in conditionals (formal)
    - *Should you need help...* (= *If you should need help...*)
    - *Were I you...* (= *If I were you...*)
    - *Were he to resign...* (= *If he were to resign...*)
    - *Had I known...* (= *If I had known...*)

## unreal time (hypothetical situations)

- hope  
    - *hope + present/future* --> future (possible)
        - *I hope it doesn't rain.*

- if only **(grammatically similar to *wish*.)**  
    - *if only + past simple* --> present unreal
    - *if only + past perfect* --> past regret
    - *if only + would + base verb* --> future frustration

- it's time  
    - *[it's (high/about) time] + past simple* --> overdue action
        - *It's time you left.*
    - *It's time* can also be followed by *for + noun*: *It's time **for a break**.*

- would rather **(grammatically similar to *wish*.)**  
    - *would rather + base verb* --> same-subject preference
        - *I'd rather stay home.*
    - *would rather + different subject + past simple* --> present/future preference
        - *I'd rather you didn't smoke.*
    - *would rather + different subject + past perfect* --> past regret about others
        - *I'd rather you hadn't told him.*

- as if / as though  
    - *[as if / as though] + past simple* --> present unreal appearance
        - *He talks as if he knew everything.*
    - *[as if / as though] + past perfect* --> past unreal appearance
        - *She looked as if she had seen a ghost.*

- what if  
    - *what if + present simple* --> future possibility
    - *what if + past simple* --> present imagined
    - *what if + past perfect* --> past hypothetical

- the sooner... better  
    - *The sooner + Subject + Verb..., the better / faster / etc*  

## subjunctive mood

- present subjunctive  
    - **base form (V1)** of the verb for **all persons**   
        - It does **not** add `-s` in the third person singular.
        - *I suggest that he **go**.*
        - *She insisted that he **be** present.*

- past subjunctive  
    - Its distinctive feature is the use of **were** for **all persons** (instead of *was*).
        - *If I **were** you...*

- negative subjunctive   
    - To form the negative subjunctive, add **not** before the base verb.  
        - Do **not** use *do not / does not / did not* with the subjunctive.
        - *I suggest that he **not go**.*
        - *It is essential that she **not be** late.*

- subjunctive in passive  
    - *be + past participle (V3)*  
        - *They demanded that it **be done**.*

- formulaic (fixed expressions)
    - *God save the King. / Be that as it may. / Come what may. / Long live... / Suffice it to say.*

- subjunctive triggers
    - verbs
        - *suggest, insist, recommend, demand, request, propose, ask, advise, require, urge, command, order*
    - adjectives
        - *essential, important, necessary, vital, crucial, imperative, advisable*
    - nouns
        - *suggestion, demand, recommendation, requirement, proposal, insistence*
    - formula
        - *verb / adj / noun + that + subject + BASE VERB*
    - BrE alternative
        - *should* + base verb
            - *I suggest that he **should go**.*

## passive voice 

- passive voice  
    - simple   
        - *(be) + past participle (v3)*  (future: will + be + (v3))
    - continuous  
        - *(be) + being + past participle (v3)*  
    - perfect  
        - *have/has/had + been + past participle (v3)* 

- modal passive
    - *(modal verb) + (be) + past participle (v3)*  
    - modal perfect passive
        - *(modal verb) + have + been + past participle (v3)*

- causative passive (have something done)
    - formal
        - *have + object + past participle (V3)*
            - *I had my hair cut.*
    - informal
        - *get + object + past participle (V3)*
            - *I need to get my car fixed.*

- passive in conditional statements 
    - first conditional
        - *if + present simple, will + be + (V3)*
    - second conditional
        - *if + past simple, would + be + (V3)*
    - third conditional 
        - *if + past perfect, would have + been + (V3)*

- passive in imperative statements  
    - *Let + object + be + past participle (formal instructions)*
        - *Let the door be locked.*

- impersonal passive (It is said...)
    - *It + passive + that-clause*
        - *It is said that he is rich.*

- personal passive (He is said to...)
    - *Subject + passive + to-infinitive*
        - *He is said to be rich.*

- passive with prepositional verbs
    - *be + V3 + preposition*
        - *The child was looked after. / He was laughed at.*

- passive gerund
    - *being + V3*
        - *She dislikes being told what to do.*

- passive infinitive
    - *to be + V3*
        - *She expects to be given a promotion.*

## reported speech

(backshift)  

- tenses changes  
    - simple  
        - *present simple -> past simple*   
        - *past simple -> past perfect*  
        - *future simple -> future simple conditional* (would)  
    - continuous  
        - *present continuous -> past continuous*   
        - *past continuous -> past perfect continuous*  
        - *future continuous -> future continuous conditional* (would be)  
    - perfect  
        - *present perfect -> past perfect*  
        - *past perfect -> past perfect (no change)*

- modals changes  
    - *can -> could*  
    - *may -> might*  
    - *must -> had to*  
    - some modals that do NOT change: *could, would, should, might, ought to, had better*  

- pronoun changes 
    - *first person -> third person*
        - `i`, `me`, `my` -> `he`, `him`, `she`, `her`
        - `we`, `us`, `our` -> `they`, `them`, `their` 
    - *second person -> first person or third person*
        - `you`, `your` -> (based on context)

- time/place changes 
    - *now -> then / at that time*
    - *today -> that day*
    - *yesterday -> the day before*
    - *tomorrow -> the next day / the following day*
    - *last week/month -> the previous week/month / the week/month before*
    - *next week/month -> the following week/month*
    - *this -> that*
    - *these -> those*
    - *here -> there*
    - *ago -> before*

- reporting verbs changes 
    - *say -> said*
    - *tell -> told*

- reporting patterns
    - statement
        - *say/tell + (that) + clause*
            - *He said (that) he was tired.*
    - yes/no question
        - *ask + if/whether + clause*
            - *She asked if I was tired.*
    - wh-question
        - *ask + wh-word + clause*
            - *She asked where I lived.*
    - command
        - *tell + object + to + V1*
            - *He told me to sit down.*
    - negative command
        - *tell + object + not to + V1*
            - *She told me not to go.*
    - suggestion
        - *suggest + -ing / that-clause*
            - *She suggested going.* / *She suggested that we go.*
    - offer
        - *offer + to + V1*
            - *He offered to help.*
    - promise
        - *promise + to + V1*
            - *She promised to call.*
    - refusal
        - *refuse + to + V1*
            - *He refused to go.*

- reporting with nouns and adjectives
    - noun + that-clause
        - *His claim that he was innocent was false.*
    - adj + that-clause
        - *It is clear that he made a mistake.*
    - adj + to-infinitive
        - *It is important to note that sales increased.*

- exceptions to backshifting
    - the statement is still true 
    - reporting verb is present tense 
    - reporting a recent conversation 
    - general truths / timeless facts
    - modals: *could, would, should, might, ought to, had better*

## auxiliary verbs 

- (to [be]) 
    - am/is/are
        - *am/is/are + V-ing*
        - *am/is/are + (v3)* (passive)
    - was/were
        - *was/were + V-ing*
        - *was/were + (v3)* (passive)
    - be
        - *will + be + V-ing*
        - *will + (v3)* (passive)
    - been
        - *has/have + been + V-ing*
        - *has/have + been + (v3)* (passive)
        - *had + been + V-ing*
        - *had + been + (v3)* (passive)

- (to [have])
    - have/has/had
        - *has/have + V3* 
        - *had + V3* 
        - *will have + V3*
        - *has/have been + V-ing*  
        - *had been + V-ing*  

- [do]
    - do/does/did  
        - questions  
            - *do/does/did + subject + V1*  
        - negatives 
            - *Subject + do/does/did + not + Base Verb (V1)*
        - emphasis 
            - *do/does/did + V1* 
                - *I **do** like it! / She **did** call you.*

- (modals)
    - `can`, `could`, `will`, `would`, `shall`, `should`, `must`, `may`, `might`, `ought to` 

- semi-modals
    - *have to, need to, be able to, be allowed to, be going to, used to, had better, would rather*

- (short responses)
    - positive statement -> affirmative
        - *So + aux + I*
    - negative statement -> negative
        - *Neither/Nor + aux + I*
    - *think/hope/believe/expect/be afraid* + clause
        - *I think so.* / *I don't think so.* / *I hope not.*

## inversion 

- inversion [after]:  
    - *Negative/Emphatic Phrase + Auxiliary/Modal Verb + Subject + Main Verb*   
        - negative adverbs   
            - `never`  
            - `rarely`  
            - `seldom`  
            - `hardly / scarcely / barely`  
                - *Hardly had I sat down when the phone rang.*
        - emphatic phrases   
            - `not only... but also`   
                - *Not only is she smart, but she is also kind.*
            - `little`   
                - *Little did she know what awaited her.*
            - `so + adj/adv + that`  
                - *So tired was he that he fell asleep.*
            - `such + be + that`  
                - *Such was the force that windows shattered.*
        - *only + time/condition phrase*
            - *Only then did I understand.*
        - *under no circumstances / in no way / at no time*
            - *Under no circumstances should you open that door.*
    - adverbs of place  
        - *Adverb of Place + Verb + Subject*  
            - `here`, `there`, `up ahead`, `in the corner`  
                - *Here comes the bus.* / *There goes my chance.*
                If the subject is a **pronoun**, standard order is maintained: *Here **it comes**.* (Not: *Here comes it*)  

- inversion [in]:  
    - conditional statements   
        - *Should + Subject + Base Verb* (= *If you should...*)
        - *Were + Subject + (to)* (= *If I were...* / *If he were to...*)
        - *Had + Subject + Past Participle* (= *If I had...*)
        - *Were it not for / Had it not been for + noun*

- inversion [with]:  
    - `neither`, `nor`, `so`  (agreement)
        - *Neither/Nor/So + Auxiliary/Modal Verb + Subject*   
            - *So do I. / Neither do I.*
    - `As` / `Though` (concession)
        - *Strange as it may seem... / Try as he might... / Child as he is...* (no article)
    - wishes with `May`
        - *May + S + V*
            - *May you be happy! / May you have a long life!*

## emphatic structures

- `It`-clefts  
    - *It + (be) + [emphasized element] + that + clause* 
        - [**emphasized element**]:  
            - subject  
            - object (thing, reason, ...)  
            - adverb of time  
            - adverb of place  
            - *`not until` + time expression*  
                - *It was not until midnight that I finished.*

- `Wh`-clefts (pseudo-clefts)  
    - *What / Where / When / Why / How + clause + (be) + [emphasized element]*
        - *What I need is rest.*

- reversed `Wh`-clefts
    - *[Focus] + be + wh-clause*
        - *A holiday is what I need. / Rest is what I need.*

- `All`-clefts
    - *All + clause + (be) + [emphasized element]*
        - *All I need is rest.*

- emphatic `do`
    - *Subject + do/does/did + base verb*
        - *I do like it!*

- emphatic `It` (without cleft)
    - Sometimes *it* is used as a dummy subject to emphasize an infinitive or *that*-clause.
        - *It is a pleasure to meet you.*

## questions 

- yes/no questions (no auxiliary)
    - *do/does/did + subject + base verb*  
        - *Do you like coffee?*
- yes/no questions (with auxiliary/modal)
    - *Aux/Modal + subject + base verb*  
        - *Can you swim? / Is she coming?*

- wh-questions   
    - *wh-word + auxiliary/modal + subject + base verb*  
        - *Where do you live?*
    - **Special Case - *Wh* as Subject:** When the wh-word is the subject of the question, no inversion occurs.
        - *Who called? / What happened?*

- embedded questions (indirect)  
    - *introductory phrase + wh-word + subject + verb*  
        - **No inversion** within the embedded wh-clause. The word order reverts to **Subject + Verb** (standard statement order).  
        - **No *do/does/did*** inside the embedded clause.
        - *Can you tell me where the station is?*

- tag-questions 
    - positive statement + negative tag
        - *You like coffee, don't you?*
    - negative statement + positive tag
        - *She isn't here, is she?*
    - special tags
        - *I'm late, aren't I?*
        - *Let's go, shall we?*
        - *Open the door, will you?*

- echo questions (surprise)
    - repeat speaker's words + rising intonation
        - *"I'm moving to Japan." - "You're moving to Japan?"*
- echo questions with wh-word
    - wh-word replaces unclear element
        - *"I met someone." - "You met who?"*

- *that*-clause questions
    - *How/Why is it that + clause?*
        - *How is it that you arrived so late?*
    - *Is it true/possible that + clause?*
        - *Is it true that she resigned?*

## negative structures 

*English forms negatives primarily by adding **not** after an auxiliary verb*

- with auxiliary verbs  
    - The basic rule is to add `not` after the auxiliary verb.
        - *She isn't ready.*

- without auxiliary verbs  
    - When there is no auxiliary verb, use `do not / does not` (present) or `did not` (past) before the base verb.
        - *I don't like it.*

- negative contractions   
    - *aux + n't*
        - *She isn't ready.*
    - *cannot* is written as one word (not *can not*). The contraction is *can't*.  

- negative adverbs and determiners   
    - `no`  
        - Use *no* before nouns to mean "not any."
            - *I have no money.*
    - `none`  
        - Use *none* alone to mean "not any" or "not one."  
    - `nothing`, `nobody`, `no one`, `nowhere`  
    - `never`  
        - *Never* means "not ever" (at no time). It is placed before the main verb.
            - *I never lie.*
    - `neither... nor...`  
        - Use *neither* and *nor* to connect two negative ideas.
            - *He neither called nor wrote.*

- negative questions  
    - affirmative questions vs negative questions   
        - Negative questions are used for confirmation, surprise, or rhetorical effect.
            - *Don't you like it?*
    - Answering negative questions can be confusing.  
        - **Yes** = affirmative (positive), **No** = negative.  

- negative commands
    - *Do not / Don't + V1*
        - *Don't go there.*

- semi-negatives
    - *hardly, barely, scarcely, rarely, seldom*
        - *I hardly know her.* (NOT *I don't hardly*)

- double negatives   
    - In standard English, **double negatives are incorrect**. Two negatives cancel each other out. Do not use double negatives in formal writing.
        - *I don't have no money.* ✗ -> *I don't have any money.* ✓
        - Sometimes a double negative is used deliberately to create a **weak positive** (litotes).

- negative prefixes and suffixes   
    - prefixes  
        - *un-*  
        - *in-*  
        - *im-*  
        - *il-*  
        - *ir-*  
        - *dis-*  
        - *non-*  
        - *mis-*  
        - *anti-*  
        - *counter-*  
    - suffix  
        - *-less*  

- negative expressions with `any`   
    - After a negative verb, use *any, anything, anyone, anybody, anywhere* instead of *some, something, someone, somewhere*.  
        - The same rule applies in questions (whether negative or not)
        - *I don't have any money.*

## exclamations and interjections

- exclamations  
    - A full sentence or phrase expressing strong emotion  
        - **Has a subject and verb** (or implied structure)  
            - Use *a* before singular countable nouns. Do not use *a/an* before plural or uncountable nouns.
            - what-exclamation:
                - *`What` + (a/an) + (adjective/noun) + (subject + verb)!*
                    - *What a beautiful day!*
            - how-exclamation:
                - *`How` + adjective/adverb + (subject + verb)!*
                    - *How kind you are!*
            - so/such-exclamation:
                - *`So` + adj! / `Such` + (a/an) + adj + noun!*
                    - *You're so kind! / Such a mess!*
            - negative question exclamation
                - *`Isn't`/`Don't`/`Weren't` + subject...!*
                    - *Isn't she lovely!*
            - infinitive exclamation (To)
                - *`To` + think/be/have...!*
                    - *To think I trusted him!*
            - one-word exclamation (Sentence Words)

- interjections
    - A single word or short phrase expressing sudden emotion  
        - **Stands alone**, no grammatical connection to the sentence  
        - common interjections
            - surprise: *Oh!, Wow!, Gosh!*
            - pain: *Ouch!, Ow!*
            - disgust: *Ew!, Yuck!, Ugh!*
            - joy: *Yay!, Hooray!*
            - annoyance: *Ugh!, Grr!*
            - realization: *Aha!, Oh!*
            - mistake: *Oops!, Whoops!*
            - hesitation: *Um, Er, Uh*
        - punctuation
            - strong emotion: `!` (exclamation mark)
            - mild emotion: `,` (comma)

## -ing and to 

> - **Gerund (`-ing`):** Functions as a noun (e.g., *Swimming is fun.*)  
> - **Infinitive (`to + V1`):** Expresses purpose or follows specific verbs (e.g., *I want to swim.*)  

- verb + `-ing` (**gerunds**)  
    - *enjoy, avoid, mind, finish, suggest, admit, deny, consider, practise, risk, postpone, miss, keep*
        - The `-ing` form of a verb, when used as a noun, is called a **gerund**. It functions as: 
            - subject
            - object
            - complement

- verb + `to` (**infinitives**)  
    - *want, need, hope, plan, decide, agree, offer, promise, fail, learn, manage, refuse, pretend, wish*
        - expresses purpose or follows specific verbs. It functions as:
            - purpose 
            - subject complement 

- verb + object + `to` + (v1) (**infinitive with object**)  
    - *tell, ask, want, advise, allow, remind, teach, encourage, order, expect*

- preposition + `-ing`  
    - ALL prepositions -> *-ing*
        - *before going, after eating, without saying, interested in learning*

- adjective + `to` + (v1) (**infinitive**)  
    - *happy to help, afraid to ask, ready to go, difficult to understand*

- meaning change: *-ing* vs. *to*
    - *stop* + -ing --> quit (*stop smoking*)
    - *stop* + to --> in order to (*stop to smoke*)
    - *remember* + -ing --> memory of past (*remember locking*)
    - *remember* + to --> don't forget future (*remember to lock*)
    - *forget* + -ing --> memory of past (*forget meeting*)
    - *forget* + to --> fail to do future (*forget to call*)
    - *regret* + -ing --> sorry about past (*regret telling*)
    - *regret* + to --> sorry to announce (*regret to inform*)
    - *try* + -ing --> experiment (*try pressing*)
    - *try* + to --> make an effort (*try to fix*)
    - *mean* + -ing --> involve (*means working*)
    - *mean* + to --> intend (*didn't mean to hurt*)
    - *need* + -ing --> passive meaning (*needs washing*)
    - *need* + to --> active obligation (*need to wash*)

- specific structures and exceptions  
    - *would rather + V1*
        - *I'd rather stay.*
    - *would prefer + to + V1*
        - *I'd prefer to leave.*
    - *be/get used to + -ing*
        - *I'm used to waking early.*
    - *look forward to + -ing*
        - *I look forward to seeing you.*
    - *there's no point in + -ing*
        - *There's no point in worrying.*
    - *it's no use + -ing*
        - *It's no use complaining.*
    - purpose clauses  
        - *to + V1* (purpose)
            - *I came to help.*
        - *for + -ing* (object's purpose)
            - *This is a tool for cutting.*
        - *so that + clause* (with modal verb)
            - *Speak clearly so that everyone can understand.*
    - afraid to do vs. afraid of doing  
        - *afraid to do* (infinitive; `to`): Fear of performing the action itself
            - *afraid to climb the ladder*
        - *afraid of doing* (gerund; `-ing`): Fear of the result or consequence 
            - *afraid of falling*
    - verbs of perception: *See*, *Hear*, *Watch*, *Feel*  
        - *see/hear/watch/feel + object + V1*: Perception of the **whole action**
            - *I saw him leave.*
        - *see/hear/watch/feel + object + -ing*: Perception of an action **in progress** 
            - *I saw him leaving.*
    - participial phrases
        - present participle (active): *-ing* phrase
            - *The man standing there is my uncle.*
        - past participle (passive): *-ed* phrase
            - *The car parked outside is mine.*
    - ditransitive: two objects
        - *She gave me a book. / She gave a book to me.*
    - ditransitive passive (IO subject)
        - *I was given a book.*
    - ditransitive passive (DO subject)
        - *A book was given to me.*

## ellipsis (omitting words)

> **Symbol:** Linguists often use **Ø** (zero symbol) to mark an omitted word.  

> **Grammatical ellipsis**: Omitting understood words; *She can swim, and I can too.*   
> **Ellipsis marks (...)**: Three dots indicating omitted text or a pause; *"He said... something about leaving."*  

- verb phrase ellipsis (VPE)  
    - *Auxiliary/Modal + Ø (verb phrase understood)*  
        - *She can swim, and I can __ too.*

- to-infinitive ellipsis
    - *to + Ø (verb phrase understood)*
        - *I wanted to go, but I forgot to __.*

- noun phrase ellipsis
    - *Determiner/Adjective/Possessive + Ø (noun understood)*
        - *I like the red car, not the blue __.*

- clausal ellipsis (answer ellipsis)
    - In responses, we omit everything except the essential new information. This is extremely common in conversation.
        - *"Where are you going?" - "To the store."*

- comparative ellipsis
    - In comparative sentences, we omit repeated elements after *than* or *as...as*.
        - *She is taller than I __.*

- coordination ellipsis (gapping)
    - When two clauses are joined by *and, but, or*, we can omit repeated words from the second clause. This is called **gapping**.
        - *John eats rice, and Mary __ beans.*

- bare ellipsis (stripping)
    - A more extreme form where only one word or phrase remains, often with *too, either, also, as well*.
        - *John likes pizza, and Mary too.*

- sluicing
    - We omit everything after a *wh-* word in a question when the meaning is clear.
        - *Someone called, but I don't know who __.*

- ellipsis in different contexts 
    - in lists 
    - in instructions and headlines 
    - in informal speech

## catenative verbs

- verb + bare infinitive
    - modals, *make, let, have, help*
        - *Let me **go**.*
- verb + to-infinitive
    - *want, decide, agree, expect, seem*
        - *I want **to leave**.*
- verb + object + to-infinitive
    - *tell, ask, advise, force, allow*
        - *She told me **to wait**.*
- verb + gerund
    - *enjoy, avoid, finish, admit, mind*
        - *I enjoy **swimming**.*
- verb + preposition + gerund
    - *insist on, succeed in, apologize for*
        - *He insisted **on paying**.*
- either - same meaning
    - *begin, start, continue, love, hate*
        - *I began **to cry / crying**.*
- either - different meaning
    - *remember, forget, regret, stop, try, mean*

## causative verbs

- *make* (force)
    - *make + obj + V1*
        - *She made him cry.*
    - passive: *He was made **to** work late.* (uses *to*!)
- *have* (delegate)
    - *have + obj + V1* / *have + obj + V3*
        - *I had the plumber fix it. / I had my hair cut.*
- *get* (persuade)
    - *get + obj + to + V1* / *get + obj + V3*
        - *I got him to help. / I got my car fixed.*
- *let* (allow)
    - *let + obj + V1*
        - *She let me go.*
- *help* (assist)
    - *help + obj + V1 / to + V1*
        - *She helped me move.*

## articles and nouns 

- articles  
    - *the* (definite)
        - specific, unique, previously mentioned, superlatives
    - *a / an* (indefinite)
        - non-specific singular countable
        - *a* before consonant SOUND (*a university, a one-eyed monster*)
        - *an* before vowel SOUND (*an hour, an MBA*)
    - *Ø* (zero article)
        - general statements, proper nouns, meals, transport, sports, languages
    - *some* (indefinite quantity)
        - positive statements, offers/requests
    - *any* (indefinite quantity)
        - negatives, questions; also "no matter which"

- nouns   
    - countable
        - can plural, can *a/an* (singular)
        - quantifiers: *many, few, a few, fewer*
    - uncountable (mass nouns)
        - no plural, no *a/an*
        - quantifiers: *much, little, a little, less*
    - proper
        - rarely plural, rarely *a/an*
        - usually no article
    - collective
        - depends
        - singular verb (US), singular/plural (UK)
            - *team, family, government*

- key article/noun rules
    - partitives for uncountable nouns
        - *a piece of advice, a glass of water, a loaf of bread*
    - nouns: plural form but singular meaning
        - *news, mathematics, physics, measles* (singular verb)
    - nouns: always plural
        - *scissors, trousers, jeans, glasses, clothes* (plural verb)
    - indefinite pronouns = singular
        - *Everyone is here. Each has a book.*

## pronouns and determiners 

- pronouns 
    - personal pronouns  
        - subject pronouns  
            - *I, you, he, she, it, we, they*
        - object pronouns

    - possessive pronouns  
        - *mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs*  
    - reflexive pronouns   
        - *myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves*  
    - interrogative pronouns  
        - *who* (subject, people)
        - *whom* (object, people)
        - *whose* (possessive, people)  
        - *what* (things or actions)  
        - *which* (choosing from a limited set)
    - indefinite pronouns  
        - people: *someone, somebody, anyone, anybody, no one, nobody, everyone, everybody*
        - things: *something, anything, nothing, everything*
    - dummy (expletive) pronouns
        - *there* (existence)
        - *it* (weather/time/distance, general observations, placeholder)
    - demonstrative pronouns 
        - *this, that, these, those*
    - reciprocal pronouns
        - *each other, one another*
    - relative pronouns
        - *who, whom, whose, which, that*
    - substitute pronouns
        - *one, ones*
            - *I need a pen. Do you have **one**?*
    - clause substitute
        - *so, not*
            - *I think **so**. / I hope **not**.*

- determiners  
    - articles 
        - *a, an, the* 
    - possessive determiners 
        - *my, your, his, her, its, our, their*
    - demonstrative determiners
        - *this, that, these, those*
    - quantifiers (positive) 
        - *some, a lot of, plenty of* 
    - quantifiers (negative/questions)
        - *any, much, many* 
    - quantity/proportion determiners   
        - *all, most, none, each, every, both, either, neither*
    - interrogative determiners 
        - *which, what, whose*
    - nuances determiners
        - *all*: Entire quantity or group (general, plural/uncountable)
        - *every*: Each individual member (singular, emphasizes distribution)

- little / few / less / fewer - quick guide
    - countable positive: *a few*
    - countable negative: *few*
    - countable comparative: *fewer*
    - uncountable positive: *a little*
    - uncountable negative: *little*
    - uncountable comparative: *less*
    - *less* + countable is common informally but use *fewer* in formal writing

## clause concepts 

> **relative pronouns**: *who, whom, whose, which, that*  
> **relative adverbs**: *where, when, why*  
> **subordinating conjunctions**: *because, since, although, if, when, while, after, before, so that, unless*, etc.  

- independent clause (main clause)  
    - A clause that can **stand alone** as a complete sentence because it expresses a complete thought.  

- dependent clause (subordinate clause)  
    - A clause that **cannot** stand alone as a complete sentence. It relies on an independent clause to make sense.  
        - adjective clauses (relative clauses)   
            - begin with **relative pronouns** or **relative adverbs**  
                - They answer questions like: *"Which one?"* or *"What kind?"*
        - adverb clauses (adverbial clauses)
            - begin with **subordinating conjunctions**
                - They answer questions like: *"When?" "Where?" "Why?" "How?" "To what extent?"* or *"Under what condition?"*
        - noun clauses
            - begin with words like *that, what, whatever, who, whoever, whom, whomever, which, whichever, when, where, why, how*
                - function like **nouns**. They can act as the subject, object, or complement of a sentence.

- specialized clauses 
    - participle clauses (reduced clauses)
        - function like adjectives or adverbs
            - start with a **present participle** (*-ing*) or a **past participle** (*-ed*), or *to*-infinitive
    - cleft sentences  
        - *It is/was... that/who..., What... is/was..., All... is/was...*

## relative clauses 

> **Relative pronouns:** *who, whom, whose, which, that*  
> **Relative adverbs:** *where, when, why*

- defining relative clauses (restrictive)
    - no commas, *that* OK, can omit object pronoun
        - *The book (that) I read was great.*

- non-defining relative clauses (non-restrictive)
    - commas, *that* NOT allowed, cannot omit pronoun
        - *My brother, who lives in Canada, is visiting.*

- reduced relative clauses (shorthand forms)
    - *-ing* (active)
        - *The man sitting there is my uncle.*
    - *-ed (V3)* (passive)
        - *The book written by her is famous.*
    - *to + V1* (future/superlative/ordinal)
        - *The first to arrive gets a prize.*
    - negative reduced
        - *not* + participle/infinitive
            - *Anyone not having a ticket cannot enter.*

- special cases 
    - prepositions (formal)
        - *The person to whom I spoke*
    - prepositions (informal)
        - *The person I spoke to*
    - *whose* for things
        - *The house whose roof was damaged*
    - *which* = whole clause
        - *He was late, which surprised everyone.*

## adjectives and adverbs 

> *Two syllable adjectives ending in `-y`, `-le`, `-er`, `-ow` take -er/-est (happy -> happier, simple -> simplest, clever -> cleverer, narrow -> narrowest)  
> Most others take more/most (famous -> more famous, careful -> most careful)  

> Using two comparative forms together is **incorrect** in standard English.  

> participle: `-ing` (present, active) and `-ed` (past, passive)  

- adjectives   
    - degrees of adjectives (formation)
        - comparative degree (comparing two things)
            - *than*
                - *[Comparative_Adjective] + than + [noun/pronoun]*
            - *as ... as* (equality / inequality)
                - *as + [adjective] + as*
                - *not as + [adjective] + as / less + [adjective] + than*
            - short (1 syllable): add *-er*
            - short (CVC): double consonant + *-er*
            - 2 syllables (*-y, -le, -er, -ow*): add *-er*
            - long (2+ syllables): *more + adj*
            - irregular: change form (*good -> better*)
        - superlative degree (comparing three or more things)
            - *the*
                - *the + [Superlative_Adjective]*
            - short (1 syllable): add *-est*
            - short (CVC): double consonant + *-est*
            - 2 syllables (*-y, -le, -er, -ow*): add *-est*
            - long (2+ syllables): *most + adj*
            - irregular: change form (*good -> best*)
        - never double: ~~*more better*~~, ~~*most fastest*~~
    - placement of adjectives   
        - **Before a noun**  
        - **After a linking verb**  
        - **After a pronoun**  
        - **After an object**  
    - predicative vs attributive adjectives 
        - **Attributive only** (before noun only) 
            - *main, principal, mere, sheer, utter, only, eventual, elder, indoor, outdoor*, etc. 
        - **Predicative only** (after linking verb only)
            - *asleep, awake, alive, alone, afraid, ill, well, glad, sorry, sure, fond, aware*, etc. 
    - participial adjectives 
        - **-ing**: for the cause 
        - **-ed**: for the effect 
    - repeating adjectives 
        - emphasis
            - *a long, long journey*
        - continuous state
        - figurative language
    - adjectives as nouns  
        - groups of people
            - *the rich, the poor, the unemployed*
        - concepts
    - compound adjectives  
        - ***Hyphenate* two or more words to act as a single adjective when they come *before* the noun.** (Do not hyphenate after the noun)
            - *Adjective + Participle*: *fast-moving*
            - *Noun + Participle*: *five-year-old*
            - *Adjective + Adjective*
            - *Number + Noun*
    - gradable and non-gradable (absolute) adjectives 
        - **Gradable**
            - Qualities that exist in degrees. Can be modified by *very, extremely, slightly*; have comparative/superlative forms  
                - *hot/cold, big/small, happy/sad, good/bad*
        - **Non-Gradable (Absolute)**
            - Absolute qualities
                - Cannot be modified by *very*; no comparative forms  
                    - *dead, unique, pregnant, impossible, true/false, boiling/freezing*
    - adjectives with prepositions (collocations)
        - *Adjective + Preposition*

## order of adjectives 

- OSASCOMP
    - **O**pinion (`1`) - *beautiful, nice*
    - **S**ize (`2`) - *big, small*
    - **A**ge (`3`) - *old, new*
    - **S**hape (`4`) - *round, square*
    - **C**olor (`5`) - *red, blue*
    - **O**rigin (`6`) - *French, Italian*
    - **M**aterial (`7`) - *wooden, silk*
    - **P**urpose (`8`) - *dining, sleeping*
- separating adjectives  
    - comma: `,`
        - use: between adjectives that are from the **same category**
        - don't use: between adjectives from **different categories**
    - `and`

- adverbs  
    - formation    
        - by rules 
            - Add *-ly* to adjective
            - Adjective ending in *-y* -> change *y* to *i* + *-ly*
            - Adjectives ending in *-able, -ible, -le, -ic* -> add *-y* or *-ally*
        - irregular 
    - placement of adverbs
        - **Before the main verb**
        - **After the verb (no object)**
        - **After the object**
        - **Between auxiliary and main verb**
        - **Before an adjective**
        - **Before another adverb**
    - adverb types
        - manner (answer "How?")
            - *quickly, carefully*
        - frequency (answer "How often?")
            - *always*: `100%`, *usually / generally*: `~90%`, *often / frequently*: `~70%`, *sometimes / occasionally*: `~50%`, *rarely / seldom / hardly ever*: `~10%`, *never*: `~0%`
        - time (answer "When?")
            - *now, then, today, yesterday, tomorrow, soon, later, immediately, recently, already, still, yet*
        - place (answer "Where?")
            - *here, there, everywhere, nowhere, inside, outside, upstairs, downstairs, abroad, nearby*
        - purpose/reason (answer "Why?")
            - *therefore, thus, consequently, hence, so, otherwise*
        - certainty (answer "How sure?")
            - *certainly / definitely, clearly / obviously, undoubtedly, probably, maybe / perhaps*
        - viewpoint (answer "From what perspective?")
            - *personally, generally, technically, theoretically, financially, scientifically, morally*
        - focusing (limit or emphasize)
            - *only, even, also, just, especially, particularly, mainly, primarily*
        - interrogative (ask questions)
            - *when, where, why, how*
        - relative (connect clauses)
            - *when, where, why*
        - degree (intensifiers and mitigators)
            - intensifiers (stronger): *so, too, very, really, extremely, incredibly*
                - *too*: often followed by an infinitive (*too heavy to lift*)
            - mitigators (weaker): *enough, quite, pretty, rather, fairly*
                - *enough*: comes after the adjective/adverb (*tall enough*) and before noun (*enough money*)

- still / yet / already / any more
    - *still*: continues, pos/neg/Q, mid position
    - *yet*: expected but hasn't happened, neg/Q, end position (mid is formal)
    - *already*: happened earlier than expected, pos/Q (surprise), mid position
    - *any more*: stopped; no longer true, neg (*not... any more*), end position
    - *no longer*: stopped (formal), positive form (but neg meaning), mid position

- even - position = meaning
    - before subject --> subject focus: *Even John helped.*
    - before main verb --> action focus: *John even helped.*
    - before object --> object focus: *John helped even me.*
    - before comparative --> degree: *even better, even more tired*
    - *not even* --> negative emphasis: *He didn't even call.*

- too / enough - quick rules
    - *too* before adj/adv: *too hot, too quickly*
    - *enough* after adj/adv: *tall enough, fast enough*
    - *enough* before nouns: *enough money*
    - *too much* + uncountable: *too much water*
    - *too many* + plural countable: *too many people*
    - *too... to* (negative result): *too heavy to lift*
    - *enough... to* (positive result): *light enough to lift*
    - *not... enough... to* (negative): *not light enough to lift*

- so / such - quick decision
    - adjective alone (no noun): *so* - *so tired*
    - adverb alone: *so* - *so quickly*
    - *much/many* + noun: *so* - *so much water, so many people*
    - *a/an* + (adj) + singular noun: *such* - *such a good idea*
    - plural/uncountable noun: *such* - *such wonderful people, such patience*

## comparative structures 

- types of comparison   
    - **Comparative**: Compares two things
        - *adjective/adverb-er + than*
        - *more + adjective/adverb + than*
    - **Superlative**: Compares three or more things 
        - *the + adjective/adverb-est*
        - *the most + adjective/adverb*
    - **Equality**: Shows two things are equal
        - *as + adjective/adverb + as* 
        - negative equality: *not as/so + adjective/adverb + as*
        - equality quantity: *as much/many... as*
    - **Inequality**: Shows two things are not equal
        - *less + adjective/adverb + than* 
    - **Proportional**: Shows a relationship between two changes 
        - *the + comparative..., the + comparative...*

- repeated comparative
    - *comparative and comparative*
        - *colder and colder*

- quantity comparison
    - *more/fewer/less + noun + than*

- modifying comparatives
    - *much / far / a lot* --> much more: *much taller*
    - *a little / a bit / slightly* --> slightly more: *a little colder*
    - *even* --> emphasizes difference: *even faster*
    - *no* --> equal in negative sense: *no richer than*

- comparing quantities (verb phrases)
    - use *than* with a clause or a reduced clause.

- special comparative patterns 
    - *No sooner... than...*
        - *No sooner had I left than it started raining.*
    - *the same + noun + as*
    - *Different from / to / than*
    - *like/unlike + noun* (similarity and contrast)
        - *Like his father, he is a doctor. / Unlike her sister, she loves coffee.*

## order of adverbs

- multiple adverbs: MPT rule
    - manner (How?) -> place (Where?) -> time (When?)
        - *She worked **carefully** (M) **in the lab** (P) **yesterday** (T).*
        - *They arrived **safely** (M) **in Paris** (P) **last night** (T).*

- multiple adverbs of same type
    - time: specific -> general
        - *at 3:00 on Monday in July*
    - place: small -> large
        - *in a flat in Marylebone, London*

- exceptions to MPT
    - time first:
        - for emphasis or contrast
            - ***Yesterday** I met an old friend.*
        - time contrasts with another time
            - ***Last year** we went to France; **this year** we're going to Italy.*
    - place first:
        - with movement verbs (place often comes before manner/means)
            - *She went **to London** by train.*

- key rule
    - don't separate the verb from its object
        - ✓ *I read **the book** yesterday.*
        - ✗ *I read yesterday **the book**.*

## conjunctions 

- **Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS)**: Connect equal elements
    - *For*: Reason (similar to *because*)
    - *And*: Addition
    - *Nor*: Negative addition (requires inversion)
    - *But*: Contrast
    - *Or*: Alternative / choice
    - *Yet*: Surprising contrast
    - *So*: Result / consequence

- **Subordinating Conjunctions**: Connect dependent to independent clause
    - [time]
        - *when, whenever, while, as, before, after, since, until / till*
    - [cause/reason]
        - *because, since, as*
    - [condition]
        - *if, unless, provided (that), providing (that), as long as, in case*
    - [contrast/concession]
        - *although, though, even though, while, whereas* 
    - [purpose] 
        - *so that, in order that*
    - [manner] 
        - *as, as if, as though, like*

- **Adversative Conjunctions**: Show contrast/opposition  
    - *but*: Most common adversative conjunction  
    - *however*: Contrast (can begin a sentence or follow a semicolon)  
    - *yet*: Surprising contrast 
    - *although / though / even though*: Introduce subordinate clause (contrast despite the clause)
    - *whereas*: Compare/contrast differences
    - *despite / in spite of*: Prepositions showing contrast (followed by noun/gerund) 

- although / despite / in spite of - quick rule
    - subject + verb (clause) --> *although / though / even though*
    - noun / gerund / *the fact that* --> *despite / in spite of*
    - *Despite* has NO *of*. *In spite* MUST have *of*.

- **Conjunctive Adverbs (Transitional Expressions)**: Connect sentences/clauses (with comma)
    - [contrast] 
        - *however, nevertheless, nonetheless*
    - [result/consequence]
        - *therefore, consequently, thus, hence*
    - [addition]
        - *moreover, furthermore, also, in addition, besides*
    - [sequence/time]
        - *then, next, afterward(s), finally, first, second*
    - [emphasis]
        - *indeed*

- **Special Conjunctions and Connectors**
    - *in case*: Condition (preparation)
    - *as long as*: Condition
    - *provided (that)*: Condition  
    - *during*: Preposition (period of time) + noun
    - *for*: Preposition (duration) + period of time
    - *by*: Deadline / latest point
    - *until / till*: Duration up to a point
    - *by the time*: Point in time when something is completed

## discourse markers 

- **Adding Information**
    - *and, also, furthermore, moreover, in addition, besides, as well as*
- **Contrasting Ideas**
    - *but, however, nevertheless, nonetheless, although / though, even though, whereas, while, on the other hand, in contrast, conversely*
- **Showing Cause and Effect / Result**
    - *so, therefore, consequently, thus, hence, as a result, because of this*
- **Ordering and Sequencing**
    - *first / firstly / first of all, second / secondly, third / thirdly, next, then, after that, finally / lastly, to begin with, in the first place*
- **Giving Opinions**
    - *in my opinion, I think, I believe, it seems to me, from my perspective, personally, to be honest, if you ask me, as far as I'm concerned*
- **Expressing Certainty or Doubt**
    - certainty
        - *certainly, definitely, undoubtedly, of course, without a doubt*
    - doubt
        - *perhaps, maybe, probably, possibly, it depends*
- **Clarifying and Explaining**
    - *in other words, that is, namely, i.e., e.g., to put it differently, to clarify*
- **Giving Examples**
    - *for example, for instance, such as, including, like* (informal)
- **Summarizing and Concluding**
    - *in conclusion, to sum up, in summary, overall, in short, in a nutshell, finally, ultimately*
- **Managing Conversation (Spoken Discourse Markers)**
    - *well*: Hesitation, thinking, or introducing a response
    - *so*: Drawing a conclusion or changing topic
    - *okay / alright*: Agreement, transition, or checking understanding
    - *right*: Acknowledgment or agreement
    - *now*: Introducing a new topic or step 
    - *actually*: Correcting or introducing new information
    - *anyway*: Returning to the main topic or ending a topic
    - *basically*: Summarizing or simplifying
    - *honestly*: Emphasizing sincerity
    - *frankly*: Being direct or blunt 
    - *you know*: Filler, assuming shared knowledge
    - *I mean*: Clarifying or rephrasing
    - *look* / *listen*: Getting attention or expressing frustration

- punctuation with discourse markers
    - at beginning of sentence: usually followed by a comma
    - in the middle of a sentence: set off by commas on both sides 
    - at end of sentence: preceded by a comma

- formal vs. informal discourse markers
    - *furthermore, moreover* [vs.] *and, also, plus*
    - *nevertheless, nonetheless* [vs.] *but, though, still*
    - *consequently, therefore, thus* [vs.] *so, that's why*
    - *in conclusion, to summarize* [vs.] *anyway, so, basically* 
    - *however* [vs.] *but, though*
    - *in my opinion* [vs.] *I think, honestly*

## correlative conjunctions

- pairs
    - *both... and...* --> inclusion, always plural verb
    - *either... or...* --> choice, verb agrees with closest subject
    - *neither... nor...* --> negative choice, verb agrees with closest subject
    - *not only... but also...* --> emphatic addition, verb agrees with closest subject
    - *whether... or...* --> alternatives
    - *rather... than...* --> preference
    - *no sooner... than...* --> immediate sequence
    - *the more... the more...* --> proportional, two clauses
    - *as... as...* --> equality
    - *such/so... that...* --> result
- key rules
    - parallel structure: both sides must match
        - *She is both **smart** and **kind**.*
    - either/neither position: place directly before the first contrasted element
    - verb agreement: with *either/or* and *neither/nor*, verb agrees with closest subject
        - *Neither the manager nor the employees **were** informed.*
    - inversion: *not only* at the start requires inversion
        - *Not only **did she win**, but she also broke the record.*

    - Verb + [Preposition]

        - (verb) + *about*: talk, think, worry, ask, dream
        - (verb) + *for*: wait, look, ask, hope, search, apply, care, pay
        - (verb) + *of*: hear, dream, think, consist, approve, die
        - (verb) + *from*: hear, come, suffer, protect, recover, differ
        - (verb) + *on*: depend, work, comment, focus, rely, insist
        - (verb) + *in*: believe, succeed, result, participate, invest
        - (verb) + *with*: agree, deal, provide, compare
    - Adjective + [Preposition]
        - (adj) + *of*
        - (adj) + *for*
        - (adj) + *from*
        - (adj) + *on*
        - (adj) + *in*
        - (adj) + *at*
        - (adj) + *with*
        - (adj) + *about*
    - Noun + [Preposition]
        - (noun) + *for / of*
        - (noun) + *of*
        - (noun) + *in*
        - (noun) + *between / among*
        - (noun) + *to*

- verbs with no preposition
    - *discuss, enter, resemble, lack, request, marry*

- types of relationships shown by prepositions 
    - **Time**: When something happens  
        - *at*: Specific clock times, mealtimes, holidays (without 'day') 
        - *on*: Specific days and dates
        - *in*: Longer periods: months, seasons, years, decades, centuries, parts of the day (except night)
        - *since*: Starting point in the past (with present/past perfect) 
        - *for*: Duration (period of time) 
        - *during*: Within a specific period 
        - *before*: Earlier than a specific time/event 
        - *after*: Later than a specific time/event
        - *until / till*: Duration up to a specific point 
        - idiomatic phrases for time
            - *on time*: Exactly at the scheduled time; punctual 
            - *in time*: With enough time to spare before a deadline 
            - *at the end (of)*: The final part of a period 
            - *in the end*: Finally, after a long process or difficulty 
    - **Place/Location**: Where something is  
        - *in*: Enclosed spaces; larger areas (cities, countries, rooms)
        - *on*: Surfaces; specific locations on a line (streets, rivers)
        - *at*: Specific points or locations
        - *to*: Direction towards a destination
        - *into*: Movement from outside to inside 
        - *over*: Movement above something; covering something
        - *under*: Below something
        - *between*: Two people, things, or points
        - *among*: Three or more people, things, or points 
        - *through*: Movement from one side to the other; passing within something
        - *across*: Movement from one side to the other (of a surface)
    - **Direction/Movement**: Where something is going 
        - *to, into, onto, through, across, over, under, around, towards*
    - **Manner**: How something is done 
        - *with, by, like, without, in (a way/manner)*
    - **Cause/Reason**: Why something happened 
        - *from, because of, due to, through*
    - **Purpose**: What something is used for
        - *for*
    - **Possession/Association**: Ownership or connection  
        - *of*
    - **Agent (Passive Voice)**: Who performs the action  
        - *by*
            - Method/Means
            - Agent (Passive voice)
            - Proximity
            - Deadline
            - Measurement
    - **Numeric and Quantifier Expressions**
        - *out of*
        - *in*
        - *up to*
        - *as high as / as many as*
        - *on average*

- Fixed Idiomatic Prepositional Phrases
    - *on purpose*: Intentionally
    - *by accident*: Unintentionally
    - *under control*: Managed properly
    - *out of control*: Unmanageable 
    - *in charge of*: Responsible for
    - *in trouble*: Facing difficulty
    - *on fire*: Burning
    - *under pressure*: Stressed or forced
    - *in pain*: Suffering physically
    - *out of stock*: Not available for sale 

- Ending a Sentence with a Preposition (Stranding)
    - **Informal / Neutral**: Preposition at the end
        - *Who did you go with?*
    - **Formal**: Preposition before relative pronoun
        - *With whom did you go?*

- Prepositions in Questions and Relative Clauses
    - Question (informal): *Who did you go with?*
    - Question (formal): *With whom did you go?*
    - Relative clause (informal): *the book I was looking for*

## phrasal verbs (idiomatic expressions)

- phrasal verb types
    - *Verb + Adverb*: to quit (*give up*)
    - *Verb + Preposition*: to take care of (*look after*)
    - *Verb + Adverb + Preposition*: to tolerate (*put up with*)
- transitive phrasal verbs (`require a direct object`)
    - **Separable** (object can come after particle or between verb and particle)
    - **With pronoun** (pronoun object **must** come between verb and particle)
    - **Inseparable** (object must always come after particle)
- intransitive phrasal verbs (`no object`)

- key points
    1. **Meaning is often idiomatic** - you cannot guess it from the individual words.
        - *give up* ≠ *give* + *up* (it means "quit")
    2. **One phrasal verb can have multiple meanings** depending on context.
        - *take off*: remove clothing / plane departs / become successful
    3. **Separable vs. inseparable** - know which type you are using.
        - separable: *turn off the light* / *turn the light off*
        - inseparable: *look after the baby* (cannot separate)
    4. **Pronouns must go between** the verb and particle in separable phrasal verbs.
        - Correct: *Turn **it** off.*
        - Incorrect: *Turn off **it**.*
    5. **Three-word phrasal verbs** (verb + adverb + preposition) are always inseparable.
        - *put up with* (tolerate): *I can't **put up with** this noise.*

- common phrasal verbs by particle 
    - *In* / *Out*
        - *give in*: to surrender, yield 
        - *log in / sign in*: to enter a system (username/password) 
        - *take in*: to deceive; to understand; to make clothing smaller
        - *find out*: to discover information
        - *go out*: to leave home socially; to stop burning
        - *work out*: to exercise; to solve; to develop successfully
        - *figure out*: to understand or solve
        - *call out*: to shout loudly; to summon 
        - *point out*: to draw attention to something 
    - *On* / *Off*
        - *put on*: to dress; to gain weight; to activate 
        - *carry on*: to continue
        - *turn on*: to activate a device
        - *get on / get along (with)*: to have a good relationship 
        - *take on*: to accept responsibility; to employ 
        - *take off*: to remove clothing; to leave the ground; to become successful 
        - *turn off*: to deactivate a device
        - *get off*: to leave a bus, train, or plane 
        - *put off*: to postpone 
        - *go off*: to stop working (alarm); to explode; to become bad (food)
    - *Up* / *Down*
        - *grow up*: to mature into an adult 
        - *make up*: to invent; to reconcile; to apply cosmetics
        - *look up*: to search for information
        - *give up*: to stop trying; to quit 
        - *clean up*: to make a place tidy
        - *show up*: to arrive or appear 
        - *write down*: to record something on paper 
        - *slow down*: to reduce speed 
        - *calm down*: to become less angry or excited
        - *break down*: to stop functioning (machine); to become emotionally distressed 
    - *Away* / *Back*
        - *throw away*: to discard something 
        - *go away*: to leave a place 
        - *put away*: to store something in its proper place
        - *take away*: to remove; to subtract 
        - *come back*: to return to a place
        - *give back*: to return something to its owner
        - *take back*: to return to a store; to retract a statement
        - *get back*: to return; to receive something again
        - *put back*: to return something to where it was

## register and formality 

- main registers 
    - **Formal** (Academic, Professional)
        - Academic papers, business letters, official documents, speeches
        - no contractions, passive common, phrasal verbs avoided, long complex sentences, Latinate vocabulary
    - **Neutral** (Standard, Semi-formal)
        - Most everyday writing, news articles, work emails, instructions
        - optional contractions, occasional passive, some phrasal verbs, medium sentences, standard vocabulary
    - **Informal** (Casual, Colloquial)
        - Conversations with friends, text messages, personal emails, social media
        - contractions yes, passive rare, phrasal verbs common, short sentences, colloquial vocabulary

- email closings
    - formal: *Yours faithfully, / Yours sincerely, / Respectfully,*
    - neutral: *Best regards, / Kind regards, / Sincerely,*
    - informal: *Best, / Cheers, / Thanks, / Talk soon,*

## hedges and softening language 

- types
    - **`Modal verbs`**: *may, might, could, would, should*
    - **`Verbs` of Opinion and Tentativeness**: *think, believe, suppose, seem, appear, tend to*
    - **`Adverbs` of Probability and Degree**: *probably, possibly, perhaps, maybe, apparently, roughly*
    - **`Adjectives` of Tentativeness**: *possible, probable, likely, unlikely, doubtful*
    - **`Nouns` of Tentativeness**: *possibility, probability, likelihood, tendency*
    - **`Approximators` (Vague Language)**: *about, around, sort of, kind of, a little, a bit*
    - **`Introductory phrases` (Parenthetical Hedges)**: *I think, In my opinion, As far as I know, It seems to me*
    - **`Tag questions`**: *isn't it?, don't you?, shouldn't we?*

- hedging strength scale (weakest -> strongest)
    - *might / possibly / maybe* -> *could / perhaps* -> *may / likely* -> *probably / should* -> *certainly / definitely*

## academic grammar

- nominalisation (making nouns from verbs/adjectives)
    - verb -> noun: *decrease -> decrease, develop -> development, analyse -> analysis*
    - adjective -> noun: *significant -> significance, available -> availability, different -> difference*
    - *The number decreased rapidly* -> *a rapid decrease* (more academic)

- complex prepositions
    - *with reference to*: regarding
    - *as a result of*: because of
    - *prior to*: before
    - *in the case of*: regarding
    - *irrespective of*: regardless of
    - *by means of*: using
    - *in light of*: considering

- expressing opinions impersonally (it-clauses)
    - opinion: *It + be + adj + to-infinitive*: *It is difficult to evaluate...*
    - opinion: *It + be + adj + that-clause*: *It is possible that...*
    - reporting others: *It + passive verb + that*: *It has been reported that...*
    - distancing: *It + passive + that... but/however...*: *It has been claimed that X... but recent studies show Y...*

- disagreeing politely (acknowledge -> refute)
    - *It is sometimes argued that... **While it is quite correct that**..., **it must also be noted that**...*

- linking ideas in academic writing
    - sentence connectors: *Furthermore, In sum, However, Firstly*
    - conjunctions: *in that, given that, whereas, albeit*
    - pro-forms: *the former, the latter, as such, these*

- referring to other work
    - *as + past participle*: ***As noted** by Kerr (2004)...*
    - *as + is/has been + V3*: *...as **has been shown** in recent studies*
    - referring to figures: ***As shown** in Figure 2...*
    - referring to sections (written): ***As discussed above**...*
    - referring to sections (spoken): ***As I said before**... / **Coming back to**...*

- lead-in phrases for academic discussion
    - cautious opinion: *I would think that..., I shouldn't have thought that...*
    - inviting opinions: *Why do you think that...?, How do you recommend we...?*
    - clarifying: *What I mean is that..., What I'm saying is that...*
    - challenging: *Then again..., I take your point, but..., That's not to say...*
    - speculating: *I'd have thought..., I imagine...*
    - introducing a problem: *The problem is that..., For one thing...*
    - asking for confirmation: *So you mean that...?, So what you're saying is...?*

## semantics

- lexical relations
    - synonymy: same meaning
        - *big/large*
    - antonymy: opposite meaning
        - *hot/cold* (gradable), *alive/dead* (complementary)
    - hyponymy: "is a type of"
        - *poodle -> dog -> animal*
    - meronymy: "is a part of"
        - *engine -> car*
    - polysemy: one word, related meanings
        - *head* (body/leader/top)
    - homonymy: different words, same form
        - *bank* (financial/river)
- thematic roles
    - agent: deliberate doer
        - ***She** opened the door.*
    - patient: entity acted upon
        - *She opened **the door**.*
    - experiencer: feels/perceives
        - ***I** felt happy.*
    - instrument: tool/means
        - *He cut it **with a knife**.*
    - goal: endpoint
        - *She walked **to the store**.*
    - source: origin
        - *She came **from Paris**.*
    - beneficiary: benefits
        - *He baked it **for her**.*
- denotation vs. connotation
    - *slender* --> positive
    - *thin* --> neutral
    - *skinny* --> negative
- entailment vs. presupposition
    - entailment: does NOT survive negation
    - presupposition: survives negation
        - *She stopped smoking.* --> entails: she doesn't smoke now | presupposes: she used to smoke
        - *She didn't stop smoking.* --> entails: she smokes now | presupposes: she used to smoke

## common errors 

- *I am knowing* -> *I know* (stative verb)
- *If I will see* -> *If I see* (no *will* in *if*-clause)
- *Despite it was raining* -> *Although it was raining* / *Despite the rain*
- *more better* -> *better* (no double comparative)
- *He is enough tall* -> *He is tall enough*
- *I suggested to go* -> *I suggested going*
- *She said me* -> *She told me*
- *Can you explain me?* -> *Can you explain it to me?*
- *I don't have no money* -> *I don't have any money* / *I have no money*
- *I look forward to see you* -> *I look forward to seeing you*
- *He is used to work late* -> *He is used to working late*
- *10 items or less* (formal) -> *10 items or fewer*
- *I wish I was rich* (formal) -> *I wish I were rich*
- *I suggest that he goes* -> *I suggest that he go* (subjunctive)
- *She drove careless* -> *She drove carelessly*
- *The thing what I saw* -> *The thing that/which I saw*
- *I didn't do nothing* -> *I didn't do anything* / *I did nothing*
- *Discuss about the problem* -> *Discuss the problem* (no preposition)
- *I gave to her a book* -> *I gave her a book* / *I gave a book to her*
