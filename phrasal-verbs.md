## 22. Phrasal Verbs (Idiomatic Expressions)

Phrasal verbs are a combination of a main verb and one or more **particles** (adverbs or prepositions). These particles significantly alter the meaning of the base verb, often creating idiomatic expressions that cannot be understood by simply combining the meanings of the individual words. They are a crucial and common feature of English, especially in informal speech and writing.

> **Note:** Phrasal verbs are sometimes called **multi-word verbs**.

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 21. Prepositions](./prefixes-suffixes.md)*.

---

### A. Structure of Phrasal Verbs

A phrasal verb can consist of:

| Structure | Example | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| *Verb + Adverb* | *give up* | to quit |
| *Verb + Preposition* | *look after* | to take care of |
| *Verb + Adverb + Preposition* | *put up with* | to tolerate |

---

### B. Transitive vs. Intransitive Phrasal Verbs

#### 1. Transitive Phrasal Verbs (Require a Direct Object)

These verbs need an object to complete their meaning.

| Feature | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Separable** (object can come after particle or between verb and particle) | *Turn **off** the light.* / *Turn the light **off**.* |
| **With pronoun** (pronoun object **must** come between verb and particle) | *Turn **it off**.* (Not: *Turn off it*) |
| **Inseparable** (object must always come after particle) | *Look **after** the baby.* (Not: *Look the baby after*) |

#### 2. Intransitive Phrasal Verbs (No Object)

These verbs do not take an object.

| Example |
|---------|
| *I **wake up** at 7 AM.* |
| *The plane **took off**.* |

### C. Common Phrasal Verbs by Particle

#### 1. *In* / *Out*

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| *give in* | to surrender, yield | *He refused to **give in** to their demands.* |
| *log in / sign in* | to enter a system (username/password) | *Please **log in** to your account.* |
| *take in* | to deceive; to understand; to make clothing smaller | *Don't let him **take you in**.* / *I need to **take in** all the information.* / *I need to **take in** this dress.* |
| *find out* | to discover information | *Can you **find out** what time the movie starts?* |
| *go out* | to leave home socially; to stop burning | *Let's **go out** for dinner.* / *The candle **went out**.* |
| *work out* | to exercise; to solve; to develop successfully | *I **work out** three times a week.* / *We need to **work out** a solution.* / *I hope things **work out**.* |
| *figure out* | to understand or solve | *I can't **figure out** how to assemble this.* |
| *call out* | to shout loudly; to summon | *He **called out** my name.* |
| *point out* | to draw attention to something | *She **pointed out** the mistake.* |

| *carry on* | to continue | *Please **carry on** with your work.* |
| *turn on* | to activate a device | ***Turn on** the lights.* |

| *take off* | to remove clothing; to leave the ground; to become successful | ***Take off** your shoes.* / *The plane **took off**.* / *Her career **took off**.* |

### E. Important Notes on Phrasal Verbs

1. **Meaning is often idiomatic** - you cannot guess it from the individual words.
   - *give up* ≠ *give* + *up* (it means "quit")

2. **One phrasal verb can have multiple meanings** depending on context.
   - *take off*: remove clothing / plane departs / become successful

3. **Separable vs. inseparable** - know which type you are using.
   - Separable: *turn off the light* / *turn the light off*
   - Inseparable: *look after the baby* (cannot separate)

4. **Pronouns must go between** the verb and particle in separable phrasal verbs.
   - Correct: *Turn **it** off.*
   - Incorrect: *Turn off **it**.*

5. **Three-word phrasal verbs** (verb + adverb + preposition) are always inseparable.
   - *put up with* (tolerate): *I can't **put up with** this noise.*

### F. Prepositions in Phrasal Verbs vs. Prepositional Verbs

Not every word that looks like a preposition functions the same way. A key distinction exists between **prepositional verbs** (true prepositions) and **phrasal verbs** (particles).

| Type | Structure | Meaning | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Prepositional Verb** | Verb + Preposition (literal) | Object is needed; meaning is transparent | *She is looking **at** the painting.* (at = direction of gaze) |
| **Phrasal Verb** | Verb + Particle (changes meaning) | Often idiomatic; meaning cannot be guessed | *She gave **up** smoking.* (gave up = quit, not "give" + "up") |

**Quick Test:** If you can replace the verb+preposition with a single verb without changing meaning, it's likely a prepositional verb. If not, it's a phrasal verb.

- *He turned **off** the road.* (prepositional: left the road)
- *He turned **off** the lights.* (phrasal: deactivated)
