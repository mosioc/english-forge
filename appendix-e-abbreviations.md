## Appendix E: Abbreviations

Abbreviations are shortened forms of words or phrases. They are used to save space, time, and effort in writing and speaking. Understanding common abbreviations is essential for reading and writing in English, especially in formal, academic, and professional contexts.

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 3. Short Forms (Contractions)](./short-forms-contractions.md)* for contracted verb forms (e.g., *don't, I'm*) and *[Appendix A: American vs. British English](./appendix-a-american-british-english.md)* for regional variations.

---

### A. Types of Abbreviations

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Acronym** | Pronounced as a word | *NASA, UNESCO, AIDS* |
| **Initialism** | Each letter pronounced separately | *FBI, CEO, ATM, DVD* |
| **Contraction** | Shortened by omitting letters (with apostrophe) | *don't, I'm, won't, can't* |
| **Clipping** | Part of the word is dropped | *phone (telephone), ad (advertisement)* |
| **Shortening** | First few letters of a word | *Jan. (January), Mon. (Monday)* |
| **Symbol/Sign** | Non-letter character representing a word | *& (and), @ (at), % (percent)* |

> **Note:** This section focuses on **acronyms, initialisms, clippings, and shortenings**. Contractions are covered in *Section 3. Short Forms*.

### D. Common Latin Abbreviations

These are frequently used in academic and formal writing.

| Abbreviation | Latin | Meaning | Example |
|--------------|-------|---------|---------|
| *e.g.* | *exempli gratia* | for example | *Citrus fruits, **e.g.**, oranges and lemons, are rich in vitamin C.* |
| *i.e.* | *id est* | that is / in other words | *He is a polyglot, **i.e.**, he speaks several languages.* |
| *etc.* | *et cetera* | and so on / and other things | *Please bring pens, paper, **etc.*** |
| *et al.* | *et alii* | and others (people) | *The study by Smith **et al.** (2020) shows...* |
| *cf.* | *confer* | compare | *See **cf.** Figure 2 for a different result.* |
| *vs.* | *versus* | against / as opposed to | *The **vs.** debate continues.* |
| *viz.* | *videlicet* | namely / that is to say | *Three authors, **viz.**, Smith, Jones, and Brown, contributed.* |
| *N.B.* | *nota bene* | note well / take note | ***N.B.** The deadline is Friday.* |
| *ca.* | *circa* | approximately (used with dates) | *The artifact dates from **ca.** 500 BCE.* |

> **Important Distinction:** *e.g.* gives **examples**. *i.e.* gives a **clarification or restatement**.
> - *I like citrus fruits, **e.g.**, oranges and lemons.* (Examples)
> - *I like citrus fruits, **i.e.**, fruits from the genus Citrus.* (Definition)

### O. Rules for Using Abbreviations

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Introduce the full form on first use** (in formal writing) | *The **Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)** released a statement. The **FBI** is investigating.* |
| **Use a period after some abbreviations** (especially Latin and titles) | *Mr., Dr., e.g., i.e., etc.* |
| **Do not use periods for acronyms and initialisms** (modern style) | *NASA, FBI, CEO* (not *N.A.S.A.* or *F.B.I.*) |
| **Do not use abbreviations in formal writing** (except standard ones) | Avoid *info, ad, phone* in academic papers |
| **Be consistent** | If you use *Jan.* for January, use *Feb.* for February |
| **Do not use *etc.* with *e.g.*** (redundant) | *Citrus fruits, e.g., oranges, lemons* (not *e.g., oranges, lemons, etc.*) |
| **Do not use *i.e.* and *e.g.* interchangeably** | *e.g.* = examples; *i.e.* = clarification |

### P. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| *e.g.* used instead of *i.e.* | *He is a polyglot, **i.e.**, he speaks many languages.* (clarification) |
| *i.e.* used instead of *e.g.* | *I like citrus fruits, **e.g.**, oranges and lemons.* (examples) |
| *ect.* instead of *etc.* | *Please bring pens, paper, **etc.*** |
| *a.k.a.* written without periods (acceptable) | *aka* or *a.k.a.* (both common) |
| Using *etc.* with a list that ends with *and* | *Apples, oranges, **and** bananas* (not *...and bananas, etc.*) |
| Using abbreviations without introducing them | *The **CDC** said...* (first use: *The **Centers for Disease Control (CDC)** said...*) |
| Using text abbreviations in formal writing | *The meeting is **ASAP**.* → *The meeting is **as soon as possible**.* |
| *vs.* written as *vs* (missing period) | *The **vs.** debate* (period is standard) |

### R. Formal vs. Informal Abbreviations

| Formal (Acceptable in Academic/Business Writing) | Informal (Avoid in Formal Writing) |
|--------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| *e.g., i.e., etc., et al.* | *LOL, OMG, BTW, FYI* (FYI is borderline acceptable in business) |
| *Mr., Mrs., Ms., Dr., Prof.* | *info, ad, phone, bike* |
| *Jan., Feb., Mon., Tue.* (in tables/calendars) | *ASAP* (acceptable in business email, not academic) |
| *AM, PM, BC, AD* | *c.* (acceptable in some academic contexts) |
| *CEO, CFO, HR, MBA* (after introduction) | *TBA, TBD* (acceptable in informal business) |
| *N/A, PTO, EOD, COB* (internal business) | *Rizz, Cap, Sus, Slay, GOAT* (internet slang) |
