## 26. Grammar in Academic Contexts

Academic English has specific grammatical features that differ from everyday spoken English. This section covers the key structures used in academic writing and speaking: complex noun phrases, nominalisation, complex prepositions, impersonal opinion structures (*it*-clauses), linking devices, referencing other work, and lead-in phrases for discussion.

### A. Complex Noun Phrases

Long, complex noun phrases are typical of academic writing, allowing a lot of information to be included within one sentence element. They function as subjects, objects, and complements.

> **Example:** *The **changes** in map data collection and representation that have arisen with the introduction of computers and digital techniques* are remarkable.

#### Word Order in Complex Noun Phrases

| Position | Common Elements | Example |
|----------|----------------|---------|
| **Before** the main noun | Determiners (*a, the, this*), adjectives, nouns | *orbiting* satellites / *rapid* changes |
| **After** the main noun | Relative clauses, prepositional phrases, participle phrases | satellites *that send data to Earth*, information *from surveys* |

> **Note:** Elements after the main noun are often embedded within others (e.g., a relative clause containing a prepositional phrase).

**More examples:**
- *The **theory** of relativity that Einstein developed in the early 20th century* revolutionized physics.
- *The **increase** in global temperatures observed over the past century* is unprecedented.
- *Researchers **studying** the effects of climate change on coastal regions* have reported alarming findings.

### B. Nominalisation

Nominalisation is using a noun to convey a meaning usually expressed with another word class (verb or adjective). It is a key feature of academic writing.

> **Definition:** Turning verbs or adjectives into nouns to make writing more formal and concise.

#### Verb/Noun Pairs

| Verb | Noun | Adjective | Noun |
|------|------|-----------|------|
| *decrease* | *decrease* | *available* | *availability* |
| *increase* | *increase* | *significant* | *significance* |
| *develop* | *development* | *different* | *difference* |
| *analyze* | *analysis* | *frequent* | *frequency* |
| *compare* | *comparison* | *probable* | *probability* |

#### Examples: Less Academic vs. More Academic

| Less Academic (Verb/Adjective) | More Academic (Noun/Nominalisation) |
|-------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| *the number of people volunteering has decreased rapidly* | *an extremely rapid **decrease** in volunteering* |
| *more opportunities have become available* | the **availability** of opportunities |
| *the economy has grown significantly* | **significant growth** in the economy |
| *the results are different* | a **difference** in the results |
| *the data is very significant* | the **significance** of the data |

#### How to Nominalise

| Original | Nominalised Form |
|----------|------------------|
| *Researchers **analyzed** the data.* | *The **analysis** of the data was conducted by researchers.* |
| *The two groups **differed** significantly.* | *A significant **difference** was observed between the two groups.* |
| *The population **increased** rapidly.* | *The rapid **increase** in population was unexpected.* |

> **Note:** While nominalisation is common in academic writing, overuse can make writing unnecessarily heavy. Use it strategically.

### C. Complex Prepositions

Complex prepositions are prepositions comprising two, three, or four words. They are common in academic writing.

| Complex Preposition | Meaning | Example |
|--------------------|---------|---------|
| *as for* | regarding | ***As for** the future, demand for electricity is expected to grow.* |
| *with reference to* | what it is about | *...inequalities in health **with reference to** race and gender.* |
| *with the exception of* | not including | *...higher than average **with the exception of** June.* |
| *as a result of* | because of | *Flooding occurred **as a result of** heavy rain.* |
| *as distinct from / as opposed to* | in contrast to | *...qualitative methods, **as opposed to** quantitative ones.* |
| *depending on* | conditional on | *The results vary **depending on** the sample size.* |
| *in comparison with / in line with* | compared to / consistent with | *...**in line with** previous findings.* |
| *in the case of* | regarding a specific instance | *...**in the case of** elderly patients.* |
| *irrespective of / regardless of* | without considering | *...**irrespective of** age or gender.* |
| *on the part of* | done by | *a lack of awareness **on the part of** the participants* |
| *prior to* | before | *...**prior to** the start of the experiment.* |
| *in light of* | considering | *...**in light of** recent evidence.* |
| *by means of* | using | *...**by means of** statistical analysis.* |

> **Note:** Four-word prepositions usually end in ***of*** and include an article (e.g., *as a result of*, *on the part of*).

#### Complex Prepositions in Complex Noun Phrases

| Example |
|---------|
| *class identity **with reference to** gender differences* |
| *a less positive attitude **in the case of** customers* |
| *the increase **as a result of** the new policy* |

### D. Expressing and Reporting Opinions: *It*-Clauses

Academic communication tends to be impersonal, focusing on actions and effects rather than people. *It*-clauses help achieve this.

#### Pattern 1: *It + be + (adverb) + adjective + to-infinitive*

| Example | Components |
|---------|------------|
| *It is extremely difficult **to evaluate** the role of tourism in urban renewal.* | It + be + adv + adj + to-infinitive |

**Common adjectives for this pattern:**
*(in)convenient, easy, hard, important, (im)possible, interesting, (un)necessary, (un)reasonable, (in)sufficient, useful*

#### Pattern 2: *It + be + (adverb) + adjective + that*

| Example | Components |
|---------|------------|
| *It is quite possible **that** a larger sample size would have shown different results.* | It + be + adv + adj + that-clause |

**Common adjectives for this pattern:**
*apparent, (un)clear, (in)conceivable, doubtful, essential, evident, important, (un)likely, obvious, probable, true*

> **Note:** Not all adverb-adjective combinations work. *Quite + possible/clear* is fine; *quite + doubtful/essential* is rare.

#### Pattern 3: *It + passive reporting verb + that* (reporting others' opinions)

| Example | Components |
|---------|------------|
| *It has been reported (Crane, 2015) **that** prolonged periods sitting in front of a computer may have significant health risks.* | It + passive verb + that-clause (+ citation) |

**Common verbs for this pattern:**
*argue*, *claim*, *contend*, *demonstrate*, *estimate*, *maintain*, *note*, *propose*, *report*, *show*, *suggest*

> Verbs marked with * are also used for distancing (see below).

#### Distancing Yourself from a Point of View

Use *it*-clauses to show you disagree with or have reservations about an earlier claim.

| Example |
|---------|
| *It has been **claimed** that cheese intake is associated with high cholesterol levels (Zemel, 2001), **but** more recent studies have found that cheese may even have a cholesterol lowering effect...* |
| *It is often **suggested** that air pollutants from traffic have a negative impact on child health... **However**, relatively few studies so far have looked at the health of children living near busy roads.* |

**Key verbs for distancing:** *argue, claim, maintain, propose, suggest*

#### Disagreeing (Acknowledging Then Refuting)

Begin a disagreement by acknowledging part of the opposing position as true, then present your counter-argument.

| Written/Formal Spoken | Example |
|----------------------|---------|
| *It is sometimes **argued** that... While it is quite correct that... it must also be noted that...* | *It is sometimes **argued** that it is important for students to outline their essays before beginning writing (e.g. Cotton, 2004). **While it is quite correct that** much of an essay can be planned in advance, **it must also be noted that** the act of writing itself facilitates thought and shapes ideas.* |
| *It's often **assumed** that... Although it's certainly possible to find... in most cases...* | *It's often **assumed** that environmental sustainability and poverty reduction can go hand-in-hand. **Although it's certainly possible to find** a small number of examples where this happens, **in most cases...** the costs fall on the poor.* |

**Less formal alternative (spoken contexts):** *We often assume that... you can certainly find...*

#### Summary: Expressing Opinions with *It*-Clauses

| Function | Pattern | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Impersonal opinion** | *It + be + adj + to-infinitive* | *It is difficult to evaluate...* |
| **Impersonal opinion** | *It + be + adj + that* | *It is possible that...* |
| **Reporting others** | *It + passive verb + that* | *It has been reported that...* |
| **Distancing** | *It + passive verb + that... but/however...* | *It has been claimed that X... but more recent studies show Y...* |
| **Disagreeing politely** | *It is argued that... While it is correct that... it must also be noted that...* | *It is sometimes argued that... While it is correct that... it must also be noted that...* |

### E. Linking Ideas in Academic Writing and Speech

Academic texts use three main types of linking devices to connect ideas.

| Device | Function | Punctuation | Position |
|--------|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Sentence connectors** | Link sentences | Full stop before; often comma after | Usually start of sentence |
| **Conjunctions** | Link clauses within a sentence | Comma often separates clauses | Usually start of their clause |
| **Pro-forms** | Refer back to specific words/phrases to avoid repetition | Varies | Within sentence |

#### Example with Annotations

> **In sum¹**, temperature is an important factor determining the shelf life of a food product **in that²** variations in temperature can result in the growth of microorganisms which can cause the product to spoil. **Furthermore³**, incorrect freezing can damage the proteins of meat or other products. **Given that⁴** the maintenance of the correct temperature over a long period is essential, it is necessary to use time temperature indicators. Studies have shown that *these* indicators reduce food waste considerably. **As well⁵**, they reduce the number of foodborne illnesses.

| Annotation | Type | Meaning |
|------------|------|---------|
| ¹ *In sum* | Sentence connector | Introduces a short summary |
| ² *in that* | Conjunction | Giving a reason |
| ³ *Furthermore* | Sentence connector | Adding information |
| ⁴ *Given that* | Conjunction | "When these things are considered" |
| ⁵ *As well* | Sentence connector | Adding information |

> **Note:** *these* (in "these indicators") is a pro-form referring back to "time temperature indicators."

#### Another Example

> Sociology researchers use two types of information: primary data and secondary data. **The former¹** is collected by the researcher themselves, **whereas²** secondary data already exists in some form, such as documents or previous research. Collecting primary data can be time-consuming and expensive, and **as such³**, undergraduate researchers often prefer to use secondary data.

| Annotation | Type | Meaning |
|------------|------|---------|
| ¹ *The former* | Pro-form | "The first thing mentioned" (primary data) |
| ² *whereas* | Conjunction | Contrasting two facts |
| ³ *as such* | Pro-form | "Because something is as it was previously described" |

#### Formal vs. Informal Alternatives

In spoken academic contexts (presentations, seminars), less formal alternatives are often used.

| Formal (Written) | Informal (Spoken) |
|------------------|-------------------|
| *In sum* | *To put it briefly* |
| *in that* | *The reason for this is that* |
| *Furthermore* | *On top of that* |
| *Given that* | *Taking into account that* |
| *As well* | *Also* |
| *in addition, as well* | *also* |
| *first, firstly* | *first of all* |
| *albeit* | *but* |
| *considering, given that, in the light of* | *bearing in mind that, taking into account that* |
| *as follows* | *in this way* |
| *as such* | *because of this* |

> **Note:** Informal alternatives will not substitute for formal linking words in every context.

#### Quick Reference: Linking Devices

| Device | Purpose | Examples |
|--------|---------|----------|
| **Sentence connectors** | Link sentences (add, contrast, summarize, sequence) | *Furthermore, In sum, However, Firstly, As well, In addition* |
| **Conjunctions** | Link clauses (give reason, contrast, condition) | *in that, given that, whereas, considering, albeit* |
| **Pro-forms** | Refer back to avoid repetition | *the former, the latter, as such, these, those, this* |

### F. Referring to Other Work and Sections

#### 1. Referring to Other Work: *as + passive / past participle*

Use *as* followed by a passive verb or past participle to refer to others' research.

**Pattern 1: *as + passive verb* (full passive)**

| Example | Components |
|---------|------------|
| *...as **is argued** in Frascarelli and Hinter (2007)* | as + is + past participle |
| *...as **has been shown** in a number of recent studies* | as + has been + past participle |

> **Important rule:** Do NOT use *it* in this clause.

| ✅ Correct | ❌ Incorrect |
|-----------|-------------|
| *as is argued by...* | *as **it** is argued by...* |
| *as has been shown...* | *as **it** has been shown...* |

**Pattern 2: *as + past participle* (reduced passive) – More common**

| Example | Components |
|---------|------------|
| ***As noted** by Kerr (2004), these figures were a significant underestimate.* | As + past participle |
| *..., **as demonstrated** in previous studies (e.g., Lam, 2002; Rich et al., 2014)* | as + past participle |

**Common verbs used in this pattern:**
*argued, described, discussed, illustrated, indicated, mentioned, pointed out, shown, suggested*

**Impersonal Agreement vs. Neutral Active**

| Structure | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|
| *as suggested by Bird (2009)* (passive/reduced passive) | Indicates **agreement** (impersonal) |
| *Bird (2009) suggests...* (active) | **Neutral** — can be followed by agreement OR disagreement |

#### 2. Referring to Other Parts of a Written Text: *as + past participle*

Use *as + past participle* to refer to figures, tables, and other sections within your own writing. This is preferred over personal forms like *as you see*.

**Referring to Tables and Figures**

| Example | Pattern |
|---------|---------|
| ***As (is) seen in the following chart**, interest rates are lower now...* | As + seen + in + [figure/table] |
| *..., **as shown in Figure 2-2*** | as + shown + in + [figure] |

**Common verbs for this pattern:**
*demonstrated, depicted, described, discussed, explained, given, illustrated, indicated, outlined, seen, shown*

**Referring to Other Sections (with adverbs)**

| Adverb | Example |
|--------|---------|
| *above* | ***As discussed above**, exploration of the Moon will provide insights...* |
| *earlier, previously* | *As mentioned earlier...* |
| *below, later, next* | *As explained below...* |
| *elsewhere* | *As noted elsewhere in this chapter...* |

#### 3. Referring to Other Parts of a Spoken Presentation

**Key difference from writing:** Use **time adverbs**, not location adverbs.

| Written (Location) | Spoken (Time) |
|--------------------|---------------|
| *as discussed **above*** | *as I said **before*** |
| *as shown **below*** | *as I'll come back to **later on*** |

**Common Structures for Spoken Presentations**

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **-ing clause** | *Coming back to the earliest development of language...* |
| **What-clause** | *What I want to go on to next is the development of written language...* |
| ***as* + past participle** | *...as I pointed out previously...* |
| **Returning to + noun** | *Returning to my point about the development of written language...* |
| **Future reference** | *What we'll do next week is begin to look at non-verbal communication...* |

#### Summary: Referring to Other Work and Sections

| Context | Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Referring to others' work (agreement)** | *as + is/has been + past participle* | *as has been shown by...* |
| **Referring to others' work (common)** | *as + past participle* (reduced) | *as noted by Kerr (2004)* |
| **Referring to figures/tables** | *as + past participle + in + figure/table* | *as shown in Figure 2-2* |
| **Referring to sections (written)** | *as + past participle + above/below/elsewhere* | *as discussed above* |
| **Referring to sections (spoken)** | *as + I + past tense + before/later on* | *as I said before* |
| **Spoken transitions** | *-ing clause* or *what-clause* | *Coming back to...* / *What I want to go on to next...* |

### G. Academic Discussion: Lead-In Phrases

Lead-in phrases help structure academic discussion to introduce ideas cautiously, challenge ideas, invite opinions, clarify, speculate, and more.

#### 1. Reporting Structures to Introduce Ideas Cautiously

Preface an idea with a reporting clause to show it is one point of view, not objective fact.

| Pattern | Example | Use |
|---------|---------|-----|
| *I should/would think that...* | *I should think that we'll still be using fossil fuels in 20 years.* | Cautious opinion |
| *I shouldn't/wouldn't think that...* | *I wouldn't think that anyone would object.* | Cautious negative opinion |
| *I should/would have thought that...* | *I would have thought that there wouldn't be any danger.* | Even more cautious |
| *I shouldn't/wouldn't have thought that...* | *I wouldn't have thought that there would be any danger.* | Even more cautious negative |

> **Important rule:** Avoid two negatives.

| ❌ Incorrect | ✅ Correct |
|-------------|------------|
| *I wouldn't have thought there **wouldn't** be any danger* | *I wouldn't have thought there **would** be any danger* |

**Challenging Another Speaker (Politely):**

| Speaker A | Speaker B (challenging) |
|-----------|------------------------|
| *I'll be targeting 40 to 60 year olds in the experiment.* | *Well, **I shouldn't have thought** the age of the subjects would make any difference.* (= I don't think that) |

#### 2. Reporting Structures to Invite Opinions

Use a **wh-clause** (*why, where, when, how*, etc.) with certain reporting verbs.

> **Pattern:** *Wh-word + do you + verb + (that) / to-infinitive*

| Speaker A | Speaker B (inviting opinion) |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| *The results show a preference for self-service over personal service in stores.* | ***Why do you think** (that) self-service is so popular?* |
| *I'll live stream the lecture to students off campus.* | ***Where do you intend to position** the camera?* |

**Verbs Used in This Pattern**

| With *that* (optional) | With *to*-infinitive |
|-----------------------|---------------------|
| *anticipate, feel, imagine, reckon, recommend, suggest, suppose* | *consider, expect, propose, want* |

**Examples:**
- *How do you **recommend** we proceed?*
- *When do you **expect** to finish the analysis?*

#### 3. Other Lead-In Phrases by Function

| Function | Example Phrases |
|----------|----------------|
| **Reduce force / be tentative** | *In a way...* / *As far as I can see/tell/judge...* |
| **Be direct** | *To be honest...* / *Frankly...* |
| **Clarify an opinion** | *What I mean is (that)...* / *What I'm saying is that...* / *I suppose I'm trying to say (that)...* |
| **Invite opinions / ask for confirmation** | *So you mean that...?* / *So what you're saying is...?* / *So you're saying (that)...?* |
| **Challenge / introduce contrast** | *There/Then again...* / *I take your point, but...* / *That's not to say...* / *Can I just say...* |
| **Introduce a problem or disadvantage** | *The (only) thing/problem/trouble is...* / *For one thing...* |
| **Speculate** | *I'd have thought...* / *I imagine...* |

#### Examples

| Function | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Reduce force** | *In a way, the Internet has completely changed communication.* / *As far as I can tell, the data supports this conclusion.* |
| **Be direct** | *To be honest, I'm not convinced by that argument.* / *Frankly, the methodology has serious flaws.* |
| **Clarify** | *What I mean is that the sample size was too small.* / *What I'm saying is that correlation doesn't equal causation.* |
| **Ask for confirmation** | *So you mean that it has improved communication?* / *So what you're saying is that age doesn't matter?* |
| **Challenge** | *Then again, there might be another explanation.* / *I take your point, but the evidence is weak.* / *That's not to say the study has no value.* |
| **Introduce problem** | *The problem is that we don't have enough data.* / *For one thing, the sample was not representative.* |
| **Speculate** | *I'd have thought the results would be different.* / *I imagine that's why they chose that method.* |
