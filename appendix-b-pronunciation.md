## Appendix B. Pronunciation

Pronunciation is the way in which a word or language is spoken. This section covers the essential elements of English pronunciation, including individual sounds, word stress, sentence stress, intonation, and common problem areas for learners.

> **Note:** This section uses the **International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)** to represent sounds. Learning IPA symbols helps you understand pronunciation from any dictionary.

---

### A. The English Sound System

English has **44 phonemes** (distinct sounds): 24 consonant sounds and 20 vowel sounds. Mastering these sounds is the foundation of clear pronunciation.

---

### D. Word Stress

Word stress means pronouncing one syllable in a word more strongly than the others. Stressed syllables are **louder, longer, and higher in pitch**.

#### 1. Identifying Stressed Syllables

Dictionaries mark stress with an apostrophe before the stressed syllable: /Ààte…™.b…ôl/ (table) - stress on first syllable.

| Word | IPA with Stress | Stressed Syllable |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| *photo* | /Ààf…ô ä.t…ô ä/ | PHO‚Äëto |
| *photography* | /f…ôÀàt…í.…°r…ô.fi/ | pho‚ÄëTO‚Äëgra‚Äëphy |
| *photographic* | /Àåf…ô ä.t…ôÀà…°r√¶f.…™k/ | pho‚Äëto‚ÄëGRA‚Äëphic |

#### 2. Stress Patterns in Two-Syllable Words

| Pattern | Rule | Examples |
|---------|------|----------|
| **Noun: stress on first** | Most two-syllable nouns | *TAble, WINdow, MOney* |
| **Verb: stress on second** | Most two-syllable verbs | *reLAX, deCIDE, preSENT* |
| **Exceptions** | Many exceptions | *HOnor (noun/verb), VISit (noun/verb)* |

#### 3. Stress Shifts Between Word Forms

| Base Word | Stress | Related Word | Stress |
|-----------|--------|--------------|--------|
| *economy* | /…™Ààk…í.n…ô.mi/ | *economic* | /ÀåiÀê.k…ôÀàn…ím.…™k/ |

| *photograph* | /Ààf…ô ä.t…ô.…°r…ëÀêf/ | *photography* | /f…ôÀàt…í.…°r…ô.fi/ |

#### 4. Suffixes That Affect Stress

| Suffix | Stress Pattern | Examples |
|--------|----------------|----------|
| **-tion, -sion** | Stress before the suffix | *edu**CA**tion, tele**VI**sion* |
| **-ic, -ical** | Stress before -ic | *eco**NO**mic, **PO**litical* |
| **-ity** | Stress before -ity | *a**BI**lity, curi**OS**ity* |
| **-graphy** | Stress on first syllable of suffix | *pho**TO**graphy, bi**OG**raphy* |
| **-logy** | Stress on first syllable of suffix | *bi**OL**ogy, psy**CHOL**ogy* |

#### 5. Suffixes That Do Not Change Stress

| Suffix | Examples |
|--------|----------|

### E. Sentence Stress

In English sentences, **content words** are stressed, while **function words** are often reduced (using schwa).

#### 1. Content Words (Stressed)

These words carry the main meaning of the sentence.

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Nouns** | *dog, house, John, idea* |
| **Main verbs** | *run, eat, think, want* |
| **Adjectives** | *big, red, happy, beautiful* |
| **Adverbs** | *quickly, often, never, well* |
| **Question words** | *what, where, when, why, how* |
| **Negative auxiliaries** | *don't, can't, won't, isn't* |

#### 2. Function Words (Unstressed / Reduced)

These words are grammatical connectors. They are pronounced quickly and often reduced to schwa.

| Category | Examples | Reduced Pronunciation |
|----------|----------|----------------------|
| **Pronouns** | *he, she, it, them* | /hi,  Éi, …™t, √∞…ôm/ |
| **Prepositions** | *to, for, of, at* | /t…ô, f…ô, …ôv, …ôt/ |
| **Auxiliary verbs** | *can, will, have, do* | /k…ôn, w…ôl, h…ôv, d…ô/ |
| **Conjunctions** | *and, but, so, or* | /…ôn, b…ôt, s…ô, …ôr/ |
| **Articles** | *a, an, the* | /…ô, …ôn, √∞…ô/ |

#### 3. Sentence Stress Example

| Sentence | Stressed Words (Bold) | Unstressed Words (lowercase) |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| *I will **meet** you at the **station**.* | MEET, STATION | I will, you at the |
| ***What** are you **doing**?* | WHAT, DOING | are you |
| *She **can't** **come** to the **party**.* | CAN'T, COME, PARTY | she, to the |

> **Natural pronunciation:** *I'll MEET you at the STAtion.* (unstressed syllables reduced)

### F. Intonation

Intonation is the rise and fall of pitch in speech. English uses intonation to convey meaning, attitude, and sentence type.

#### 1. Falling Intonation (‚Üò)

Used for statements, commands, and wh-questions.

| Sentence Type | Example | Intonation |
|---------------|---------|------------|
| Statement | *I'm going home.* | ‚Üò |
| Command | *Close the door.* | ‚Üò |
| Wh-question | *Where do you live?* | ‚Üò |

#### 2. Rising Intonation (‚Üó)

Used for yes/no questions, questions of surprise, and listing items (except the last).

| Sentence Type | Example | Intonation |

| Surprise question | *You did what?* | ‚Üó |

#### 3. Fall-Rise Intonation (‚Üò‚Üó)

### G. Connected Speech

In natural speech, sounds change, link, or disappear to make pronunciation smoother.

### H. Common Pronunciation Problems by Language Background

#### 1. For Spanish/Italian Speakers

| Problem | Example | Correction |
|---------|---------|------------|
| /…™/ vs. /iÀê/ | *ship/sheep* | Short vs. long vowel |
| /v/ vs. /b/ | *very/berry* | Teeth on lip for /v/ |
| Initial /s/ + consonant | *school* ‚Üí /eskul/ | Add no vowel before /s/ |
| /d í/ vs. /j/ | *yes/jazz* | /d í/ has a "d" start |

#### 2. For Chinese/Japanese/Korean Speakers

| Problem | Example | Correction |
|---------|---------|------------|
| /r/ vs. /l/ | *right/light* | /r/: curl tongue; /l/: touch roof |
| /Œ∏/ & /√∞/ | *three/this* | Tongue between teeth |
| Final consonants | *cat* ‚Üí /k√¶/ | Pronounce final consonants |
| Vowel length | *beat/bit* | Distinguish long vs. short |
| Word stress | Many errors | Learn stress patterns |

#### 3. For Arabic Speakers

| Problem | Example | Correction |
|---------|---------|------------|
| /p/ vs. /b/ | *pen/ben* | /p/ has a puff of air |
| /…™/ vs. /iÀê/ | *ship/sheep* | Short vs. long vowel |
| Consonant clusters | *spring* ‚Üí /spr…™≈ã/ | Pronounce all consonants |
| Final consonants | Many errors | Don't add vowel after final consonant |

#### 4. For French Speakers

| Problem | Example | Correction |
|---------|---------|------------|
| /h/ sound | *house* ‚Üí /a äs/ | Pronounce /h/ (air from throat) |
| /Œ∏/ & /√∞/ | *think/this* | Tongue between teeth |
| /…™/ vs. /iÀê/ | *ship/sheep* | Short vs. long vowel |

#### 5. For German Speakers

| Problem | Example | Correction |
|---------|---------|------------|
| /w/ vs. /v/ | *west/vest* | /w/ has rounded lips |
| /Œ∏/ & /√∞/ | *think/this* | Tongue between teeth |
| Final devoicing | *dog* ‚Üí /d…ík/ | Keep final consonants voiced |

### I. Tips for Improving Pronunciation

| Tip | How to Practice |
|-----|-----------------|
| **Listen actively** | Watch movies/TV with subtitles; notice stress and intonation |
| **Record yourself** | Compare your pronunciation to native speakers |
| **Use a dictionary** | Check IPA symbols and stress marks |
| **Practice minimal pairs** | *ship/sheep, right/light, three/tree* |
| **Learn the schwa** | Most unstressed vowels are schwa; this is key to natural rhythm |
| **Shadow native speakers** | Repeat immediately after hearing a phrase |
| **Slow down** | Speaking slowly gives you time to pronounce correctly |
| **Focus on word stress** | Wrong stress can make words unrecognizable |

### K. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction |
|-------|------------|
| Pronouncing every syllable equally | Use word stress (one syllable stronger) |
| Pronouncing every word separately | Use connected speech (linking, elision) |
| Using native language vowel sounds | Learn English vowel distinctions |
| Adding a vowel before /s/+consonant | *school* not *eschool* |
| Dropping final consonants | *cat* not *ca* |
| Not using the schwa | Reduce unstressed vowels to /…ô/ |
| Flat intonation | Use rising/falling pitch to show meaning |
