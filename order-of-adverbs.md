## 19.3 Order of Adverbs

### A. The Basic Rule: MPT Rule (Manner - Place - Time)

When multiple adverbs or adverbial phrases appear in the same clause, they generally follow this order:

| Order | Category | Answers | Examples |
|-------|----------|---------|----------|
| **1st** | **Manner** | How? | *quickly, carefully, well, on foot, by bus* |
| **2nd** | **Place** | Where? | *at home, in the office, outside, there* |
| **3rd** | **Time** | When? | *yesterday, last week, at 3:00, soon* |

#### Classic MPT Example

| Sentence | Order |
|----------|-------|
| *She worked **carefully** (M) **in the lab** (P) **yesterday** (T).* | M -> P -> T |
| *They arrived **safely** (M) **in Paris** (P) **last night** (T).* | M -> P -> T |
| *He ran **quickly** (M) **to the station** (P) **this morning** (T).* | M -> P -> T |

> **Memory Aid:** "**M**y **P**arents **T**aught me" -> Manner, Place, Time

---

### B. The Expanded Rule: SMEPT (Sentence - Manner - Evaluation - Place - Time)

A more detailed hierarchy accounts for additional adverb types. The full order is:

| Position | Category | Answers | Examples |
|----------|----------|---------|----------|
| **1** | **Sentence / Viewpoint** | From what perspective? | *fortunately, surprisingly, honestly, officially* |
| **2** | **Manner** | How? | *slowly, carefully, well, by hand, with care* |
| **3** | **Evaluation / Degree** | To what degree? | *completely, almost, nearly, hardly* |
| **4** | **Place** | Where? | *at work, in the garden, outside, there* |
| **5** | **Time** | When? | *today, later, at noon, in July* |

#### SMEPT Examples

| Adverb Sequence | Manner | Place | Time |
|-----------------|--------|-------|------|
| *She sang **beautifully** at the concert last night.* | beautifully | at the concert | last night |
| *He drove **recklessly** through town yesterday.* | recklessly | through town | yesterday |
| *They ate **hungrily** at the restaurant after work.* | hungrily | at the restaurant | after work |

#### With Sentence/Viewpoint Adverbs

| Example | S | M | P | T |
|---------|---|---|---|---|
| ***Fortunately**, she arrived safely at the hospital on time.* | Fortunately (S) | safely (M) | at the hospital (P) | on time (T) |
| ***Honestly**, he behaved badly at the party last night.* | Honestly (S) | badly (M) | at the party (P) | last night (T) |

---

### C. Multiple Adverbs of the Same Type

#### 1. Multiple Time Adverbs: Specific Before General

When you have more than one time expression, more specific times come before more general ones.

| Order | Type | Examples |
|-------|------|----------|
| **1st** | Specific Time (clock time) | *at 3:00, at noon, at midnight* |
| **2nd** | Day / Date | *on Monday, on June 5th* |
| **3rd** | Month / Season | *in July, in winter* |
| **4th** | Year / General Time | *in 2024, last year* |

| ✓ Correct | ✗ Incorrect |
|------------|--------------|
| *I was born **at 4 a.m. on Tuesday, July 15th, 1990**.* | *I was born **in 1990 on July 15th at 4 a.m.**.* |

**Note:** Native speakers rarely string together all four. The rule applies when two or three time expressions appear.

| Practical Example |
|-------------------|
| *The meeting is **at 3:00 on Monday**.* |
| *She arrived **late on Friday night**.* |
| *We met **in July last year**.* |

#### 2. Multiple Place Adverbs: Small Before Large

More specific places come before more general ones.

| Order | Type | Examples |
|-------|------|----------|
| **1st** | Specific Location | *in the kitchen, at 221B Baker Street* |
| **2nd** | Area / District | *in Marylebone, downtown* |
| **3rd** | City / Town | *in London, in New York* |
| **4th** | Country / Region | *in England, in the US* |

| ✓ Correct | ✗ Incorrect |
|------------|--------------|
| *She lives **in a small flat in Marylebone, London**.* | *She lives **in London, in Marylebone, in a small flat**.* |
| *They work **at a hospital downtown in Chicago**.* | *They work **in Chicago, downtown, at a hospital**.* |

#### 3. Multiple Manner Adverbs: More Objective Before Subjective

When manner adverbs stack, more objective/physical descriptions come before evaluative ones.

| Example |
|---------|
| *She spoke **loudly and aggressively**.* (physical manner -> attitude) |
| *He moved **slowly and cautiously**.* (speed -> attitude) |

**Note:** Multiple manner adverbs are uncommon. When they occur, they're often separated by *and* instead of commas.

---

### D. Adverb Position Within the Clause

Adverb placement depends on the adverb type and the verb structure.

#### 1. Front Position (Beginning of Clause)

| Adverb Type | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| **Sentence / Viewpoint** | ***Fortunately**, no one was hurt.* |
| **Time (for emphasis/contrast)** | ***Yesterday**, I finished the report. / **Last week**, we went to Rome.* |
| **Place (literary/emphatic)** | ***On the hill** stood a small cottage.* (inversion) |
| **Connecting / Sequence** | ***First**, mix the flour. **Then**, add the eggs.* |

#### 2. Mid Position (Before Main Verb / After First Auxiliary)

| Adverb Type | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| **Frequency** | *She **often** visits us. / He has **never** been late.* |
| **Certainty** | *They will **probably** arrive soon. / She **definitely** knows.* |
| **Focusing** | *I **only** need five minutes. / She **even** baked a cake.* |
| **Degree (before adj/adv)** | *It's **extremely** cold. / She runs **very** fast.* |

#### 3. End Position (After Verb/Object - Most Common for MPT)

| Adverb Type | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| **Manner** | *She spoke **quietly**. / He drives **carefully**.* |
| **Place** | *They are waiting **outside**. / Put it **on the table**.* |
| **Time** | *I'll see you **tomorrow**. / We met **last year**.* |
| **Purpose** | *She went to the store **to buy milk**.* |

#### Mid-Position Hierarchy (When Multiple Mid-Position Adverbs Occur)

When several adverbs appear in mid-position, the order is:

| Order | Adverb Type | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **1st** | Frequency | *always, often, sometimes, never* |
| **2nd** | Certainty | *probably, definitely, certainly* |
| **3rd** | Focusing | *only, even, just* |

| Example |
|---------|
| *She **always probably just** wants to help.* (frequency -> certainty -> focusing) |

**Note:** Multiple mid-position adverbs are rare and often sound awkward. Native speakers usually avoid stacking them.

---

### E. Exceptions and Variations

#### 1. Time Before Place for Emphasis

The MPT rule can be broken when time needs emphasis or contrast.

| Neutral (MPT) | Emphasis on Time (TPM) |
|---------------|------------------------|
| *They arrived safely in Paris last night.* | ***Last night** they arrived in Paris.* |
| *She went to the hospital yesterday.* | ***Yesterday**, she went to the hospital.* |

#### 2. Place Before Manner with Movement Verbs

With verbs of movement (*go, come, run, walk, drive*), place often comes before manner or is inseparable from the verb.

| Emphasis on Movement (Place First) | Emphasis on Manner (Manner First) |
|-------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| *She went **to London by train**.* | *She travelled **comfortably to London**.* |
| *He ran **to the shop quickly**.* | *He ran **quickly to the shop**.* |

**Guideline:** When the place is the destination, it tends to stay close to the verb. When the manner is the main point, it comes first.

#### 3. Short Adverbs Before Longer Phrases

Shorter adverbs tend to come before longer adverbial phrases, even if it breaks the category order.

| ✓ Natural | ✗ Awkward |
|------------|------------|
| *She waited **patiently at the bus stop on the corner**.* | *She waited **at the bus stop on the corner patiently**.* |
| *He worked **quietly in the small office on the third floor**.* | *He worked **in the small office on the third floor quietly**.* |

#### 4. Time adverbs *before* place when place is very long

When the place adverbial is a long phrase, time can shift forward for clarity.

| ✓ Natural |
|------------|
| *I saw him **yesterday** at that little café on the corner near the station.* |
| *She'll be working **in July** at the new research facility they just opened in Cambridge.* |

---

### F. Order of Adverbs vs. Order of Adverbials (Phrases and Clauses)

The same ordering principle applies whether the adverbial is a single word, a phrase, or a clause.

| Type | Manner | Place | Time |
|------|--------|-------|------|
| **Single Word** | *quickly* | *here* | *yesterday* |
| **Phrase** | *with great care* | *in the library* | *last Tuesday* |
| **Clause** | *as if he were exhausted* | *where they first met* | *when the bell rang* |

| Example |
|---------|
| *She spoke **softly** (M) **in the corner** (P) **when the meeting ended** (T).* |
| *He worked **as if his life depended on it** (M) **at his desk** (P) **all night** (T).* |

---

### G. Negative Adverbials: Inversion Changes Everything

When the sentence begins with a negative or restrictive adverb, the subject and auxiliary **invert**. This overrides normal order.

| Negative Adverb | Inverted Example |
|-----------------|------------------|
| *Never* | ***Never have I seen** such beauty.* |
| *Rarely / Seldom* | ***Rarely does she complain**.* |
| *Hardly / Scarcely* | ***Hardly had I arrived** when it started.* |
| *Not only* | ***Not only did she win**, she broke the record.* |
| *No sooner* | ***No sooner had he left** than the phone rang.* |
| *Only then / Only later* | ***Only later did I realize** my mistake.* |
| *Under no circumstances* | ***Under no circumstances should you open** that door.* |

> **See also:** *[Section 11. Inversion](./inversion.md)*

---

### H. Adverb Order Quick Reference Decision Chart

```txt
Is there a negative/restrictive adverb at the front?
├── YES -> Inversion: Adverb + Auxiliary + Subject + Verb
│
└── NO -> What type of adverb?
│
├── Sentence / Viewpoint (fortunately, honestly)?
│ -> Front position
│
├── Frequency / Certainty / Focusing (often, probably, only)?
│ -> Mid position (before main verb, after auxiliary)
│
├── Manner, Place, or Time?
│ -> End position, in order: MANNER -> PLACE -> TIME
│ │
│ ├── Is time being emphasized?
│ │ -> YES: Time can go to front position
│ │
│ ├── Is place very long?
│ │ -> YES: Time can move before place for clarity
│ │
│ └── Is the manner adverb short, place long?
│ -> YES: Keep manner close to verb
│
└── Multiple adverbs of same type?
├── Time: specific before general (3:00 -> Monday -> July -> 2024)
└── Place: small before large (flat -> street -> city -> country)
```

## I. Common Errors and Corrections

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| *She arrived yesterday at the office.* | *She arrived at the office **yesterday**.* | MPT: Place before Time |
| *He ran to the station quickly.* | *He ran **quickly** to the station.* | Manner before Place (unless place is destination emphasis) |
| *They spoke at the meeting nervously.* | *They spoke **nervously** at the meeting.* | Manner before Place |
| *I was born in 1990 on July 15th.* | *I was born **on July 15th, 1990**.* | Specific time before general time |
| *She lives in London, in Marylebone, in a flat.* | *She lives **in a flat in Marylebone, London**.* | Small place before large place |
| *Never I have seen such a thing.* | ***Never have I seen** such a thing.* | Negative adverb -> inversion |
| *Fortunately, arrived she safely.* | *Fortunately, **she arrived** safely.* | No inversion with non-negative adverbs |
| *She carefully in the lab yesterday worked.* | *She **worked carefully in the lab yesterday**.* | End-position adverbs go after the verb/object |

## J. Practice: Put the Adverbials in the Correct Order

Arrange the given elements into a natural sentence.

| Elements | Correct Order |
|----------|---------------|
| *arrived / she / at the party / late / last night* | *She arrived **late** (M) **at the party** (P) **last night** (T).* |
| *spoke / he / to the audience / confidently / this morning* | *He spoke **confidently** (M) **to the audience** (P) **this morning** (T).* |
| *was waiting / she / at the station / patiently / at noon* | *She was waiting **patiently** (M) **at the station** (P) **at noon** (T).* |
| *met / we / at the café / on Tuesday / at 2:00* | *We met **at 2:00 on Tuesday at the café**.* (Time specificity, then place) |
| *have / I / seen / never / such a beautiful sunset* | ***Never have I seen** such a beautiful sunset.* (Negative inversion) |
