## 20. Conjunctions

Conjunctions are words that link together words, phrases, clauses, or sentences. They are essential for creating complex sentences and showing the relationships between different ideas.

---

### A. Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS)

These conjunctions connect elements of equal grammatical rank (words, phrases, or independent clauses). The most common are remembered by the acronym **FANBOYS**: *For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So*.

| Conjunction | Function | Example |
|-------------|----------|---------|
| *For* | Reason (similar to *because*) | *He must be tired, **for** he has been working all day.* |
| *And* | Addition | *She likes coffee **and** tea.* / *He walked to the store, **and** he bought some milk.* |
| *Nor* | Negative addition (requires inversion) | *She doesn't like coffee, **nor does she** like tea.* / *He is not tall, **nor is he** short.* |
| *But* | Contrast | *He is rich, **but** he is unhappy.* / *I wanted to go, **but** I couldn't.* |
| *Or* | Alternative / choice | *Would you like coffee **or** tea?* / *You can stay home, **or** you can come with us.* |
| *Yet* | Surprising contrast | *The weather was cold, **yet** we enjoyed the picnic.* / *He studied hard, **yet** he failed.* |
| *So* | Result / consequence | *It was raining, **so** we stayed inside.* / *He was hungry, **so** he ate an apple.* |

> **Note for *Nor*:** When *nor* connects two independent clauses, it requires **inversion** of the subject and verb in the second clause.

---

### B. Subordinating Conjunctions

These conjunctions connect a **dependent (subordinate) clause** to an **independent (main) clause**. The dependent clause cannot stand alone as a sentence.

#### 1. Time

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *when, whenever* | ***When** the bell rings, the students leave.* / *I call him **whenever** I have free time.* |
| *while, as* | ***While** I was walking, I saw an accident.* / *He reads **as** his sister watches TV.* |
| *before* | *Wash your hands **before** you eat.* |
| *after* | *We can leave **after** the movie finishes.* |
| *since* | *I haven't seen her **since** last year.* |
| *until / till* | *Wait here **until** I return.* |

> **Note:** *While* (conjunction) + clause is different from *during* (preposition) + noun. The meaning is similar, but the grammar is different. See **[Section 21. Prepositions](./prepositions.md)** for the distinction.

#### 2. Cause / Reason

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *because* | *He stayed home **because** he was sick.* |
| *since* (at beginning of sentence) | ***Since** you're not busy, could you help me?* |
| *as* (at beginning of sentence) | ***As** it was getting late, we decided to go home.* |

#### 3. Condition

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *if* | ***If** it rains, we will cancel the picnic.* |
| *unless* (= if not) | *You won't pass **unless** you study harder.* |
| *provided (that), providing (that)* | *We will go **provided that** the weather is good.* |
| *as long as* | *You can borrow my car **as long as** you are careful.* |
| *in case* | *Take an umbrella **in case** it rains.* |

#### 4. Contrast / Concession

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *although, though, even though* | ***Although** it was difficult, he succeeded.* / *She went out **even though** she felt unwell.* |
| *while* (contrast) | *Some people like tea, **while** others prefer coffee.* |
| *whereas* | *My brother is tall, **whereas** I am short.* |

#### 5. Purpose

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *so that, in order that* | *Speak clearly **so that** everyone can understand.* / *He saved money **in order that** he could buy a house.* |

#### 6. Manner

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *as* | *Do **as** I say, not as I do.* |
| *as if, as though* | *He acts **as if** he owns the place.* / *It looks **as though** it might rain.* |
| *like* (informal - connecting clauses) | *He sings **like** he's famous.* (Informal; *as if* is preferred in formal writing) |

---

### C. Adversative Conjunctions

Adversative conjunctions connect two clauses or phrases that express **contrasting or opposing ideas**. They highlight a situation where the second part presents something unexpected, contradictory, or in opposition to the first part.

| Conjunction / Connector | Function | Example |
|------------------------|----------|---------|
| *but* | Most common adversative conjunction | *I wanted to go, **but** I was too tired.* |
| *however* | Contrast (can begin a sentence or follow a semicolon) | *The weather was terrible. **However**, we still enjoyed our vacation.* / *She studied diligently; **however**, she didn't pass.* |
| *yet* | Surprising contrast | *He is very wealthy, **yet** he is unhappy.* |
| *although / though / even though* | Introduce subordinate clause (contrast despite the clause) | ***Although** it was raining, we went for a walk.* / *He finished the race, **though** he came in last.* |
| *whereas* | Compare/contrast differences | *Some prefer coffee, **whereas** others prefer tea.* |
| *despite / in spite of* | Prepositions showing contrast (followed by noun/gerund) | ***Despite** the difficulties, they succeeded.* / ***In spite of** the heavy traffic, we arrived on time.* |

> **Key Idea:** When you see these words, expect a **shift in thought** - one idea presented in opposition to, or as a surprising counterpoint to, another.

#### C.1. *Although, Despite, In Spite Of* (Full Contrast)

*Although*, *despite*, and *in spite of* all express contrast - they mean "even though something is true, something else happens." However, they use **different grammatical structures**, and this is one of the most common sources of error at intermediate and advanced levels.

> **Related Sections:** See *[Section 20. Conjunctions](#b-subordinating-conjunctions)* B.4 for *although/though/even though* as subordinating conjunctions, and *[Section 7. If-Clauses, Wish-Clauses, and Conditionals](./if-clauses-wish-clauses-conditionals.md#1-even-if)* (G.1) for *even if*.

---

##### A. Meaning

All three expressions introduce an **unexpected or contrasting** situation. They answer the question: "What is surprising about this?"

| Expression | Meaning | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| ***Although/Though/Even though*** | "Despite the fact that..." | ***Although** it was raining, we went out.* |
| ***Despite*** | "Without being affected by..." | ***Despite** the rain, we went out.* |
| ***In spite of*** | Same as *despite* | ***In spite of** the rain, we went out.* |

> **Note:** *Despite* and *in spite of* are **identical in meaning**. *Despite* is slightly more common and concise. *In spite of* is perfectly correct and interchangeable.

---

##### B. The Structural Difference

The key to using these correctly is knowing **what follows them**:

| Expression | Followed By | Structure |
|------------|-------------|-----------|
| ***although / though / even though*** | A **clause** (subject + verb) | *although + clause* |
| ***despite / in spite of*** | A **noun**, **gerund** (*-ing*), or **the fact that + clause** | *despite + noun/gerund/fact that* |

**This is the rule that learners most often break:**

| Correct | Incorrect | Why |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| ***Although** it was raining, we went out.* | - | *Although* + clause ✓ |
| ***Despite** the rain, we went out.* | ***Despite** it was raining, we went out.* | *Despite* + clause ✗ (needs a noun/gerund/fact that) |
| ***In spite of** the rain, we went out.* | ***In spite of** it was raining, we went out.* | *In spite of* + clause ✗ (needs a noun/gerund/fact that) |

---

##### C. *Although / Though / Even Though*

These are **subordinating conjunctions** - they introduce a dependent clause with a subject and verb.

| Conjunction | Strength | Example |
|-------------|----------|---------|
| ***although*** | Neutral - standard formal/written | ***Although** he is rich, he is unhappy.* |
| ***though*** | Slightly more informal; can go at the end of a sentence | *He is rich. He is unhappy, **though**.* |
| ***even though*** | Stronger - emphasizes the contrast or surprise | ***Even though** he is a millionaire, he lives very simply.* |

**Position:**

| Position | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Beginning** (common) | ***Although** it was late, she continued working.* |
| **Middle** (no comma) | *She continued working **although** it was late.* |

**Examples:**

| Conjunction | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| *although* | ***Although** the exam was difficult, I passed.* |
| *though* | *It was cold, **though** we still enjoyed the walk.* |
| *even though* | ***Even though** she apologized, I'm still angry.* |

***Though* at the end of a sentence (informal spoken English):**

| Example | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| *The film was long. I enjoyed it, **though**.* | ...but I enjoyed it despite that. |
| *It's expensive. It's worth it, **though**.* | ...but it's worth it despite the price. |

> **Note:** *Although* cannot go at the end of a sentence. Only *though* can do this.

---

##### D. *Despite* and *In Spite Of*

These are **prepositions** - they must be followed by a noun, pronoun, gerund (*-ing*), or *the fact that + clause*. They **cannot** be followed directly by a clause with a subject and verb.

| Structure | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| ***despite/in spite of + noun*** | *Despite the traffic, we arrived on time.* |
| | *In spite of his age, he runs every day.* |
| ***despite/in spite of + pronoun*** | *He failed despite that.* |
| ***despite/in spite of + gerund*** (*-ing*) | *She passed despite not studying much.* |
| | *In spite of working hard, he didn't get promoted.* |
| ***despite/in spite of + the fact that + clause*** | *Despite the fact that it was raining, we went out.* |
| | *In spite of the fact that she was tired, she finished.* |

**Examples by Structure:**

| Structure | *Despite* | *In Spite Of* |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| **+ noun** | *Despite the rain...* | *In spite of the rain...* |
| **+ gerund** | *Despite being ill...* | *In spite of being ill...* |
| **+ the fact that** | *Despite the fact that it was late...* | *In spite of the fact that it was late...* |

---

##### E. Expressing the Same Idea: Three Ways

Any contrast can be expressed using all three patterns:

| Idea | *Although + clause* | *Despite + noun/gerund* | *Despite the fact that + clause* |
|------|---------------------|-------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Rain → went out | *Although it was raining, we went out.* | *Despite the rain, we went out.* | *Despite the fact that it was raining, we went out.* |
| Tired → finished | *Although she was tired, she finished.* | *Despite her tiredness, she finished.* | *Despite the fact that she was tired, she finished.* |
| Studied → failed | *Although he studied hard, he failed.* | *Despite studying hard, he failed.* | *Despite the fact that he studied hard, he failed.* |
| Poor → happy | *Although they are poor, they are happy.* | *Despite their poverty, they are happy.* | *Despite the fact that they are poor, they are happy.* |

---

##### F. Comparison Table: All Structures

| | *Although / Though / Even Though* | *Despite / In Spite Of* |
|---|-----------------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Part of speech** | Subordinating conjunction | Preposition |
| **Followed by** | Clause (subject + verb) | Noun / pronoun / gerund / *the fact that* + clause |
| **Can go at beginning?** | Yes | Yes |
| **Can go in middle?** | Yes | Yes |
| **Can go at end of sentence?** | *Though* only | No |
| **Formality** | *Although* = neutral/formal; *Though* = neutral; *Even though* = emphatic | Both are neutral |

---

##### G. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction | Rule |
|-------|------------|------|
| ***Despite it was raining**, we went out.* | ***Although it was raining**, we went out.* or ***Despite the rain**, we went out.* | *Despite* cannot be followed by a subject + verb clause. Use *although* or add a noun/gerund/*the fact that*. |
| ***Although the rain**, we went out.* | ***Despite the rain**, we went out.* | *Although* must be followed by a clause (subject + verb), not a noun. |
| ***Despite of the rain**, we went out.* | ***Despite the rain**, we went out.* or ***In spite of the rain**, we went out.* | *Despite* has no *of*. *In spite of* has *of*. Don't mix them. |
| ***In spite the rain**, we went out.* | ***In spite of the rain**, we went out.* | *In spite* must have *of*. |
| ***Although being tired**, she worked.* | ***Although she was tired**, she worked.* or ***Despite being tired**, she worked.* | *Although* + gerund is incorrect. Use *although + clause* or *despite + gerund*. |
| ***Despite she was tired**, she worked.* | ***Although she was tired**, she worked.* or ***Despite the fact that she was tired**, she worked.* | *Despite* + clause ✗ (unless *the fact that* is added) |
| ***Although** he finished last, **but** he was proud.* | ***Although** he finished last, he was proud.* | Don't use both *although* and *but* in the same sentence. One contrast word is enough. |

### D. Conjunctive Adverbs (Transitional Expressions)

These words or phrases function as adverbs but also connect ideas between sentences or clauses, similar to conjunctions. They are typically followed by a **comma** when they start a sentence.

#### 1. Contrast

| Conjunctive Adverb | Example |
|--------------------|---------|
| *however* | *He studied diligently. **However**, he didn't pass the test.* |
| *nevertheless, nonetheless* | *The task was challenging. **Nevertheless**, she completed it on time.* |

#### 2. Result / Consequence

| Conjunctive Adverb | Example |
|--------------------|---------|
| *therefore, consequently, thus, hence* | *The evidence was clear. **Therefore**, the jury found him guilty.* / *She missed the bus. **Consequently**, she was late.* |

#### 3. Addition

| Conjunctive Adverb | Example |
|--------------------|---------|
| *moreover, furthermore, also, in addition, besides* | *The hotel has a gym. **Moreover**, it offers a spa.* / *He is intelligent. **Besides**, he is very kind.* |

#### 4. Sequence / Time

| Conjunctive Adverb | Example |
|--------------------|---------|
| *then, next, afterward(s), finally, first, second* | *He finished his work. **Then**, he went home.* / ***First**, preheat the oven. **Next**, mix the ingredients.* |

#### 5. Emphasis

| Conjunctive Adverb | Example |
|--------------------|---------|
| *indeed* | *It was a good decision **indeed**.* |

---

### E. Special Conjunctions and Connectors

These are multi-word conjunctions or special connectors that function as single units.

| Connector | Function | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| *in case* | Condition (preparation) | *Bring a map **in case** you get lost.* |
| *as long as* | Condition | *You can borrow the book **as long as** you return it next week.* |
| *provided (that)* | Condition | *You can go **provided that** you finish your homework.* |
| *during* | Preposition (period of time) + noun | *She worked **during** the night.* |
| *for* | Preposition (duration) + period of time | *He slept **for** three hours.* |
| *by* | Deadline / latest point | *Please submit your report **by** Friday.* / *I will finish **by** 5 PM.* |
| *until / till* | Duration up to a point | *We waited **until** midnight.* |
| *by the time* | Point in time when something is completed | ***By the time** we arrived, the show had already started.* |

---

### F. Quick Reference Summary Table

| Type | Function | Examples |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Coordinating (FANBOYS)** | Connect equal elements | *for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so* |
| **Subordinating** | Connect dependent to independent clause | *because, although, if, when, while, unless* |
| **Adversative** | Show contrast/opposition | *but, however, yet, although, whereas* |
| **Conjunctive adverbs** | Connect sentences/clauses (with comma) | *however, therefore, moreover, then, indeed* |

### G. Common Errors

| Incorrect | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| *Despite it was raining, we went out.* | *Although it was raining, we went out.* or *Despite the rain, we went out.* | *Despite* + noun/gerund, not + clause |
| *Despite of the rain, we went out.* | *Despite the rain, we went out.* | *Despite* has no *of* |
| *In spite the rain, we went out.* | *In spite of the rain, we went out.* | *In spite of* needs *of* |
| *Although the rain, we went out.* | *Despite the rain, we went out.* | *Although* + clause, not + noun |
| *Although being tired, she worked.* | *Although she was tired, she worked.* or *Despite being tired, she worked.* | *Although* + subject + verb, not + gerund |
