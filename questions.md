## 13. Questions

English questions are typically formed by inverting the usual word order, swapping the subject and the auxiliary or modal verb.

> **Basic Pattern:** *Statement (Subject + Verb)* → *Question (Verb + Subject)*  
> *Example:* You **are** happy. → **Are** you happy?

---

### A. Yes/No Questions

Yes/No questions can be answered with a simple "yes" or "no."

> **Structure:** *Auxiliary/Modal Verb + Subject + Base Verb (or main verb)*

#### 1. Using *Do/Does/Did* (Present and Past Simple)

When there is no auxiliary or modal verb already present, use *do/does/did* to form the question. The main verb remains in its **base form** (V1).

| Tense | Form | Example |
|-------|------|---------|
| Present Simple | *Do + subject + V1?* | *Do you like coffee?* |
| Present Simple (third person) | *Does + subject + V1?* | *Does she work here?* (The *-s* moves to *does*) |
| Past Simple | *Did + subject + V1?* | *Did they call you?* (The past tense moves to *did*) |

#### 2. Using Existing Auxiliary or Modal Verbs

When a modal verb (*can, should, will, must*, etc.) or an auxiliary verb (*be, have*) is already present, it is used for inversion.

| Verb Type | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Modal (*can, should, will, must*) | *Can you help?* / *Should we go?* / *Will it rain?* |
| *Be* (am/is/are/was/were) | *Is she coming?* / *Were they happy?* |
| *Have* (perfect tenses) | *Have you finished?* / *Has she arrived?* |

---

### B. Wh-Questions

Wh-questions seek specific information using interrogative words.

> **Structure:** *Wh-Word + Auxiliary/Modal Verb + Subject + Base Verb (or main verb)*

**Common Wh-Words:** *who, what, where, when, why, how*

| Example | Breakdown |
|---------|-----------|
| *Where do you live?* | Where + do + you + live |
| *Why did he leave?* | Why + did + he + leave |
| *How can I help?* | How + can + I + help |
| *What is she doing?* | What + is + she + doing |
| *When have they arrived?* | When + have + they + arrived |
| *Who is that?* | Who + is + that (no auxiliary needed when *who* is the subject) |

> **Special Case - *Wh* as Subject:** When the wh-word is the subject of the question, no inversion occurs.
> - *Who called you?* (Not: *Who did call you?*)
> - *What happened?* (Not: *What did happen?* - unless for emphasis)

---

### C. Embedded (Indirect) Questions

Embedded questions are clauses within a larger sentence, often used to be more polite or to report what someone else asked.

> **Key Rule:** **No inversion** within the embedded wh-clause. The word order reverts to **Subject + Verb** (standard statement order). **No *do/does/did*** inside the embedded clause.

| Direct Question | Embedded Question |
|----------------|-------------------|
| *Where do you live?* | *Can you tell me where **you live**?* |
| *Where does she live?* | *Can you tell me where **she lives**?* |
| *Why did he leave?* | *I wonder why **he left**.* |
| *What time is it?* | *Do you know what time **it is**?* |

**Common Introductory Phrases for Embedded Questions:**
- *Can you tell me...*
- *Do you know...*
- *I wonder...*
- *Could you explain...*
- *He asked me...*

---

### D. Tag Questions

Tag questions are used to seek confirmation or encourage a response. They consist of a **statement** followed by a short **tag**.

> **Structure:** *Statement + Tag (Auxiliary/Modal Verb + Subject?)*

**Key Rules:**
1. The tag uses the **opposite polarity**:
   - Positive statement → Negative tag
   - Negative statement → Positive tag
2. The tag uses the **same auxiliary/modal verb** as the statement (or *do/does/did* if no auxiliary is present).
3. The subject in the tag is always a pronoun.

| Statement | Tag | Complete Question |
|-----------|-----|-------------------|
| *You like coffee* (positive) | *don't you?* (negative) | *You like coffee, don't you?* |
| *She isn't here* (negative) | *is she?* (positive) | *She isn't here, is she?* |
| *He can swim* (positive) | *can't he?* (negative) | *He can swim, can't he?* |
| *They won't be late* (negative) | *will they?* (positive) | *They won't be late, will they?* |
| *You have finished* (positive) | *haven't you?* (negative) | *You have finished, haven't you?* |
| *She doesn't like it* (negative) | *does she?* (positive) | *She doesn't like it, does she?* |

**Special Cases:**
- *I am* → *aren't I?* (Not: *amn't I?*)
  - *I'm late, **aren't I**?*
- *Let's* → *shall we?*
  - *Let's go, **shall we**?*
- Imperatives → *will you?* / *won't you?*
  - *Open the door, **will you**?*

---

### E. Special Cases: Stylistic Inversion

This is a stylistic or literary construction, often found in narrative or formal speech, used for emphasis or rhetorical effect.

> **Structure:** *Verb + Subject* (without an auxiliary)

| Example | Meaning / Effect |
|---------|------------------|
| *Here comes the bus.* | Emphasizes the arrival of the bus |
| *So goes the story.* | Emphasizes how the narrative proceeds |
| *Never have I seen such a thing!* | Emphasis on "never" (with auxiliary) |

> **Note:** This overlaps with inversion structures covered in *Section 11. Inversion*.
