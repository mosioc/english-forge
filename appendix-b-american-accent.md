## Appendix B1: American Accent

### A. What Makes an Accent "American"?

The "General American" accent (also called Standard American English or Broadcast English) is what you hear on most US national news broadcasts. While there are many regional American accents, several features are common across most varieties and distinguish American pronunciation from British.

| Feature | American | British (RP) |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| **Rhoticity** | R is pronounced everywhere | R often dropped after vowels |
| **Flap T** | *water* sounds like "wadder" | *water* has a crisp T |
| **Broad A** | *dance* with /æ/ (cat vowel) | *dance* with /ɑː/ (father vowel) |
| **Yod dropping** | *tune* = "toon" | *tune* = "tyoon" |
| **Short O** | *hot* with /ɑ/ | *hot* with /ɒ/ |
| **Cot-Caught** | Often merged (sound same) | Always distinct |
| **Stress** | *ad'vertisement* | *ad'vertisement* / *adver'tisement* |

---

### B. The Rhotic R

#### 1. The Rule

**In General American English, R is ALWAYS pronounced.** This is the single most important feature distinguishing American from many British accents.

| Position | Example | American | British (RP) |
|----------|---------|----------|--------------|
| Before a vowel | *red, rain, tree* | R pronounced | R pronounced |
| End of word | *car, door, teacher* | R pronounced | R silent |
| Before consonant | *park, world, turn* | R pronounced | R silent |
| In suffixes | *-er, -or, -ar* (*better, doctor*) | R pronounced | R silent (schwa only) |

#### 2. Practice

Read these aloud, pronouncing every R clearly:

| Word Group | Words |
|------------|-------|
| -er endings | *better, letter, water, teacher, computer, brother, sister, mother, father* |
| -ar endings | *car, far, star, dark, park, hard, start, market* |
| -or endings | *door, floor, more, store, before, explore* |
| -ir/-ur/-ear | *bird, girl, first, turn, burn, learn, earth* |
| R + consonant | *world, forty, north, force, course* |

> **Key Tip:** American R is made with the tongue tip curling BACK toward the roof of the mouth (retroflex). Don't touch the roof - just curl close to it.

---

### C. The Flap T (Tap T)

#### 1. The Rule

When T (or D) comes **between two vowels** (or between a vowel and a syllabic L/R) and is **not stressed**, it becomes a quick tap - the tongue flicks the roof of the mouth once, sounding like a very quick D.

This is **not lazy** - it's standard American pronunciation.

| Written | Sounds Like | IPA |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| *water* | "wadder" | /ˈwɑːɾər/ |
| *city* | "ciddy" | /ˈsɪɾi/ |
| *better* | "bedder" | /ˈbɛɾər/ |
| *butter* | "budder" | /ˈbʌɾər/ |
| *little* | "liddle" | /ˈlɪɾəl/ |
| *whatever* | "whadever" | /wəˈɾɛvər/ |
| *getting* | "gedding" | /ˈɡɛɾɪŋ/ |
| *party* | "pardy" | /ˈpɑːrɾi/ |
| *waiting* | "wading" | /ˈweɪɾɪŋ/ |
| *shut up* | "shuddup" | /ˈʃʌɾʌp/ |

#### 2. When NOT to Flap

| Situation | Example | Why No Flap |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| T begins a stressed syllable | *a**tt**ack, a**tt**end, ba**tt**alion, po**t**ential* | The T starts the stressed syllable |
| T at the start of a word | ***t**ime, **t**able, **t**oday* | Word-initial |
| T after N | e**nt**er, wi**nt**er, cou**nt**er, ce**nt**er | T after N is usually held or glottal, not flapped |
| T at the end | *ca**t**, ha**t**, si**t*** | No following vowel |
| T is emphasized | *That's exac**t**ly right!* | Emphasis preserves the true T |

#### 3. Practice Pairs

| True T (Careful) | Flap T (Natural) |
|-------------------|------------------|
| *writer* | *rider* (these become homophones in AmE) |
| *latter* | *ladder* (homophones) |
| *metal* | *medal* (homophones) |
| *hit it* -> "hidit" | |
| *not at all* -> "noddadall" | |

### D. The Glottal Stop T

Before a syllabic N (words ending in *-ton, -ten, -tain*), many Americans use a **glottal stop** [ʔ] - a catch in the throat - instead of a true T.

| Written | Sounds Like |
|---------|-------------|
| *button* | "buh-ʔn" |
| *kitten* | "ki-ʔn" |
| *mountain* | "moun-ʔn" |
| *certain* | "cer-ʔn" |
| *important* | "impor-ʔnt" |
| *written* | "wri-ʔn" |
| *cotton* | "co-ʔn" |
| *Clinton* | "Clin-ʔn" |
| *sentence* | "sen-ʔnce" |

> **Note:** This is common but not universal. Some speakers use a held T instead. Both sound natural.

---

### E. The Held T (Unreleased T)

When T comes before another consonant (except R), it's often **held** - the tongue moves into position but doesn't release air.

| Word | What Happens |
|------|--------------|
| *hot dog* | T is held, not released |
| *not now* | T is held |
| *at midnight* | T is held |
| *great job* | T is held |
| *that car* | T is held |
| *it looks* | T is held |

---

### F. Vowel Shifts

#### 1. The American Short O

The short O in words like *hot, not, top, clock* uses /ɑ/ (the "father" vowel) in American English, not /ɒ/ as in British.

| Word | British /ɒ/ | American /ɑ/ |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| *hot* | /hɒt/ | /hɑːt/ |
| *not* | /nɒt/ | /nɑːt/ |
| *top* | /tɒp/ | /tɑːp/ |
| *clock* | /klɒk/ | /klɑːk/ |
| *job* | /dʒɒb/ | /dʒɑːb/ |
| *possible* | /ˈpɒsɪbəl/ | /ˈpɑːsɪbəl/ |

> **Quick Tip:** Open your mouth more for the American version. Think of saying "ah" at the doctor.

#### 2. The American Broad A

Words with *a* before *-ss, -st, -sk, -th, -nt, -nce, -nd, -ff, -ph* keep the /æ/ (cat vowel) in American, but shift to /ɑː/ (father vowel) in British.

| Word | British /ɑː/ | American /æ/ |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| *dance* | /dɑːns/ | /dæns/ |
| *class* | /klɑːs/ | /klæs/ |
| *pass* | /pɑːs/ | /pæs/ |
| *last* | /lɑːst/ | /læst/ |
| *fast* | /fɑːst/ | /fæst/ |
| *bath* | /bɑːθ/ | /bæθ/ |
| *path* | /pɑːθ/ | /pæθ/ |
| *ask* | /ɑːsk/ | /æsk/ |
| *grass* | /ɡrɑːs/ | /ɡræs/ |
| *chance* | /tʃɑːns/ | /tʃæns/ |
| *can't* | /kɑːnt/ | /kænt/ |
| *half* | /hɑːf/ | /hæf/ |
| *laugh* | /lɑːf/ | /læf/ |

**Exception:** *Father, palm, calm, spa* use /ɑ/ in both accents.

#### 3. The American R-Colored Vowels

Vowels before R often merge into a single R-colored sound in American English. This is one of the hardest features for non-native speakers.

| Spelling | Examples | Sound | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|-------|
| -er, -ir, -ur, -ear | *her, bird, turn, learn* | /ɝ/ | All the same in AmE |
| -er (unstressed) | *teacher, paper* | /ɚ/ | Weaker version |
| -ar | *car, far, hard* | /ɑːr/ | Like "ah" + R |
| -or, -ore | *for, door, more* | /ɔːr/ | Like "aw" + R |
| -air, -are | *hair, care, air* | /ɛːr/ | Like "eh" + R |
| -eer, -ear | *deer, near, fear* | /ɪːr/ | Like "ih" + R |

| Practice Set | Words |
|--------------|-------|
| /ɝ/ (stressed) | *bird, word, turn, learn, first, girl, earth, work, church, hurt* |
| /ɚ/ (unstressed) | *better, color, doctor, summer, later, paper, finger, mother* |
| /ɑːr/ | *car, far, start, hard, large, market, army, artist* |
| /ɔːr/ | *door, floor, more, store, north, sport, order, forty* |
| /ɛːr/ | *hair, care, bear, where, there, share, parent, Mary* |
| /ɪːr/ | *deer, near, fear, clear, here, idea, career, serious* |

#### 4. The Cot-Caught Merger

About half of Americans merge /ɑ/ (cot) and /ɔ/ (caught) into the same sound. This is most common in the Western US, Midwest, and parts of the Northeast.

| Merged (Same Sound) | Not Merged (Different) |
|----------------------|------------------------|
| *cot = caught* | *cot ≠ caught* |
| *don = dawn* | *don ≠ dawn* |
| *stock = stalk* | *stock ≠ stalk* |
| *collar = caller* | *collar ≠ caller* |
| *nod = gnawed* | *nod ≠ gnawed* |

Both pronunciations are standard. If you're learning, choose one system and be consistent.

### G. The Yod Dropping

After /t, d, n, s, z, l, θ/, the "y" sound (/j/) before /uː/ is **dropped** in American English.

| Word | British | American |
|------|---------|----------|
| *tune* | /tjuːn/ "tyoon" | /tuːn/ "toon" |
| *duke* | /djuːk/ "dyook" | /duːk/ "dook" |
| *new* | /njuː/ "nyoo" | /nuː/ "noo" |
| *assume* | /əˈsjuːm/ | /əˈsuːm/ |
| *resume* | /rɪˈzjuːm/ | /rɪˈzuːm/ |
| *student* | /ˈstjuːdənt/ | /ˈstuːdənt/ |
| *Tuesday* | /ˈtjuːzdeɪ/ | /ˈtuːzdeɪ/ |
| *enthusiasm* | /ɪnˈθjuːziæzəm/ | /ɪnˈθuːziæzəm/ |
| *suit* | /sjuːt/ | /suːt/ |

**Exceptions (Yod is kept in both):**
- *cute, few, music, human, pure, cure* - the Y sound follows /k, f, m, h, p, k/

### I. Intonation Patterns

#### 1. Statement Intonation

American statements use a **falling intonation** at the end, similar to British. But the fall starts from a higher pitch and is more dramatic.

> *I went to the ↘store.*

#### 2. Yes/No Question Intonation

American Yes/No questions use **rising intonation**, often with a sharper rise than British.

> *Are you ↗coming?*
> *Did you ↗finish?*

#### 3. Wh-Question Intonation

Wh-questions use **falling intonation** (same as British).

> *Where are you ↘going?*
> *What did you ↘say?*

#### 4. The "Uptalk" (High Rising Terminal)

**Uptalk** (or "upspeak") is using rising intonation at the end of statements, making them sound like questions. It's common among younger speakers, especially in California and on the West Coast, but has spread nationally.

| Traditional Statement | With Uptalk |
|-----------------------|-------------|
| *I went to the store. ↘* | *I went to the store? ↗* |
| *My name is Sarah. ↘* | *My name is Sarah? ↗* |
| *We're leaving at 8. ↘* | *We're leaving at 8? ↗* |

**Usage notes:**
- Uptalk can signal that the speaker is not finished talking
- It can invite the listener to confirm they're following
- It's common in narratives and explanations
- In formal/professional settings, overuse can sound uncertain or lacking confidence

### J. Connected Speech Features

#### 1. Gonna, Wanna, Gotta, Hafta, Hasta

These reductions are **standard in spoken American English** (but avoid in formal writing).

| Full Form | Reduced | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| *going to* | *gonna* | *I'm gonna leave soon.* |
| *want to* | *wanna* | *I wanna go home.* |
| *got to* | *gotta* | *I gotta finish this.* |
| *have to* | *hafta* | *I hafta work tomorrow.* |
| *has to* | *hasta* | *She hasta be there by 8.* |
| *kind of* | *kinda* | *I'm kinda tired.* |
| *sort of* | *sorta* | *That's sorta weird.* |
| *out of* | *outta* | *Get outta here!* |
| *let me* | *lemme* | *Lemme see that.* |
| *give me* | *gimme* | *Gimme a minute.* |
| *don't know* | *dunno* | *I dunno.* |
| *should have* | *shoulda* | *I shoulda called.* |
| *could have* | *coulda* | *I coulda won.* |
| *would have* | *woulda* | *I woulda gone.* |
| *must have* | *musta* | *He musta forgot.* |

#### 2. Reductions of Common Phrases

| Written | Spoken |
|---------|--------|
| *What are you doing?* | *Wha'dya doin'?* |
| *What do you want?* | *Whaddaya want?* |
| *Where are you?* | *Where ya at?* |
| *How are you?* | *How ya doin'?* |
| *Did you eat yet?* | *Jeet yet?* |
| *Let me get a...* | *Lemme getta...* |
| *I'm going to get a...* | *I'm'onna getta...* |
| *I don't know.* | *I dunno.* |
| *You know what I mean?* | *Ya know what I mean?* / *Know'm sayin'?* |

#### 3. Word Linking

In connected speech, words flow together. American English links words in specific ways:

| Type | Rule | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| **Consonant + Vowel** | Final consonant attaches to next vowel | *turn off* -> "tur-noff" |
| **Consonant + Consonant** (same sound) | Merge into one longer sound | *bad dog* -> "badog" |
| **Vowel + Vowel** | Insert a glide /j/ or /w/ | *see it* -> "see-yit" / *go out* -> "go-wout" |
| **T + Y** | Becomes "ch" | *got you* -> "gotcha", *what you* -> "whacha" |
| **D + Y** | Becomes "j" | *did you* -> "didja", *would you* -> "wouldja" |

#### 4. Dropping Final Vowels in Unstressed Syllables

In casual and informal American speech, unstressed vowels at the end of words are often **reduced or dropped entirely**. This is not "lazy" - it's a systematic feature of American connected speech.

##### The G-Dropping (-ing -> -in')

The most well-known example is reducing *-ing* /ɪŋ/ to *-in'* /ɪn/ at the end of words.

| Written | Formal | Casual/Spoken |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| *swimming* | /ˈswɪmɪŋ/ | /ˈswɪmɪn/ "swimmin'" |
| *going* | /ˈɡoʊɪŋ/ | /ˈɡoʊɪn/ "goin'" |
| *doing* | /ˈduːɪŋ/ | /ˈduːɪn/ "doin'" |
| *talking* | /ˈtɔːkɪŋ/ | /ˈtɔːkɪn/ "talkin'" |
| *nothing* | /ˈnʌθɪŋ/ | /ˈnʌθɪn/ "nothin'" |
| *something* | /ˈsʌmθɪŋ/ | /ˈsʌmθɪn/ "somethin'" |
| *working* | /ˈwɝːkɪŋ/ | /ˈwɝːkɪn/ "workin'" |
| *looking* | /ˈlʊkɪŋ/ | /ˈlʊkɪn/ "lookin'" |
| *coming* | /ˈkʌmɪŋ/ | /ˈkʌmɪn/ "comin'" |
| *having* | /ˈhævɪŋ/ | /ˈhævɪn/ "havin'" |

**Usage Notes:**
- **Very common** in casual speech across all regions and social classes
- **Less appropriate** in formal speeches, presentations, or professional settings
- In song lyrics, poetry, and informal writing, often written as *-in'* with an apostrophe
- Not a "mistake" - it's a legitimate stylistic choice. Almost all Americans do this in relaxed conversation.

**Practice Sentences:**

| Written | Natural Spoken |
|---------|----------------|
| *What are you doing?* | *Whatcha doin'?* |
| *I'm going to the store.* | *I'm goin' to the store.* |
| *Nothing is happening.* | *Nothin's happenin'.* |
| *She's coming over later.* | *She's comin' over later.* |
| *Stop talking so loud.* | *Stop talkin' so loud.* |

##### Dropping Final Schwa (/ə/) in Common Words

Some common words with a final unstressed vowel (schwa /ə/) drop it entirely in rapid speech.

| Word | Formal | Casual/Spoken |
|------|--------|---------------|
| *gonna* (going to) | /ˈɡʌnə/ | /ɡʌn/ "gun" |
| *wanna* (want to) | /ˈwɑːnə/ | /wɑːn/ "wahn" |
| *kinda* (kind of) | /ˈkaɪndə/ | /kaɪnd/ "kind" |
| *sorta* (sort of) | /ˈsɔːrtə/ | /sɔːrt/ "sort" |
| *outta* (out of) | /ˈaʊtə/ | /aʊt/ "out" |
| *shoulda* (should have) | /ˈʃʊdə/ | /ʃʊd/ "should" |
| *coulda* (could have) | /ˈkʊdə/ | /kʊd/ "could" |

**Examples:**
- *I'm gonna go.* -> *I'm'n go.* (or even *m'gun go*)
- *I kinda like it.* -> *I kind like it.*
- *Get outta here.* -> *Get out here.*

##### Dropping Final Consonants After Nasals

In rapid speech, final consonants after nasal sounds can be dropped, especially when the next word starts with a consonant.

| Phrase | Careful | Rapid Casual |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| *want to* | /wɑːnt tuː/ | /wɑːnə/ -> /wɑːn/ |
| *don't know* | /doʊnt noʊ/ | /dʌnoʊ/ "dunno" |
| *going to* | /ɡoʊɪŋ tuː/ | /ɡʌnə/ -> /ɡʌn/ |
| *didn't* | /dɪdnt/ | /dɪn/ "din" |
| *doesn't* | /dʌznt/ | /dʌzn/ "duzn" |
| *isn't* | /ɪznt/ | /ɪzn/ "izn" |

**Example:**
- *I didn't know you were coming.* -> *I din know you were comin'.* -> *I din' know ya were comin'.*

##### Dropping Final /t/ and /d/

Final /t/ and /d/ are often dropped before words starting with consonants, especially in common phrases.

| Phrase | Careful | Casual |
|--------|---------|--------|
| *last night* | /læst naɪt/ | /læs naɪt/ |
| *next day* | /nɛkst deɪ/ | /nɛks deɪ/ |
| *old man* | /oʊld mæn/ | /oʊl mæn/ |
| *and then* | /ænd ðɛn/ | /æn ðɛn/ -> /ən ðɛn/ |
| *just because* | /dʒʌst bɪˈkɔːz/ | /dʒʌs bɪˈkɔːz/ |
| *must be* | /mʌst biː/ | /mʌs biː/ |
| *kept going* | /kɛpt ɡoʊɪŋ/ | /kɛp ɡoʊɪn/ |

##### The "Schwa Drop" in Common Expressions

Familiar phrases compress dramatically. The unstressed vowel (schwa) disappears entirely.

| Written | Careful Spoken | Very Casual |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| *I don't know* | /aɪ doʊnt noʊ/ | /aɪ dʌnoʊ/ -> /aɪ noʊ/ even just "I'no" |
| *Let me see* | /lɛt miː siː/ | /lɛmi siː/ "lemme see" -> /lɛm si/ |
| *Give me that* | /ɡɪv miː ðæt/ | /ɡɪmi ðæt/ "gimme that" -> /ɡɪm ðæt/ |
| *I'm going to* | /aɪm ɡoʊɪŋ tuː/ | /aɪm ɡʌnə/ -> /aɪŋɡʌnə/ -> /mʌnə/ "m'gunna" |

### K. Regional American Accents (Overview)

While "General American" is the neutral broadcast standard, these major regional accents exist:

| Accent | Region | Key Features |
|--------|--------|--------------|
| **General American** | Most of US (standard) | Rhotic, flap T, cot-caught often merged |
| **Southern** | Southeast US | Drawl (lengthened vowels), monophthongization (*ride* -> "rahd"), "y'all" |
| **New York City** | NYC metro | Non-rhotic (R dropped), *thought* -> "thawt" with raised vowel |
| **Boston / New England** | Northeast | Non-rhotic (*car* -> "cah"), broad A in *bath/path* |
| **Inland North** | Great Lakes (Chicago, Detroit) | Northern Cities Vowel Shift (*cat* sounds like "kyat") |
| **Mid-Atlantic** | Philadelphia, Baltimore | *water* -> "wooder", *on* -> "awn" |
| **California** | West Coast | Uptalk, *cot-caught* merged, *that* -> "thot" for some younger speakers |
| **Minnesota / Upper Midwest** | North Central | Long O's (*boat* -> "bow-at"), *about* -> "a boat" (from Canadian raising influence) |
| **African American English** | Nationwide | Non-rhotic (some regions), distinct vowel patterns, different intonation contours |

### M. Practice Paragraph (All Features)

Read this aloud, applying all the General American features:

> *I'm gonna head over to the store in a little bit. I wanted to get some water and a carton of milk before dinner. My sister said she'd meet me there, but I dunno if she's coming. It's kinda cold out, so I shoulda brought a better jacket. Whatever - I'll just walk faster. The store's not far from here, maybe forty blocks north. Last time I went, I got a parking ticket, so I'm not driving. Anyway, I gotta hurry up 'cause it's getting dark. See ya later!*

**Key features to apply:**
- *gonna, wanna, gotta, kinda, shoulda, dunno*
- Flap T in *water, better, getting, forty, later, city*
- Glottal T in *carton, certain, button* (if you prefer)
- Held T in *not far, hot cold*
- Rhotic R in *store, better, sister, there, her, forty, north, park, faster, dark, later*
- Short O in *got, not, hot, on*
- Broad A in *last, faster, asked*
- R-colored vowels in *sister, better, her, forty, north*

### N. Common Errors to Avoid

| Error | Correction | Why |
|-------|------------|-----|
| Dropping R after vowels | Pronounce all R's: *car* not "cah" | American is rhotic |
| Using true T everywhere | Flap T between vowels: *water* not "waTer" | Sounds stiff/unatural |
