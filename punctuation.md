## 1.2 Punctuation

Punctuation marks are symbols used in writing to clarify meaning, separate ideas, and indicate pauses or intonation. Proper punctuation is essential for clear and effective communication.

---

### A. End Marks (Sentence Endings)

| Mark | Name | Use | Example |
|------|------|-----|---------|
| **.** | Period (BrE: Full stop) | Declarative sentences, commands, indirect questions | *She is a doctor.* / *Close the door.* / *He asked if I was ready.* |
| **?** | Question mark | Direct questions | *Where do you live?* / *Are you coming?* |
| **!** | Exclamation mark | Strong emotion, surprise, commands, emphasis | *Watch out!* / *That's amazing!* / *Stop!* |

> **Note:** Do not use a question mark for indirect questions: *He asked where I lived.* (Not: *He asked where I lived?*)

---

### B. Commas (,)

The comma is the most frequently used (and misused) punctuation mark. It indicates a brief pause and helps organize sentences.

#### 1. Lists (Series)

Use commas to separate three or more items in a list.

| Example | Note |
|---------|------|
| *I bought apples, bananas, and oranges.* | The comma before *and* is called the **Oxford comma** (optional but often recommended for clarity) |
| *She likes hiking, swimming, and cycling.* | |

**Clarity example with/without Oxford comma:**
- *I love my parents, Lady Gaga and God.* (Without Oxford comma -> parents are Lady Gaga and God?)
- *I love my parents, Lady Gaga, and God.* (With Oxford comma -> three separate entities)

#### 2. After Introductory Elements

Use a comma after introductory words, phrases, or clauses.

| Example |
|---------|
| *However, I disagree with your conclusion.* |
| *After the movie ended, we went for coffee.* |
| *To be honest, I don't care.* |
| *Running quickly, she caught the bus.* |

#### 3. Joining Independent Clauses (with Conjunctions)

Use a comma before a coordinating conjunction (*for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so*) when joining two independent clauses.

| Example |
|---------|
| *I wanted to go to the party, but I was too tired.* |
| *She studied hard, so she passed the exam.* |
| *He didn't call, nor did he send a message.* |

> **Note:** Do not use a comma if the second part is not an independent clause: *I wanted to go but was too tired.* (No comma)

#### 4. Non-Restrictive (Non-Defining) Information

Use commas around extra information that is not essential to the sentence's meaning.

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| *My brother, who lives in Canada, is visiting next month.* | I have one brother; the clause adds extra info |
| *Paris, which is the capital of France, attracts many tourists.* | Extra info, not essential |

> **Compare with restrictive (no commas):** *The man who lives next door is a doctor.* (Essential info – which man?)

#### 5. Direct Address

Use commas to address someone directly.

| Example |
|---------|
| *John, can you help me?* |
| *Thank you, everyone, for coming.* |
| *Please sit down, ladies and gentlemen.* |

#### 6. Quotations and Dialogue

Use commas to introduce or follow quoted speech.

| Example |
|---------|
| *She said, "I'll be there soon."* |
| *"I'm tired," he replied, "but I'll keep working."* |
| *"Let's go," she said.* |

#### 7. Dates, Addresses, and Numbers

| Context | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Dates** | *July 4, 1776, is Independence Day.* (Year separated by commas) |
| **Addresses** | *She lives at 123 Main Street, Boston, MA 02108.* |
| **Numbers** | *There were 1,234,567 people.* (Thousands separator) |

#### 8. Contrasting Phrases

Use commas to set off contrasting expressions.

| Example |
|---------|
| *It was a difficult task, not impossible though.* |
| *The movie was boring, not to mention too long.* |

#### 9. Tag Questions

Use a comma before a tag question.

| Example |
|---------|
| *You like coffee, don't you?* |
| *She isn't here, is she?* |

---

### C. Semicolons (;)

The semicolon is stronger than a comma but weaker than a period. It connects closely related ideas.

#### 1. Between Two Independent Clauses (Without Conjunction)

Use a semicolon to join two related independent clauses when no conjunction is used.

| Example |
|---------|
| *The sun was setting; the sky turned orange and pink.* |
| *She loves reading; he prefers watching movies.* |

#### 2. Before Conjunctive Adverbs

Use a semicolon before conjunctive adverbs (*however, therefore, moreover, nevertheless, consequently, otherwise, thus, hence*).

| Example |
|---------|
| *He studied hard; however, he didn't pass the exam.* |
| *The weather was terrible; nevertheless, we went for a walk.* |
| *She missed the bus; consequently, she was late for work.* |

> **Note:** A comma usually follows the conjunctive adverb.

#### 3. In Complex Lists (With Internal Commas)

Use semicolons to separate list items when the items themselves contain commas.

| Example |
|---------|
| *I have lived in Paris, France; London, England; and Rome, Italy.* |
| *The committee included John Smith, the treasurer; Mary Jones, the secretary; and Tom Brown, the chair.* |

---

### D. Colons (:)

The colon introduces or announces what follows.

#### 1. Introducing Lists

| Example |
|---------|
| *Please bring the following items: a pen, a notebook, and a calculator.* |
| *I have three hobbies: reading, swimming, and painting.* |

> **Note:** Do not place a colon directly after a verb or preposition: *I need: a pen and paper.* ❌ -> *I need a pen and paper.* ✓

#### 2. Introducing Explanations or Examples

| Example |
|---------|
| *She had one goal: to win the championship.* |
| *The reason is simple: we ran out of time.* |

#### 3. Quotations (Formal)

| Example |
|---------|
| *The president declared: "We will not give up."* |

#### 4. Time and Ratios

| Example |
|---------|
| *The meeting is at 2:30 PM.* |
| *The ratio was 3:1.* |

#### 5. Titles and Subtitles

| Example |
|---------|
| *Grammar Made Simple: A Complete Guide to English* |

---

### E. Apostrophes (')

#### 1. Contractions (Omitted Letters)

| Example | Full Form |
|---------|-----------|
| *don't* | do not |
| *it's* | it is / it has |
| *they're* | they are |
| *I've* | I have |

> **Warning:** *Its* (possessive) has NO apostrophe. *It's* always means *it is* or *it has*.

| Correct | Incorrect |
|---------|-----------|
| *The dog wagged **its** tail.* | *The dog wagged **it's** tail.* ❌ |
| ***It's** raining outside.* | |

#### 2. Possession (Singular Nouns)

Add `'s` to singular nouns (including proper nouns ending in `s`).

| Example |
|---------|
| *John's car* |
| *The cat's toy* |
| *James's house* (or *James' house* – both acceptable) |
| *The boss's office* |

#### 3. Possession (Plural Nouns)

- Add `'` to plural nouns ending in `s`
- Add `'s` to plural nouns not ending in `s`

| Example |
|---------|
| *The students' books* (more than one student) |
| *The dogs' leashes* |
| *The children's toys* (irregular plural) |
| *The women's room* |

#### 4. Joint vs. Separate Possession

| Joint Possession (shared) | Separate Possession (individual) |
|---------------------------|----------------------------------|
| *John and Mary's house* (they share one house) | *John's and Mary's cars* (each has their own car) |

---

### F. Quotation Marks (" " / ' ')

#### 1. Direct Speech and Quotations

Use double quotation marks for direct speech (American English). Single quotation marks are used for quotes within quotes.

| Example |
|---------|
| *She said, "I'll be there at 5 PM."* |
| *He asked, "Did you hear her say 'I'm leaving'?"* |

> **British English** often uses single quotation marks for direct speech and double for quotes within quotes.

#### 2. Punctuation with Quotation Marks (American Style)

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| Commas and periods go **inside** the closing quotation mark | *"I'm tired," she said.* / *He called it "a masterpiece."* |
| Colons and semicolons go **outside** | *He called it "a masterpiece"; everyone agreed.* |
| Question marks and exclamation marks go **inside** if part of the quote, **outside** if part of the whole sentence | *She asked, "Are you ready?"* / *Did she really say "I'm fine"?* |

#### 3. Scare Quotes (Sarcasm or Emphasis)

Use quotation marks to indicate irony, sarcasm, or non-standard usage.

| Example |
|---------|
| *His "brilliant" idea caused the whole project to fail.* |
| *The "fresh" fish was three days old.* |

#### 4. Titles of Short Works

Use quotation marks for titles of short works: articles, poems, short stories, songs, episodes of TV shows.

| Example |
|---------|
| *I read "The Tell-Tale Heart" by Edgar Allan Poe.* |
| *My favorite song is "Bohemian Rhapsody."* |

> **Note:** Longer works (books, movies, albums) are italicized.

---

### G. Parentheses ( )

Parentheses enclose extra information that is not essential to the sentence.

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| *My brother (who lives in Canada) is visiting next month.* | The information is helpful but not essential |
| *The results (see Table 1) show a clear trend.* | Cross-reference |

**Punctuation with parentheses:**
- If the parenthetical is at the end of a sentence, place the period **outside** the closing parenthesis (unless the parenthetical is a complete sentence).

| Example |
|---------|
| *He arrived late (around 8 PM).* |
| *He arrived late. (He said he was stuck in traffic.)* |

---

### H. Brackets [ ]

Brackets are used primarily in quotations to add clarification or editorial comments.

| Example | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| *"He [John] was the first to arrive."* | Clarifying who "he" refers to |
| *"They said [sic] it was acceptable."* | *[sic]* indicates an error in the original text |

---

### I. Dashes (— / –)

#### 1. Em Dash (—)

The em dash is the longest dash (width of a capital **M**). It adds emphasis or indicates a sudden break in thought. **No spaces** around the em dash.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Emphasis** | *She finally understood the truth—years too late.* |
| **Sudden break** | *I was going to say—never mind, it's not important.* |
| **Parenthetical (stronger than parentheses)** | *Three people—John, Mary, and Tom—were chosen.* |
| **Interruption in dialogue** | *"But I thought—" "You thought wrong."* |

> **Typing:** On Windows: `Alt + 0151`; on Mac: `Shift + Option + -`; or type two hyphens `--` and auto-convert.

#### 2. En Dash (–)

The en dash is medium length (width of a capital **N**). It indicates ranges, connections, or conflicts. **No spaces** around the en dash.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Number ranges** (dates, times, pages) | *The years 1990–2000* / *See pages 10–25* |
| **Scores** | *The team won 3–1.* |
| **Connections between equal elements** | *The New York–London flight* / *The mother–daughter relationship* |
| **Compound adjectives with proper nouns** | *The post–World War II era* |

> **Typing:** On Windows: `Alt + 0150`; on Mac: `Option + -`.

> **Important:** Do not mix *from... to...* with en dash: *She worked from 1990–2000.* ❌ -> *She worked from 1990 to 2000.* ✓ or *She worked 1990–2000.* ✓

---

### J. Hyphens (-)

The hyphen is the shortest dash. It connects words to form compound terms. **No spaces** around the hyphen.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Compound adjectives before nouns** | *a well-known author* / *a five-year-old boy* |
| **Compound nouns** | *mother-in-law, passer-by, check-in* |
| **Numbers 21–99** | *twenty-one, thirty-four, ninety-nine* |
| **Prefixes before proper nouns** | *pro-British, anti-American, pre-2000* |
| **Clarity** (to avoid confusion) | *re-sign* (vs. *resign*) / *re-cover* (vs. *recover*) |
| **Word breaks at end of line** | *The report discussed eco- nomic trends.* |

> **Important:** Do not use a hyphen after an adverb ending in *-ly*: *a **highly** respected author* (not *highly-respected*).

---

### K. Slash (/)

The slash (also called forward slash or virgule) has several specific uses.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Alternatives or choices** | *Please bring your passport and/or driver's license.* / *Yes/No* |
| **Per (in rates and measurements)** | *The speed limit is 100 km/h.* / *The cost is $50/hour.* |
| **Fractions** | *½, ¾* (or use fraction symbols) |
| **Dates (informal)** | *The event is on 01/15/2024.* |
| **Line breaks in poetry** | *"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day? / Thou art more lovely and more temperate."* |
| **Abbreviations** | *c/o (care of), w/o (without), w/ (with)* |

> **Note:** Do not use spaces around the slash unless it separates lines of poetry.

---

### L. Backslash (\)

The backslash is **not** a punctuation mark for ordinary writing. It is used only in computing and programming.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **File paths (Windows)** | *C:\Users\Documents\file.txt* |
| **Escape characters in programming** | *\n (new line), \t (tab)* |

> **Important:** Do not use the backslash in place of the forward slash in regular writing.

---

### M. Asterisk (*)

The asterisk has several specialized uses.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Footnote marker** | *This is a fact.* *See page 10 for details.* |
| **Correction or omission** | *She went to the store.* (Original: "She went to the market") |
| **Wildcard in searches** | *Search for "gramm*" to find grammar, grammarian, etc.* |
| **Emphasis (informal)** | *That was **awesome**!* |

---

### N. Ampersand (&)

The ampersand represents the word "and."

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Company names** | *Johnson & Johnson* / *Barnes & Noble* |
| **Informal writing** | *We went to the movies & had dinner.* |
| **Citations (APA)** | *(Smith & Jones, 2020)* |

> **Note:** Avoid using the ampersand in formal writing (use "and" instead) except in company names.

---

### O. At Symbol (@)

The at symbol is used primarily in digital contexts.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Email addresses** | *username@example.com* |
| **Social media mentions** | *@username* |
| **Informal quantity** (price per unit) | *Apples @ $1.50/lb* |

---

### P. Number Sign / Hash (#)

The hash symbol has multiple names and uses.

| Name | Use | Example |
|------|-----|---------|
| **Number sign** | Before a number | *#1, #5* |
| **Hash** | Social media hashtags | *#grammar #English* |
| **Pound sign** | After a number (telephone) | *Please call extension #123* |

> **Note:** On telephone keypads, # is called "pound" (US) or "hash" (UK).

---

### Q. Percent Sign (%)

The percent sign indicates a percentage.

| Example |
|---------|
| *Sales increased by 25%.* |
| *Only 5% of the population speaks the language.* |

> **Note:** In formal writing, "percent" is often spelled out: *25 percent*.

---

### R. Ellipsis (...)

The ellipsis indicates omitted words, a pause, or a trailing off of thought.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Omitted words** (in quotations) | *"He said... something about leaving."* |
| **Pause or hesitation** | *"I'm not sure... maybe we should wait."* |
| **Trailing off** | *"I was thinking that maybe..."* |

> **Note:** In formal writing, use three dots with spaces between ( . . . ) or without spaces (…). Do not add a fourth dot unless the ellipsis follows a complete sentence.

### W. Common Punctuation Errors

| Error | Incorrect | Correct |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Comma splice** | *I like coffee, she likes tea.* | *I like coffee, but she likes tea.* / *I like coffee; she likes tea.* |
| **Run-on sentence** | *I went home I was tired.* | *I went home because I was tired.* |
| **Apostrophe confusion** | *The dog wagged it's tail.* | *The dog wagged its tail.* |
| **Its vs. It's** | *Its a beautiful day.* | *It's a beautiful day.* |
| **Your vs. You're** | *Your late.* | *You're late.* |
| **Their vs. There vs. They're** | *Their going to there house.* | *They're going to their house.* |
| **Missing comma after intro** | *After dinner we went for a walk.* | *After dinner, we went for a walk.* |
| **Comma before 'that'** | *She said, that she was tired.* | *She said that she was tired.* (No comma) |
| **Hyphen vs. en dash vs. em dash** | *The years 1990-2000* (should be en dash) | *The years 1990–2000* |
| **Space around slashes** | *Yes / No* (spaces unnecessary) | *Yes/No* |
| **Using backslash instead of slash** | *C:/Users/* (should be forward slash or backslash depending on OS) | *C:\Users\* (Windows) or *C:/Users/* (Unix/Mac) |
| **Period with parentheses** | *He arrived late (around 8 PM.).* | *He arrived late (around 8 PM).* |

### X. Punctuation in Different English Varieties

| Mark | American English | British English |
|------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Quotation marks** | Double (" ") for primary quotes | Single (' ') for primary quotes |
| **Period with quotes** | Inside the closing quotation mark | Outside unless part of the quote |
| **Comma with quotes** | Inside the closing quotation mark | Outside unless part of the quote |
| **Date format** | *July 4, 1776* | *4 July 1776* |
| **Decimal point** | Period (2.5) | Period (2.5) – same |
| **Thousands separator** | Comma (1,000) | Comma or space (1,000 or 1 000) |
| **Titles (Mr/Ms/Dr)** | Period after (Mr., Ms., Dr.) | Often no period (Mr, Ms, Dr) |
| **Time (hours/minutes)** | Colon (3:30 PM) | Colon or period (3:30 or 3.30) |
